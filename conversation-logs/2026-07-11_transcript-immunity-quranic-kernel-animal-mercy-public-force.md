## IV. Animals as a civilizational X-ray

The user argued that civilizations are usually judged through inventions, wealth, science, military reach, and monuments, while animal treatment reveals moral and ethical achievement.

### Core thesis

Animals cannot vote, bargain, testify, lobby, reward rulers, retaliate, or preserve their own testimony.

> A civilization’s moral architecture appears most clearly in how it treats beings that cannot reward it.

### Golden Age expansion

A complete Golden Age includes:

- capacity;
- distribution;
- restraint;
- mercy;
- correction.

Material achievement measures what a society could do. Treatment of animals measures what power chose not to do when cruelty or disposal was easy.

### Qur’anic animal ontology

The Qur’an presents animals as:

- communities;
- worshippers with their own relation to God;
- co-inhabitants of the earth;
- creatures of utility and beauty;
- lives harmed by corruption.

The resulting model is **restrained use under moral accountability** rather than either absolute non-use or unrestricted dominion.

### Comparative thesis

The project must not claim Islam invented animal welfare or was supreme in every dimension.

- Jewish traditions supplied important earlier protections.
- Jain and other Indian traditions often imposed stricter nonviolence.
- Christian, Greco-Roman, Persian, Chinese, Buddhist, Hindu, and other traditions contained meaningful animal ethics.
- The potential Islamic distinction is integration at civilizational scale within a meat-eating, commercial, urban, and imperial society.
