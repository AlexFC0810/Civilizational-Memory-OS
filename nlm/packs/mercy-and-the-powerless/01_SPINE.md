## ACT I — THE HOOK
In one tradition, a woman is condemned for starving a cat — and a person is forgiven everything for giving water to a dying dog.

- The Qur'an condemns the moral world in which baby girls could be buried alive by giving the buried girl a voice on the Day of Judgment. *(confidence: gate-verified record)*
- Islam places wealth under moral accountability: the poor, needy, debtor, traveler, captive, and orphan are central concerns of the moral-legal order, not objects of optional charity. *(confidence: graded ledger)*
- The Prophetic tradition makes treatment of powerless creatures a measure of the soul: punishment for starving a cat, forgiveness for watering a thirsty dog, reward for serving every living being, ihsan even in slaughter, prohibition on making living beings targets. *(confidence: gate-verified record)*

## ACT III — THE PAYOFF
- The Qur'an commands protection of orphans' property with witnesses and no self-dealing, framing misappropriation as damnable — with uneven enforcement across later societies. *(confidence: graded ledger)*
- Dhimmi status was a structured, unequal legal category that — compared to several same-era alternatives — more often than not functioned as survivable, taxable minority status rather than forced-conversion-or-death, though enforcement varied and sometimes escalated into forced conversion or massacre (Granada 1066; the Almohads). *(confidence: gate-verified record)*

Mercy here is not sentiment; it is obligation with legal teeth — reaching orphans, debtors, strangers, and even the creature that cannot speak.
