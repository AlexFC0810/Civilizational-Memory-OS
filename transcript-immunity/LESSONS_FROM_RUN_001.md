# Lessons from Transcript-Immunity Run 001

**Run:** TIP-2026-001  
**Purpose:** Convert first-use friction into protocol improvements before automation.

## 1. The load-bearing unit is often a claim stack, not a single claim

A speaker can compress multiple dependencies into one emotional sentence:

`source claim → historical claim → population claim → threat claim → policy demand`

Future packets should identify both atomic claims and the **claim stack** that gives them force. A weak link may collapse several downstream conclusions.

## 2. Add an authority-layer graph

Religious and ideological disputes repeatedly collapse:

`foundational text → transmitted corpus → commentary → jurisprudence → institution/state → movement → individual`

This should become a reusable graph object, not merely a tag. Each claim should identify its asserted and actual layer.

## 3. Add a topic-flooding quarantine

Long debates generate current-event branches unrelated to the initial dispute. The protocol needs three states:

- **core:** necessary to resolve the main argument;
- **dependency:** must be verified because it supports a core claim;
- **quarantine:** separate research packet; cannot derail the current run.

This protects the system from reproducing the Gish gallop inside its own research workflow.

## 4. Distinguish truth-fragment weaponization from pure fabrication

The most durable propaganda often begins with a true fragment—conquest, crime, hierarchy, demographic change—and attaches it to an unsupported totalizing map. The correction must preserve the fragment or the audience will correctly detect evasion.

New diagnostic:

> **Truth fragment → missing bridge → totalizing conclusion**

## 5. Reciprocal scapegoating needs an automatic brake

A defense of one maligned group can mutate into collective blame of another. The engine must detect when map repair becomes mirror propaganda. Named states, lobbies, corporations, officials, ideologies, and policies may be investigated; ethnic or religious populations cannot be substituted for those entities without evidence.

## 6. Self-correction clips may outperform rebuttal clips

The strongest segments may be where a speaker:

- retreats from a literal claim;
- concedes the central point;
- cannot produce the named evidence;
- contradicts a prior standard.

These can reduce adversarial temperature and make the audience update without requiring loyalty to the opposing speaker.

## 7. The first content asset should teach a skill, not settle the universe

This transcript contains dozens of disputed branches. The best MVP is the source-layer skill—“Which layer said it?”—because it transfers across religions, ideologies, states, and media controversies.

## 8. Add three implementation fields

Future packet/tool schemas should include:

- `claim_stack_id`
- `authority_layer_asserted`
- `authority_layer_actual`
- `branch_state: core | dependency | quarantine`

## 9. First evaluation target

Measure whether viewers can correctly classify unseen claims by source/authority layer. Do not treat agreement with the Islamic correction as proof that immunity was learned.

## Canonical lesson

> The system should not chase every sentence. Find the claim stack, separate the authority layers, quarantine the tangents, preserve the true fragment, and teach the audience the move.
