# Historical Moral Formation Case-Study Atlas

**Status:** Research agenda  
**Parent canon:** `canon/WORSHIP_EXEMPLAR_AND_MORAL_FORMATION_MODEL.md`

## Research Objective

Build a comparative, evidence-weighted atlas of how theology, ideology, exemplars, ritual, socialization, institutions, and incentives shaped historical actors and communities.

The goal is not to place people into crude “religious” and “atheist” boxes. It is to reconstruct each subject's operative moral ecology and compare declared ideals with actions under power.

## Core Research Question

> **What did this person or community treat as ultimate, how was that ultimacy rehearsed, what kind of human did it attempt to produce, and what happened when its values became costly?**

## Case-Study Evidence Hierarchy

### Tier A — Private or Introspective Self-Witness

- diaries;
- notebooks not intended for publication;
- private correspondence;
- marginalia;
- recorded private conversations with strong provenance.

These can reveal internal tension, but may still be performative, edited, or incomplete.

### Tier B — Public Self-Definition

- manifestos;
- speeches;
- books;
- legal codes;
- creeds;
- official proclamations;
- inscriptions.

These reveal the public operating system the actor wanted others to adopt, not necessarily private belief.

### Tier C — Observed Action

- administrative records;
- orders;
- budgets;
- laws;
- military conduct;
- appointments;
- punishment and reward patterns;
- institutional outputs.

### Tier D — External Witness

- allies;
- opponents;
- victims;
- diplomats;
- court chroniclers;
- later historians.

Every witness requires incentive and proximity analysis.

### Tier E — Posthumous Memory

- hagiography;
- demonology;
- national myth;
- religious memory;
- propaganda;
- popular culture.

This layer is important for downstream moral formation even when historically unreliable.

## Mandatory Case Card

For every person or community record:

1. Date, geography, and political ecology.
2. Childhood and formative institutions.
3. Professed metaphysics.
4. Moral authority.
5. Sacred or ideological exemplars.
6. Daily rituals and habits.
7. Model of human nature.
8. In-group and out-group boundaries.
9. Concept of suffering and legitimate violence.
10. Accountability above the self.
11. Public writings.
12. Private writings.
13. Actions under low constraint.
14. Actions under high constraint.
15. Treatment of powerless beings.
16. Institution-building.
17. Contradictions.
18. Change over time.
19. Victim testimony.
20. Source-confidence rating.

## Roster A — Prophetic and Sacred Exemplars

### Jesus of Nazareth

**Evidence problem:** no accepted autograph; teachings mediated through early Christian textual and oral traditions.

Research:

- God, mercy, service, enemy love, judgment, wealth, the least, animals;
- relationship between Jesus's teachings and later Christology;
- how worship of Jesus changes moral imitation in Christian traditions;
- divergence between Jesus as exemplar and Jesus as metaphysical object of worship;
- communities that most closely attempted to embody his teachings.

### Muhammad ﷺ

**Evidence problem:** Qur'an, hadith, sira, documentary evidence, later legal reception, and sectarian memory must be separated.

Research:

- al-Amīn and trustworthiness;
- mercy and disciplined power;
- justice, treaty fidelity, animals, poor people, orphans, women, servants, enemies;
- example under persecution and sovereignty;
- distinction between imitation, love, obedience, and worship;
- how later political cultures altered the connotation network.

### Moses

Research:

- liberation, law, covenant, judgment, humility, anger, collective formation;
- Mosaic monotheism as anti-idolatry and social constitution;
- later Jewish, Christian, and Islamic moral uses of Moses.

### Abraham

Research:

- anti-idolatry;
- hospitality;
- trust;
- migration;
- sacrifice narratives;
- fatherhood and covenant across traditions.

## Roster B — Philosophical and Ethical Rulers

### Marcus Aurelius

**High-value sources:** *Meditations* as an introspective notebook; imperial correspondence and administrative record.

Questions:

- Did Stoic cosmopolitanism restrain imperial power?
- Where did emperor-role override philosopher-role?
- How did he treat enemies, subjects, slaves, Christians, animals, and family?
- Are later “philosopher king” connotations supported by full fruit analysis?

### Ashoka

**High-value sources:** rock and pillar edicts.

Questions:

- How did remorse, Buddhist moral language, welfare, tolerance, and animal protection become imperial policy?
- Were inscriptions aspirational propaganda, actual administration, or both?
- What survived after the ruler?

### Akbar

**Evidence:** court histories, administrative policy, religious debates, opponents, and later memory.

Questions:

- Did pluralism emerge from conviction, imperial strategy, or both?
- How did personal curiosity become institutions?

### Umar ibn al-Khattab

**Evidence problem:** early reports require source criticism and differentiation from later idealized memory.

Questions:

- ruler accountability;
- austere leadership;
- welfare;
- justice toward weak parties;
- treatment of conquered populations;
- relationship between ideal memory and recoverable history.

## Roster C — Conquest and Imperial Sacred Order

### Alexander III of Macedon

Sources:

- near-contemporary fragments preserved in later works;
- Arrian, Plutarch, Diodorus, Curtius;
- inscriptions and administrative evidence.

Questions:

- heroic emulation;
- divine sonship and ruler cult;
- glory as ultimacy;
- cosmopolitan integration versus mass violence;
- posthumous transformation into “the Great.”

### Genghis Khan

**Evidence warning:** *The Secret History of the Mongols* is not a personal autobiography; the Yassa is incompletely recoverable.

Questions:

- Tengri, destiny, charisma, loyalty, merit, religious policy;
- extreme exemplary terror versus administrative integration;
- protection of envoys and trade;
- treatment of surrendered and resisting populations;
- how later Mongol and nationalist memory edits the balance.

### Augustus

**High-value source:** *Res Gestae Divi Augusti* as public self-presentation.

Questions:

- pietas, peace, family law, military victory, civic religion, ruler cult;
- difference between “peace” as moral order and peace as successful domination;
- how public ritual made imperial hierarchy sacred.

### King Leopold II

**Evidence:** speeches, correspondence, company/state records, missionary and victim testimony, diplomatic reports.

Questions:

- professed Christianity and humanitarian rhetoric;
- private extraction incentives;
- distance from violence through bureaucracy;
- how moral branding enabled atrocity.

## Roster D — Secular or Quasi-Secular Ideological States

### Vladimir Lenin

**High-value sources:** extensive books, pamphlets, letters, directives, and party records.

Questions:

- Marxism, historical necessity, class morality, vanguard authority;
- religion as competitor;
- when liberation justified coercion;
- party as court of final appeal.

### Joseph Stalin

**Evidence:** public writings, correspondence, archival orders, meeting notes, edited memoirs, and victim records.

Questions:

- Marxism-Leninism versus personal rule;
- party, state, nation, and leader cult;
- suspicion, purification, and fear as formation technologies;
- avoid armchair psychiatric diagnosis.

### Mao Zedong

**Evidence:** writings, speeches, marginalia, party records, policy documents, and testimony.

Questions:

- class struggle, continuous revolution, voluntarism;
- leader as sacred exemplar;
- ritualized criticism and mass campaigns;
- how utopian moral certainty interacted with famine and violence.

### Adolf Hitler

**Evidence:** *Mein Kampf*, speeches, directives, correspondence, contemporaneous diaries, bureaucratic records. Treat alleged private-table-talk texts with provenance caution.

Questions:

- race, nation, struggle, providence rhetoric, Führer authority;
- Christian inheritance, anti-Christian currents, occult myths, and political instrumentalization without forcing a simple “Christian” or “atheist” label;
- dehumanization pipeline;
- myth versus policy;
- victim evidence as indispensable counter-record.

### Benito Mussolini

Questions:

- movement from anticlerical socialism to fascist civil religion and accommodation with Catholic institutions;
- state, masculinity, violence, empire, and leader spectacle.

### Maximilien Robespierre

Questions:

- virtue, terror, republican purification, Cult of the Supreme Being;
- what happens when moral perfection becomes state enforcement.

## Roster E — Religious Reform and High-Fidelity Communities

### Early Christian Communities

Compare:

- communal care;
- widows, orphans, prisoners, sick people, infants;
- martyrdom;
- military service;
- slavery;
- internal discipline;
- treatment of outsiders;
- transition after imperial adoption.

### Desert and Monastic Movements

Questions:

- resistance to wealth and imperial Christianity;
- hospitality, discipline, care, hierarchy, and abuse risks;
- escape from capture versus creation of new authority systems.

### Franciscans

Questions:

- poverty, animals, non-domination, fraternity;
- difference between founder memory, order institutions, and later romanticization.

### Quakers

Questions:

- inner light, equality, pacifism, abolition, prison reform;
- early complicity and later internal correction;
- community discipline and long-term institutional fruit.

### Islamic Scholarly, Sufi, and Waqf Communities

Select cases where independent moral formation visibly constrained rulers, funded care, protected animals or weak parties, or preserved truth against state capture.

Do not treat “ulama,” “Sufis,” or “waqf” as uniformly virtuous categories.

Sources:

Questions:

- self-formation through literacy;
- dignity, power, resistance, and constitutional argument.

### Mahatma Gandhi

Questions:

- Hindu, Jain, Christian, and modern influences;
- nonviolence as discipline;
- political effectiveness;
- caste, race, sexuality, family, and contradictions.

### Leo Tolstoy

Questions:

- Jesus as moral teacher;
- nonviolence;
- property;
- institutional church rejection;
- gap between writings and family conduct.

### Albert Schweitzer

Questions:

- reverence for life;
- Christian theology;
- medicine;
- colonial paternalism;
- mixed fruit.

### George Orwell

Questions:

- secular conscience;
- anti-totalitarianism;
- truth, cruelty, class, empire;
- personal contradiction and moral witness.

## Roster G — Deliberate Counterexamples

The atlas should include people whose theology was humane but whose conduct was cruel, and people whose theology was absent or unorthodox but whose conduct was unusually humane.

Purpose:

- prevent theological determinism;
- identify mediating mechanisms;
- show why exemplar, institution, incentive, and accountability matter alongside belief.

Candidate categories:

- slaveholding Christians who wrote piously;
- Muslim rulers who funded learning but committed political atrocities;
- humane agnostics and atheists;
- violent secular ideologues;
- polytheist rulers who established welfare or restraint;
- reformers whose private lives contradicted public doctrine.

## Comparative Output Matrix

Each completed case should receive calibrated scores—not moral numerology, but structured comparison—across:

- transcendence over ruler;
- moral universalism;
- treatment of weak parties;
- truth constraints;
- violence constraints;
- self-restraint;
- institutional mercy;
- dissent protection;
- victim standing;
- source-to-action fidelity;
- capture resistance;
- renewal capacity.

Every score must link to evidence and uncertainty.

## Highest-Priority Sequence

### Phase 1 — Method Calibration

1. Marcus Aurelius — strong private text plus imperial record.
2. Ashoka — ruler inscriptions plus policy claims.
3. Hitler — manifesto, speeches, bureaucracy, and victim record.
4. Muhammad ﷺ — complex sacred, historical, and institutional source stack.
5. Jesus — no autograph, multiple early witness traditions, enormous later connotation network.

These five cases stress-test different evidence conditions.

### Phase 2 — Ideology and Capture

6. Lenin.
7. Stalin.
8. Mao.
9. Leopold II.
10. Augustus.

### Phase 3 — Correction and Renewal

11. Quakers.
12. Frederick Douglass.
13. Islamic scholar-state restraint case.
14. Early Christian epidemic-care case.
15. High-confidence waqf or animal-care case.

## Falsification Conditions

The model must be revised if evidence shows:

- professed god concepts have little explanatory value once institutions and incentives are controlled;
- “merciful deity” beliefs do not predict a wider moral circle or lower cruelty;
- war-god devotion does not meaningfully influence martial values beyond state structure;
- secular systems demonstrate equal or superior capture resistance without transcendence;
- prophetic exemplar traditions consistently generate personality cults rather than restraint;
- private writings systematically fail to improve prediction beyond public behavior.

## Final Research Standard

The atlas should never answer:

> “Was this person religious or atheist?”

as though that resolved the case.

It should answer:

> **What moral ecology formed this actor, what did they treat as sacred, what did they do when power removed external restraint, and which part of the system best predicts the fruit?**
