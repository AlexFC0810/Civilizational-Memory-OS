# Comparative Moral Civilizations Atlas

**Status:** Active research agenda  
**Parent canon:** `canon/COMPARATIVE_MORAL_ECOLOGIES_AND_PROGRESS_PATHWAYS.md`

## Objective

Build a source-led comparative atlas of the moral operating systems surrounding the ages of Jesus and Muhammad ﷺ.

The atlas should explain:

- what each tradition believed about human nature;
- how it defined moral authority;
- which beings received moral standing;
- how it formed people;
- how it constrained power;
- what institutions it generated;
- where it failed;
- how it renewed itself;
- what historical fruit can be attributed with calibrated confidence.

## Methodological Rule

Do not compare:

- one tradition's scripture to another civilization's worst atrocity;
- one saint to another population's median person;
- a legal ideal to an anecdote of violation;
- a modern concept to an ancient term without translation notes;
- entire civilizations using one-dimensional moral rankings.

Every case must separate:

1. source;
2. formation ideal;
3. institution;
4. enforcement;
5. median practice;
6. elite practice;
7. dissent and reform;
8. failure and capture;
9. long-term fruit.

## Comparative Evidence Ledgers

### 1. Child Protection and Infanticide

Questions:

- Where was exposure, abandonment, sale, sacrifice, or direct killing documented?
- Was the practice legal, tolerated, condemned, or regulated?
- Were girls disproportionately affected?
- What evidence is archaeological, textual, legal, literary, or polemical?
- What institutions cared for abandoned children?

Truth guardrail:

The Qur'an's condemnations are primary evidence of a moral intervention, but they do not by themselves establish that female live burial was universal or common throughout all Arabia.

### 2. Animal Moral Standing

Compare:

- nonviolence;
- sacrifice;
- hunting;
- slaughter;
- work animals;
- stray animals;
- veterinary care;
- overloading;
- entertainment;
- habitat and conservation;
- animal hospitals or shelters;
- legal enforcement.

Key counterexamples that must be included:

- Jain ahimsa;
- Buddhist compassion;
- Ashokan edicts;
- Jewish animal duties;
- Zoroastrian treatment of dogs;
- Greek philosophical vegetarianism and animal arguments.

### 3. Women, Marriage, Property, and Inheritance

Separate:

- legal capacity;
- property ownership;
- inheritance;
- marriage consent;
- divorce;
- guardianship;
- political authority;
- education;
- lived enforcement;
- class and regional variation.

Avoid modern equality claims where the historical category was a defined but unequal legal protection.

### 4. Slavery, Servitude, and Manumission

Record:

- sources of enslavement;
- treatment rules;
- sexual exploitation;
- family integrity;
- self-purchase;
- manumission;
- abolitionist reasoning;
- resistance;
- institutional dependence;
- gap between spiritual equality and legal continuity.

### 5. Poor, Orphans, Widows, Travelers, and Debtors

Map:

- obligatory versus voluntary support;
- who administered it;
- whether recipients had legal standing;
- whether aid depended on kin, patronage, citizenship, moral worth, or universal need;
- funding durability;
- geographic reach.

### 6. War, Conquest, and the Defeated

Compare:

- initiation of war;
- treatment of civilians;
- surrender;
- captives;
- enslavement;
- tribute;
- destruction of crops and religious sites;
- amnesty;
- collective punishment;
- rhetoric versus practice.

## Anti-Capture Casebook

For each community, investigate:

- How did it fund itself?
- Who could discipline leaders?
- Could ordinary members read the source?
- Were dissenters protected?
- Did beneficiaries have standing?
- Did one state control appointments?
- Were there multiple centers of authority?
- How were false teachings or fabricated traditions detected?
- How did the system respond to hypocrisy?
- Did it improve after scandal or become more secretive?

## Completion Standard

The atlas succeeds when it can answer:

> **Which moral systems most reliably transformed compassion from an occasional human impulse into a portable, funded, enforceable, scalable, and self-correcting civilizational practice?**
