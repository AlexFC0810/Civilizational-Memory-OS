# Faith, Authority, and Atrocity — Comparative Atlas

**Status:** Canonical research agenda  
**Purpose:** Determine when religion, sacred belief, religious identity, clergy, scripture, institutions, and transcendent accountability function as restraints, accelerants, targets, disguises, or sources of refusal during genocide and mass atrocity.

## Central question

> **What makes a believer obey God against a murderous ruler—and what makes another believer accept the ruler's violence as God's command?**

This agenda rejects two simplistic theories:

### Theory A — Religion prevents atrocity

Religious belief automatically restrains killing because believers fear God and possess sacred prohibitions.

Historical problem:

- religious populations have participated in atrocity;
- clergy and sacred institutions have sometimes legitimated violence;
- shared religion has not always prevented co-religionists from killing one another.

### Theory B — Religion causes atrocity

Mass violence is explained by irrational faith and would disappear under secular reason.

Historical problem:

- secular nationalist, racial, revolutionary, colonial, and authoritarian systems have committed mass atrocity;
- religious people and institutions have also rescued, resisted, documented, and condemned;
- many “religious conflicts” are driven by state power, land, empire, class, ethnicity, security, or nationalism while religion functions mainly as identity marker or legitimating language.

The stronger hypothesis is conditional:

> **Religion restrains atrocity when sacred accountability remains above tribe, state, and clergy. It accelerates atrocity when sacred authority is captured by them.**

## Research dimensions

For every case, map:

1. **Target identity** — religious, ethnic, national, racial, political, or mixed.
2. **Perpetrator ideology** — explicit doctrine, nationalism, state security, racial theory, empire, revolution, sectarianism.
3. **Religion's actual role** — belief, identity marker, institution, mobilization network, sacred authorization, resistance resource, or target.
4. **Authority stack** — scripture, clergy, ruler, party, military, local community, family, conscience.
5. **Text access** — literacy, translation, censorship, clerical monopoly, independent scholarship.
6. **Moral rules invoked** — sanctity of life, collective guilt, war, enemy protection, obedience, martyrdom, punishment, purity.
7. **Religious institution behavior** — collaboration, silence, ambiguity, protest, rescue, documentation, repentance.
8. **Ordinary believer behavior** — participation, opportunism, conformity, refusal, rescue, flight, denunciation.
9. **Secular guardrails** — law, courts, professional ethics, independent media, civil society, international norms.
10. **Penalty for refusal** — social exclusion, job loss, imprisonment, torture, death.
11. **Rescuer motivations** — faith, universal dignity, friendship, professional oath, politics, family memory, empathy.
12. **Post-atrocity truth capacity** — confession, trials, apology, denial, victim inversion, curriculum, memorialization.

## First comparative case set

### 1. Bosnia and Srebrenica

Research:

- Bosniak Muslim identity as target;
- Serbian ethnic nationalism and Orthodox symbols/institutions;
- difference between theology, church institutions, and nationalist sacralization;
- Muslim, Orthodox, Catholic, secular, and international refusers;
- UN failure and the legal recognition of Srebrenica genocide;
- denial and glorification after the crime.

Load-bearing question:

> When shared Yugoslav citizenship collapsed, why did ethno-religious identity become morally superior to the neighbor?

### 2. Armenian, Assyrian, and Greek destruction in the late Ottoman Empire

Research:

- Committee of Union and Progress nationalism;
- Christian identity as target marker;
- wartime security narratives, deportation, property transfer, and demographic engineering;
- Islamic language or authorities used for legitimation;
- Muslim officials, clerics, tribes, and households who resisted or rescued;
- distinction between Ottoman state policy, Turkish nationalism, Kurdish participation, local variation, and Islamic doctrine;
- denial and diaspora memory.

Load-bearing question:

> What allowed a collapsing empire to redescribe ancient civilian communities as an existential internal enemy?

### 3. Genocide against the Tutsi in Rwanda

Research:

- overwhelmingly Christian social context;
- colonial racial classification and church education;
- church-state relations;
- clergy who collaborated, remained silent, or rescued;
- why shared Christianity failed to override ethnic identity;
- the institutional meaning of later apology and accountability.

Load-bearing question:

> How can a highly Christian society preserve worship while ethnic classification becomes the stronger moral authority?

### 4. The Holocaust

Research:

- Nazi racial and national ideology;
- Christian-majority societies and long antisemitic inheritance;
- churches as institutions, collaborators, resisters, and rescuers;
- religious versus secular motivations among the Righteous;
- why baptism, conversion, or shared citizenship did not reliably protect Jewish people;
- Nuremberg's individual responsibility principle.

Load-bearing question:

> Which moral sources remained capable of saying no after the law itself became criminal?

### 5. Yazidi and Christian communities under ISIS

Research:

- extremist claims to Islamic authority;
- enslavement, forced conversion, sexual violence, and exterminatory intent;
- Qur'anic and Islamic legal counterevidence;
- Muslim scholars, fighters, families, and communities who condemned, resisted, or rescued;
- collapse of state institutions and coercive theological monopoly.

Load-bearing question:

> How does a violent movement convert selective religious fragments into a closed system where dissenting Muslims are also enemies?

### 6. Anfal against Iraqi Kurds

Research:

- mostly Muslim perpetrators and mostly Muslim victims;
- Ba'athist secular Arab nationalism and state security ideology;
- religious language versus state ideology;
- chemical warfare, village destruction, detention, disappearance;
- why shared faith failed to protect Kurdish civilians.

Load-bearing question:

> What does a Muslim-on-Muslim atrocity reveal about nationalism outranking religious universalism?

### 7. Darfur

Research:

- Muslim perpetrators and victims;
- racialized Arab/African identity despite religious commonality;
- state, militia, land, climate, and political-economy mechanisms;
- religious institutions and local peacemaking;
- limits of “religious conflict” categories.

### 8. Rohingya persecution and mass atrocity

Research:

- Muslim minority as target;
- Buddhist nationalism and monastic political networks;
- military state, citizenship law, demographic fear, and social media;
- Buddhist refusers and rescuers;
- international legal and prevention response.

### 9. Partition of India

Research:

- Muslims, Hindus, and Sikhs as both victims and perpetrators across local contexts;
- rumor, migration, state collapse, revenge chains, masculinity, honor, and sacred geography;
- religious leaders who inflamed or restrained;
- rescue across communal lines;
- how rapid uncertainty and population movement altered moral behavior.

### 10. Albania and Besa as a protection counter-case

Research:

- Muslim and Christian Albanian households protecting Jews;
- honor, hospitality, faith, nationalism, social networks, and local authority;
- limits and geographic complications of the rescue narrative;
- what transformed abstract moral teaching into concrete risk-taking.

Load-bearing question:

> Why did some communities treat the stranger as a sacred trust when others treated the neighbor as a contaminant?

## Theological literacy audit

For Muslim cases, distinguish:

- Qur'an;
- hadith and authenticity level;
- jurisprudence;
- ruler-made law;
- ethnic custom;
- modern ideology;
- wartime propaganda;
- individual violation.

For Christian cases, distinguish:

- teachings attributed to Jesus;
- biblical canon and interpretation;
- church doctrine;
- denominational institution;
- nationalism;
- racial ideology;
- ruler or party propaganda;
- individual violation.

For every tradition:

> **Do not allow the perpetrator to define the religion merely because the perpetrator spoke religious language. Do not allow defenders to erase institutional complicity merely because the foundational text contains a prohibition.**

## Rescuer and refuser study

Every case packet must include people from the perpetrator-associated community who refused.

Research:

- what they believed;
- what they noticed early;
- whether they possessed independent access to moral sources;
- who supported them;
- what risks they accepted;
- whether their professional role mattered;
- whether prior cross-group friendship mattered;
- whether they imagined divine judgment;
- whether they expected earthly accountability;
- how they described the victim;
- what made action possible rather than merely sympathy.

The goal is to identify **trainable and institutionally supportable refusal conditions**.

## Hypotheses to test

### H1 — Transcendent accountability hypothesis

People who believe they answer to a moral authority above the state may be more capable of refusing criminal commands when earthly punishment disappears.

Counter-risk:

The same belief may intensify violence when leaders successfully claim that God commands it.

### H2 — Authority pluralism hypothesis

Religious or moral systems with independent scholars, institutions, texts, and dissent channels are harder for a ruler to capture completely.

### H3 — Source-access hypothesis

Access to foundational texts reduces dependence on corrupt intermediaries only when accompanied by interpretive skill, historical literacy, and permission to question.

### H4 — Cross-cutting identity hypothesis

People with meaningful relationships and identities that cross the perpetrator-victim boundary are more resistant to total dehumanization.

### H5 — Visible-refusal hypothesis

One public refuser can change perceived consensus and lower the psychological cost of further refusal.

### H6 — Sacred-personhood hypothesis

Traditions that keep enemy civilians inside the moral universe create stronger friction against collective punishment.

### H7 — Institutional-protection hypothesis

Private conscience becomes prevention only when refusers possess channels, allies, safe reporting, sanctuary, and credible protection.

## Falsification and caution

Do not infer that:

- a rescuer acted from religion merely because they were religious;
- a perpetrator acted from theology merely because they belonged to a religion;
- literacy caused restraint;
- religion caused atrocity when nationalism or state power better explains it;
- religious prohibition mattered when institutions systematically ignored it;
- one heroic figure represents an entire community;
- shared religion should automatically prevent violence;
- secularism or religiosity has one universal effect.

## Public outputs

### 1. When God Outranks the King

Stories of believers who refused rulers, armies, parties, or clergy to protect targeted people.

### 2. When the Altar Serves the State

Cases where religious institutions legitimized, concealed, or failed to resist atrocity.

### 3. The No-Camera Test

Interactive moral scenarios testing what remains binding when earthly accountability disappears.

### 4. Same Faith, Different Fate

Rwanda, Anfal, Darfur, and other cases where perpetrators and victims shared formal religion.

### 5. The Religion Stress Test

Not “Is this religion peaceful?” but:

- Can it condemn its own side?
- Can followers refuse a ruler?
- Does it protect enemy civilians?
- Does it reject collective guilt?
- Can it confess institutional failure?

### 6. The Refusal Library

Comparative profiles of Muslim, Christian, Jewish, Buddhist, Hindu, secular, and other refusers and rescuers.

## Success condition

The agenda succeeds when users can:

- distinguish religious identity from causal theology;
- identify sacred capture;
- understand why shared faith may fail;
- recognize transcendent accountability as a potential guardrail rather than an automatic guarantee;
- name the institutional conditions that let conscience become action;
- turn criticism inward without turning identity into inherited guilt;
- recognize that no community is permanently victim, perpetrator, innocent, or immune.

## Canonical close

> **The question is not whether religion exists in a violent society. The question is which authority the believer obeys when God, ruler, tribe, clergy, conscience, and fear appear to point in different directions.**
