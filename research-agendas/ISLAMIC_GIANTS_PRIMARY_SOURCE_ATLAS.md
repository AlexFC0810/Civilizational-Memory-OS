# Islamic Giants Primary-Source Atlas

**Status:** Active research agenda  
**Parent canon:** `canon/ISLAMIC_INTELLECTUAL_GENOME_AND_COGNITIVE_ARCHAEOLOGY.md`

## Objective

Build a provenance-aware atlas of the prophets, Companions, Ahl al-Bayt, scholars, jurists, theologians, philosophers, scientists, physicians, mystics, historians, administrators, poets, and institution-builders whose work reveals how Islamic civilization perceived reality and converted thought into practice.

This is not a hall of fame.

The atlas must answer:

> **What formed this person's mind, what method did they contribute, what did they actually write or transmit, what remains valid, where were they wrong, and what civilizational loops allowed their work to matter?**

## Research Rules

1. Primary sources before reputation.
2. Secure attribution before quotation.
3. Original language where feasible.
4. Date the source, manuscript, edition, and translation.
5. Separate the historical person from later sectarian or nationalist memory.
6. Preserve disagreements among Sunni, Shia, Ibadi, Zaydi, Ismaili, philosophical, theological, and modern academic traditions.
7. Distinguish direct influence from resemblance.
8. Distinguish idea, method, institution, and later fruit.
9. Include failures and blind spots.
10. Do not use “polymath” as a substitute for understanding the architecture of a person's thought.

## Atlas Card Schema

Every figure receives a structured card.

### Identity and Provenance

- canonical name;
- alternate names and Latinized forms;
- dates with uncertainty;
- geography;
- language(s);
- religious, legal, philosophical, or sectarian affiliations, including disputes;
- teachers, students, institutions, patrons, and adversaries.

### Source Base

- securely attributed works;
- disputed works;
- lost works known through quotation;
- earliest surviving manuscripts;
- critical editions;
- reliable translations;
- autobiographical material;
- contemporary external evidence;
- later biographical legend.

### Cognitive Formation

- foundational texts;
- memorization and educational curriculum;
- daily practices;
- political conditions;
- social class and professional role;
- available instruments and technologies;
- languages and foreign traditions accessible;
- questions considered urgent.

### Intellectual Operating System

- ontology;
- anthropology;
- epistemology;
- moral hierarchy;
- methods of proof;
- view of authority;
- approach to disagreement;
- relationship between revelation and reason;
- relationship between knowledge and character;
- relationship between scholarship and power.

### Contribution

- core concepts;
- methodological innovation;
- institutional contribution;
- problems solved;
- important errors corrected;
- errors later corrected by others;
- influence inside Islamic civilization;
- influence outside Islamic civilization;
- modern applications that remain valid.

### Adversarial Audit

- strongest criticism;
- internal contradictions;
- dependence on inherited assumptions;
- moral blind spots;
- political or patronage incentives;
- later mythmaking;
- confidence score by claim.

### Compression Layer

- one-sentence contribution;
- five primary passages;
- three methods worth recovering;
- three claims not to repeat uncritically;
- one modern problem to which the figure's method can be applied.

## Phase 8 — Women, Households, and Under-Mapped Intellectual Networks

The atlas must avoid reproducing a male-only canon caused partly by survival and cataloging bias.

Research:

- women hadith scholars;
- women jurists and teachers;
- patrons of schools, hospitals, mosques, and waqf;
- court scholars and physicians;
- household transmission;
- manuscript ownership and audition certificates;
- regional figures omitted from modern survey texts.

Candidate starting points:

- ʿAʾisha bint Abi Bakr;
- Umm al-Dardaʾ;
- Karima al-Marwaziyya;
- Fatima al-Fihri, with source scrutiny;
- Zaynab bint al-Kamal;
- women documented in biographical dictionaries and isnad records.

## Phase 9 — Non-Muslim Participants in Islamic Civilization

Islamic civilization was not produced only by Muslims.

Map:

- Syriac Christian translators and physicians;
- Jewish philosophers, physicians, and administrators;
- Sabian/Harranian scholars;
- Persian, Central Asian, Arab, Berber, Turkic, African, Andalusian, and Indian networks;
- multilingual transmission across Greek, Syriac, Persian, Sanskrit, Hebrew, and Arabic.

Candidate cases:

- Hunayn ibn Ishaq;
- Thabit ibn Qurra;
- Qusta ibn Luqa;
- Maimonides as an Arabic-writing Jewish thinker within the Islamic intellectual ecology;
- Christian medical families in Abbasid courts.

The purpose is not to dilute Islamic civilization but to understand its actual plural production system.

## Anti-Myth Guardrails

Red-team these common narratives:

- “one House of Wisdom contained everything”;
- “al-Ghazali ended science”;
- “the Mongols ended Islamic thought everywhere”;
- “Muslims only preserved Greek books”;
- “every modern discovery was invented by a Muslim first”;
- “all Islamic scholars agreed on reason and revelation”;
- “the schools were static from their founders onward”;
- “post-classical scholarship was merely commentary”;
- “women were absent from scholarly transmission”;
- “non-Muslims were peripheral to Islamic intellectual production”;
- “the Golden Age was one uninterrupted place and time.”

## Completion Standard

The atlas reaches minimum viable mastery when it can answer:

> **How did the Prophetic seed become multiple intellectual disciplines, how did those disciplines interact to produce golden-age fruit, and which methods can be recovered without reproducing the historical errors and blind spots of their original context?**
