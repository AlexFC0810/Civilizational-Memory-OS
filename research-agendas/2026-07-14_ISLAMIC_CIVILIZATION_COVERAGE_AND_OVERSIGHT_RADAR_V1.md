# Islamic Civilization Coverage and Oversight Radar — V1

> **STATUS: NONCANONICAL RESEARCH AGENDA**  
> This is a systematic blind-spot detector, not a claim that every named region or period qualifies as a Golden Age.

## Why this exists

The omission of al-Andalus and Islamic Sicily from an early map revealed a systemic risk:

> A synthesis can be individually intelligent and still be structurally incomplete because the retrieval frame favors familiar capitals, canonical languages, famous men, political dynasties, and the fields modern audiences already value.

The correct response is not simply to append two places.

It is to install a coverage radar that asks what kinds of worlds the current map is likely to erase.

## Candidate geographic oversights

### Western Mediterranean and Maghreb

- al-Andalus beyond Córdoba: Seville, Toledo, Zaragoza, Granada, Valencia, Málaga, Almería, taifa and later ecologies;
- Islamic Sicily and Malta;
- Norman Sicily’s Islamic administrative, linguistic, agricultural, scientific, and artistic afterlives;
- Kairouan, Tunis, Mahdia, Fez, Marrakesh, Tlemcen, Sijilmasa, Ceuta, and trans-Saharan corridors;
- Almoravid, Almohad, Hafsid, Marinid, Zirid, Aghlabid, and related institutional worlds.

### Nile, Red Sea, and Horn of Africa

- Fatimid, Ayyubid, and Mamluk Cairo as distinct periods rather than one Egyptian line;
- Alexandria and Red Sea ports;
- Yemen, Hadramawt, Oman, and merchant-scholarly diasporas;
- Harar, Zeila, Ifat, Adal, and Muslim Ethiopia/Horn networks;
- Nubian and Sudanic contact zones.

### West and Central Africa

- Timbuktu alongside Djenné, Gao, Walata, Chinguetti, Agadez, Kano, Katsina, Sokoto, and Kanem-Bornu;
- Senegambian and Saharan scholarly networks;
- Ajami literatures and oral-textual scholarly systems;
- trade, jurisprudence, education, manuscript culture, and local institutional forms beyond the famous libraries.

### East African and Indian Ocean worlds

- Swahili coast cities such as Kilwa, Mombasa, Lamu, Zanzibar, and Mogadishu;
- maritime law, commerce, architecture, language, and cosmopolitan networks;
- Gujarat, Malabar, Coromandel, Bengal, and Hadrami connections;
- circulation among East Africa, Arabia, India, Southeast Asia, and China.

### Persianate and Iranian worlds

- Nishapur, Rayy, Isfahan, Shiraz, Tabriz, Qazvin, Hamadan, and Mashhad;
- Buyid, Seljuk, Ilkhanid, Timurid, Safavid, and regional scholarly ecologies;
- Persian-language intellectual production rather than Arabic-only retrieval;
- philosophy, medicine, poetry, administration, art, astronomy, and theology across political ruptures.

### Central Asia and steppe worlds

- Bukhara and Samarqand plus Merv, Balkh, Khwarazm, Kashgar, Herat, and Fergana;
- Turkic and Persian multilingual scholarship;
- Golden Horde, Volga-Ural, Crimean, and steppe Islam;
- post-Mongol reconstruction and Islamization rather than only Mongol destruction.

### Anatolia, Ottoman, and Balkan worlds

- Seljuk Anatolia;
- Ottoman development across Bursa, Edirne, Istanbul, Sarajevo, Skopje, Thessaloniki, Cairo, Damascus, and provincial centers;
- law, administration, medicine, architecture, cartography, waqf, engineering, diplomacy, and reform;
- Balkan Muslim, Christian, and Jewish participation;
- six centuries separated into actual periods rather than one civilizational block.

### South Asia

- Delhi Sultanates, Deccan sultanates, Bengal, Gujarat, Kashmir, Sindh, and Mughal worlds;
- cities such as Delhi, Agra, Lahore, Hyderabad, Bijapur, Ahmedabad, Dhaka, and Lucknow;
- Persian, Arabic, Urdu, Bengali, and regional-language knowledge;
- medicine, astronomy, architecture, music, administration, translation, law, education, and devotional life;
- Hindu, Jain, Sikh, Christian, and other participants and interlocutors.

### Southeast Asia

- Aceh, Malacca, Java, Patani, Brunei, Sulu, Mindanao, and wider Malay-Indonesian networks;
- Islamization through merchants, scholars, courts, Sufi networks, local adaptation, and print/manuscript cultures;
- law, education, women’s roles, anti-colonial thought, and maritime institutions;
- the world’s largest Muslim population as more than a rebuttal to “spread by sword.”

### China and Inner Asia

- Hui, Uyghur, and other Muslim intellectual and mercantile communities;
- Xi’an and mosque-education networks;
- Chinese-language Islamic scholarship and the Han Kitab tradition;
- connections among China, Central Asia, maritime Southeast Asia, and imperial courts.

## Candidate chronological oversights

- Umayyad institution-building before Abbasid Baghdad;
- Fatimid and Mamluk developments not treated as Abbasid derivatives;
- field-specific peaks that do not share one start or end date;
- post-1258 Maragha, Tabriz, Cairo, Damascus, Samarqand, Herat, Istanbul, Safavid, Mughal, West African, and Southeast Asian continuities;

- colonial rupture and extraction;

- civilizational afterlives under successor regimes, as in Norman Sicily and Christian Iberia.

## Structural biases the agent must audit

### Capital-city bias

Famous capitals can erase provincial and networked knowledge.

### Dynasty bias

Political timelines do not necessarily track intellectual, moral, commercial, or institutional peaks.

### Arabic-language bias

A complete map needs Persian, Ottoman Turkish, Urdu, Malay, Swahili, Hausa/Ajami, Chinese, Berber, Bengali, and other source worlds.

### Manuscript-survival bias

What survives may reflect climate, war, collecting, colonial acquisition, cataloguing, and digitization—not actual historical importance.

### Elite-male bias

Named court scholars are easier to retrieve than women, artisans, local teachers, healers, and collective institutions.

### Science-only bias

A contribution list can celebrate technical achievement while missing conscience, law, care, and institutional design.

### Morality-only bias

An inspiring ethical text does not prove implementation, scale, enforcement, or comparative advantage.

### Europe-only comparison

Europe is important but not the universal baseline. Compare with Byzantine, Sasanian, Indian, Chinese, African, steppe, Southeast Asian, and other contemporary systems.

### State-centric bias

Civilization also travels through merchants, scholars, families, waqfs, guilds, pilgrimage, diasporas, ports, and languages.

### Contributionism

“Who invented it first?” is often weaker than:

- who made it reliable;
- who institutionalized it;
- who expanded access;
- who integrated it with other knowledge;
- who transmitted it;
- what system reproduced it.

### Golden-age teleology

Do not force every history into rise → peak → decline. Look for overlapping cycles, migrations, afterlives, renewals, and field-specific trajectories.

### Apologetic selection bias

Do not search only for evidence that makes the civilization admirable. Retrieve contradictions, implementation failures, rival achievements, and strongest countermodels.

### Hostile selection bias

Do not let battles, punishments, rulers, and modern failed states stand in for the complete civilization.

## Required coverage check before “full map” language

Before calling a synthesis comprehensive, the Steward should report:

1. regions represented and absent;
2. centuries represented and absent;
3. source languages represented and absent;
4. fields represented and absent;
5. elite versus ordinary-life evidence;
6. gender and minority coverage;
7. urban, rural, maritime, and network coverage;
8. comparison civilizations used;
9. primary-source and modern scholarship gaps;
10. known distortions caused by preservation and digitization.
