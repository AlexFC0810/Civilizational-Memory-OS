# Prophetic Restraint of Power — Research Program

**Status:** Active research agenda  
**Parent canon:** `canon/PROPHETIC_ANTI_IMPUNITY_OPERATING_SYSTEM.md`

## Research Objective

Test the thesis that prophetic monotheism functioned as a durable anti-impunity architecture by placing rulers, ordinary people, and institutions beneath a moral authority they could not legitimately control.

The research must distinguish:

- moral seed;
- prophetic implementation;
- later interpretation;
- institutionalization;
- political capture;
- lived practice;
- historical fruit;
- renewal capacity.

The goal is not to prove that Christian or Muslim societies were consistently merciful. The goal is to identify what the sources made morally visible, which restraints were implemented, how power captured them, and whether the traditions preserved internal resources for correction.

## Governing Questions

1. What did prophetic monotheism add, intensify, universalize, or reorganize relative to surrounding moral systems?
2. How did Jesus and Muhammad ﷺ address status, violence, wealth, rulers, enemies, servants, women, children, animals, and the poor?
3. How did the different political situations of Jesus and Muhammad ﷺ shape the surviving moral and institutional record?
4. How did Christian and Islamic communities change after gaining political authority?
5. When did the source constrain power, and when did power capture interpretation?
6. Which moral teachings became institutions, law, funding mechanisms, professional norms, or popular stories?
7. Which claims represent ideals only, and which are supported by evidence of practice?
8. What kinds of abuse became contestable from within the tradition?
9. Does religious literacy predict different outcomes from nominal religious identity?
10. How can the historical architecture inform modern protection of powerless beings?

## Workstream 5 — Jesus, the Least, and Roman Power

Use primary and high-quality historical sources to examine:

- service versus domination;
- wealth and the poor;
- enemies and retaliation;
- children and marginalized people;
- imperial execution and the symbolism of the cross;
- early Christian attitudes toward military service, slavery, exposure, charity, and rulers;
- Jewish prophetic and Second Temple precedents;
- Greco-Roman ethical precedents and parallels.

Truth guardrail:

Do not claim that Rome or pagan philosophy lacked compassion, universalism, justice, or restraint. Establish the specific change in emphasis, scope, narrative centrality, or institutional form.

### Key Test

Did the Jesus movement merely pacify subjects, or did it relocate moral greatness away from domination and toward service, dignity, and judgment of power?

## Workstream 6 — Muhammad ﷺ Under Persecution and Sovereignty

Build a chronological dossier covering:

- Meccan persecution;
- migration;
- constitutional and treaty formation;
- treatment of enemies and captives;
- rules of war;
- amnesty and punishment;
- welfare obligations;
- property and market rules;
- treatment of minorities and treaty partners;

- hadith with grading and transmission analysis;
- documentary evidence where available;
- early legal development without retrojecting later consensus into the founding period.

### Key Test

What happens when prophetic ethics move from resistance to governance?

## Workstream 7 — The Prophetic Capture Cycle

Test the proposed sequence across case studies:

1. prophetic disruption;
2. persecuted community;
3. moral institution;
4. political success or adoption;
5. imperial capture;
6. ritualization;
7. reform and renewal.

Potential Christian cases:

- pre-Constantinian communities;
- Constantinian patronage;
- Theodosian settlement;
- Byzantine political theology;
- monastic and reform critiques;
- medieval church-state conflict;
- abolitionist and civil-rights retrievals of scripture.

Potential Islamic cases:

- Prophetic polity;
- Rashidun memory and contestation;
- Umayyad state formation;
- Abbasid scholarly and political differentiation;
- ulama-state relations;
- Sufi, juristic, and revival movements;
- anti-colonial and modern reform projects.

### Key Test

When does a moral movement successfully judge power, and when does power convert it into legitimacy infrastructure?

## Workstream 8 — Rome, Mongols, and Comparative Restraint

The user referenced Rome and the Mongols as examples of worlds in which violence and domination could be extreme.

Study without caricature:

- laws and customs that restrained violence;
- treatment of surrendered versus resisting populations;
- slavery and extraction;
- military discipline;
- religious policy;
- administrative adaptation;
- elite ideology;
- spectacle and punishment;
- local variation;
- change over time.

### Key Test

The comparison is not “religion versus no morality.” It is:

> Which systems made the powerful answerable to whom, through what institutions, with what reliability, and with what renewal mechanism?

## Workstream 9 — Predatory Efficiency vs Civilizational Strength

Develop measurable dimensions for two different forms of power.

### Predatory Efficiency

- conquest speed;
- extraction capacity;
- coercive compliance;
- intimidation;
- willingness to inflict suffering;
- elite concentration;
- low cost of treating outsiders as disposable.

### Civilizational Strength

- generalized trust;
- contract reliability;
- care infrastructure;
- legitimacy;
- knowledge production;
- protection of minorities and weak parties;
- restraint after victory;
- correction of rulers;
- intergenerational durability;
- capacity for repentance and reform.

### Key Test

Do norms condemned as “softness” reduce predatory efficiency while improving more durable forms of civilizational capacity?

## Workstream 10 — Religious Identity, Literacy, and Capture

Construct a causal model separating:

### Key Test

## Workstream 11 — Fear, Scrupulosity, and Religious Abuse

Research the corruption of divine accountability into:

- pathological fear;
- coercive control;
- victim-blaming;
- clerical immunity;
- obsession with visible minor infractions;
- moral licensing for in-groups;
- threat-heavy preaching detached from mercy and justice.

Develop the **Warning-Comfort Gradient**:

- warning should increase with power, harm, arrogance, and refusal to repair;
- comfort should increase with vulnerability, sincerity, repentance, and injury.

### Key Test

Can the tradition's own sources distinguish healthy taqwa from institutionalized terror?

## Source Hierarchy

Prioritize:

1. foundational primary texts;
2. manuscripts, documentary records, legal instruments, and administrative manuals;
3. critical editions and peer-reviewed scholarship;
4. specialist monographs from diverse historiographical traditions;
5. responsible syntheses;
6. popular sources only as leads.

For contested religious reports, separate:

- traditional authenticity judgment;
- modern historical probability;
- legal reception;
- ethical reception;
- public rhetorical usefulness.
