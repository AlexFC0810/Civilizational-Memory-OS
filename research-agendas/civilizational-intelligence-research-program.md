# Civilizational Intelligence Research Program

**Status:** Strategic research agenda  
**Purpose:** Convert the religion–secularism debate into a long-term, testable program for studying how civilizations perceive reality, coordinate power, protect the weak, preserve meaning, and correct themselves.

---

## 1. Foundational definition

> **Civilizational Intelligence is the capacity of a society to perceive reality, preserve wisdom, coordinate action, constrain domination, protect the vulnerable, correct error, and transmit meaning across generations.**

This is not identical to:

- GDP;
- literacy;
- military power;
- technological sophistication;
- religiosity;
- democracy;
- state capacity;
- scientific output.

Those are components or proxies.

Civilizational intelligence is the integrated system.

---

## 2. Master research question

> **What combination of moral vision, material capacity, institutions, culture, meaning, and feedback loops most reliably produces flourishing without allowing power to become ultimate?**

---

## 3. Core hypotheses

### H1 — Cross-layer alignment hypothesis

Civilizations flourish when reality, production, institutions, culture, and meaning remain mutually aligned.

### H2 — Error-correction hypothesis

Long-run survival depends more on correctability than on initial ideological superiority.

### H3 — Hybrid-accountability hypothesis

The strongest civilizations combine inner accountability—conscience, virtue, taqwa—with outer accountability—law, democracy, transparency, education, and institutional checks.

### H4 — Moral-progress alignment hypothesis

Durable progress occurs when moral vision, material capacity, and institutional enforcement converge.

### H5 — Propaganda-corruption hypothesis

Civilizations decline when propaganda corrupts the feedback loop between reality and power.

### H6 — Inherited-capital hypothesis

Current institutional and moral outcomes partly reflect accumulated cultural capital from prior religious, philosophical, legal, economic, and political layers.

### H7 — Sacred-slot hypothesis

When formal religion weakens, societies do not become value-neutral; states, markets, nations, parties, identities, technology, or the self can occupy the ultimate-value slot.

### H8 — Mechanism-cost hypothesis

Low violence or high order cannot be evaluated without identifying whether it is produced by trust, conscience, welfare, surveillance, coercion, shame, or some combination.

## 6. Prototype studies

### Study 1 — Religion is not one variable

Compare identity, attendance, literacy, intrinsic conviction, state religion, and clerical power.

### Study 2 — Raw vs controlled religiosity correlations

Reproduce popular charts, then add major confounders and interaction effects.

### Study 3 — Violence-reduction mechanism map

Compare countries with similarly low homicide but different mixes of trust, welfare, surveillance, policing, and social conformity.

### Study 4 — Moral capital after secularization

Track trust, family, volunteering, welfare support, and moral language across generations in Nordic countries.

### Study 5 — Islam and slavery stock-flow model

Model enslavement inputs and manumission exits under Qur'anic rules, classical law, and historical practice.

### Study 6 — Democracy as error correction

Compare formal voting with real access to power under different levels of money, media concentration, and lobbying.

### Study 7 — Meaning deficit

Examine whether high material security predicts flourishing once loneliness, addiction, family stability, and purpose are included.

### Study 8 — Propaganda immune systems

Measure which institutions allow societies to detect and reverse false narratives before catastrophe.

## 8. Methodological guardrails

1. No screenshot becomes final evidence.
2. Never use one country as proof of one ideology.
3. Separate correlation, mechanism, and causation.
4. Compare ideals, implementations, corruptions, and trajectories symmetrically.
5. Include breaker cases.
6. Separate scripture, tradition, law, and empire.
7. Track confidence levels.
8. Preserve unresolved questions.
9. Define falsification criteria.
10. Publish strong claims only after adversarial review.
