# Prophetic Civilizational Science Research Program

**Status:** Strategic research agenda  
**Created:** 2026-07-13  
**Program entrypoint:** `PROPHETIC_CIVILIZATIONAL_SCIENCE.md`

---

## 1. Mission

Build a truth-first comparative discipline for studying how prophetic and philosophical claims become human formation systems, institutions, civilizations, golden ages, adherence gaps, decline cycles, and renewal movements.

This program is not designed to prove that one community was flawless.

It is designed to answer:

> **Which truths, practices, institutions, and correction mechanisms most reliably convert power into stewardship, knowledge into service, wealth into obligation, desire into discipline, and civilizational capacity into distributed flourishing?**

---

## 3. Master questions

1. What did the highest-authority source actually teach?
2. What kind of human being did its practices attempt to form?
3. Which institutions plausibly arose from those teachings and practices?
4. Which institutions were inherited, borrowed, or independently developed?
5. How were benefits and harms distributed?
6. What restrained rulers, clergy, merchants, warriors, majorities, and families?
7. What did the civilization do when obedience became costly?
8. What was its predictable corruption pathway?
9. Could it correct itself before catastrophe?
10. Which outcomes are best explained by geography, technology, trade, demography, inherited state capacity, or rival weakness instead?
11. Which truths were discovered or implemented outside the civilization’s explicit theology?
12. What survives after political collapse?

## 4. Core hypotheses

### H1 — Truth-to-institution hypothesis

Moral or metaphysical truth affects civilizational outcomes only when translated into formation practices, incentives, funded institutions, and enforceable constraints.

### H2 — Adherence-gap hypothesis

The distance between authoritative norm and lived practice predicts hypocrisy, trust decay, legitimacy loss, and vulnerability to capture.

### H3 — Dual-accountability hypothesis

The strongest systems combine internal accountability—conscience, repentance, taqwa, virtue—with external accountability—law, audit, plural authority, evidence, and removal mechanisms.

### H4 — Distributed-golden-age hypothesis

Golden ages are usually distributed pulses across regions, disciplines, institutions, and centuries rather than one uninterrupted civilizational block.

### H5 — Borrowed-truth hypothesis

Civilizations can temporarily prosper through inherited or borrowed moral, scientific, legal, and institutional capital even when their explicit ideology cannot fully justify it.

### H6 — Power-capture hypothesis

Every sacred or moral system develops predictable pathways through which rulers, clergy, jurists, merchants, nations, parties, or ethnic identities capture its legitimacy.

### H7 — Vulnerability-test hypothesis

A civilization’s deepest moral quality is revealed by its treatment of beings with little leverage, especially when mercy is costly and cannot be repaid.

### H8 — Correctability hypothesis

Long-run civilizational intelligence depends less on never erring than on preserving channels through which truth can reach power and trigger correction.

### H9 — Prophetic-path hypothesis

The recurring language of walking, way, path, law, submission, repentance, and straightness across Abrahamic traditions reflects a shared prophetic grammar of religion as lived orientation, though not necessarily identical theology.

### H10 — Multi-causal flourishing hypothesis

No credible explanation of civilizational success can omit worldview, institutions, inherited capital, material environment, strategic environment, and contingency.

## 5. Civilizational case architecture

### Tier 1 — Foundational Abrahamic cases

#### Case A — Ancient Israel and Second Temple Judaisms

Tests:

- law above king;
- prophetic rebuke;
- covenant and collective identity;
- exile and memory;
- priesthood, sect, empire, and reform;
- moral treatment of stranger, poor, debtor, servant, and enemy;
- the transition from Temple-centered plurality into later Jewish and Christian formations.

#### Case B — The early Jesus movement

Tests:

- religion before imperial sponsorship;
- “the Way” and lived discipleship;
- charity, martyrdom, community, baptism, Eucharist, asceticism, and leadership;
- doctrinal diversity and consolidation;
- the transition from Jewish movement to transethnic church.

#### Case C — Byzantine Christian civilization

Tests:

- Christianity operating through Roman imperial machinery;
- church–state symphonia and capture;
- hospitals, charity, law, art, learning, and formation;
- slavery, coercion, class hierarchy, and political violence;
- longevity, renewal, and decline;
- causal weight of Orthodoxy versus geography, state capacity, and inheritance.

#### Case D — Latin Christendom

Tests:

- competing jurisdictions among papacy, monarchy, nobility, cities, monasteries, guilds, and universities;
- canon law and ruler restraint;
- charity, education, hospitals, and social discipline;
- crusade, inquisition, serfdom, coercion, and reform;
- whether institutional pluralism improved correction.

#### Case E — Repeated Golden Ages of Islamic Civilization

Tests:

- revelation, law, worship, knowledge, waqf, markets, welfare, and plural communities;
- distributed regional peaks;
- translation, synthesis, experimentation, and transmission;
- state, scholar, merchant, and endowment relationships;
- slavery, sectarianism, empire, gender hierarchy, and political violence;
- renewal after invasion and fragmentation.

#### Case F — Rabbinic and medieval Jewish civilizations

Tests:

- law-centered continuity under long periods without sovereign empire;
- education, charity, community courts, diaspora networks, and identity preservation;
- internal pluralism and authority;
- relations with Christian and Muslim states;
- mystical, philosophical, mercantile, and legal flowerings;
- the distinction between Judaism, Jewish civilization, nationalism, and Zionism.

### Tier 2 — Control and comparison cases

- **Achaemenid Persia:** plural imperial administration and legitimacy.
- **Mauryan India under Ashoka:** post-conquest moral kingship, welfare, tolerance, and animal concern.
- **Song China:** state capacity, printing, markets, technology, urbanization, and non-Abrahamic moral-political order.
- **Aksum and Ethiopian Christianity:** African Christian civilization and Red Sea integration.
- **Mali, Songhai, Timbuktu, and Swahili networks:** African Islamic learning, trade, manuscripts, law, and urbanity.
- **Mongol and post-Mongol Eurasia:** destruction, route integration, knowledge movement, and renewal.
- **Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal worlds:** mature multipolar Islamic statecraft and early-modern civilizational peaks.
- **Modern liberal-capitalist civilization:** rights, science, abundance, empire, commodification, loneliness, ecological cost, and inherited moral capital.

## 6. Golden-age definition protocol

A period may be classified as a golden-age candidate only after specifying the domain.

Possible domains:

- material welfare;
- knowledge production;
- education;
- medicine;
- law;
- charity and welfare;
- art and architecture;
- trade and infrastructure;
- political stability;
- plural coexistence;
- moral reform;
- vulnerability protection;
- error correction;
- civilizational influence.

Mandatory questions:

1. Golden for which region?
2. Golden for which class or community?
3. Golden in which domain?
4. Compared with what contemporaneous baseline?
5. How long did the peak last?
6. What institutional stack produced it?
7. What exploitation or exclusion subsidized it?
8. What survived after the peak?

> **No civilization receives one undifferentiated Golden Age score.**

## 7. Research Packet 1 — Byzantium: Christian Creed, Roman Power, and the Adherence Gap

### Exact question

> Which Byzantine institutions and outcomes are best explained by Christian moral architecture, which by Roman inheritance and material conditions, and where did Byzantine practice betray or expose limits in its Christian blueprint?

### Required subquestions

1. What were the major Byzantine golden-age periods by domain?
2. Which charitable and medical institutions existed, at what scale, and how were they funded?
3. How did Christian preaching, law, and patronage change Roman benefaction?
4. How did slavery function across periods?
5. Which groups experienced religious coercion and under what laws?
6. What formal and informal restraints existed on emperors?
7. How did church–state conflict sometimes correct and sometimes amplify abuse?
8. How much did stable coinage, geography, walls, trade, and bureaucracy explain resilience?
9. Did Orthodoxy improve social trust, sacrifice, continuity, or legitimacy in observable ways?
10. Which outcomes contradict a simple “truth caused success” theory?

### Comparison classes

- late Roman Empire;
- Sasanian Persia;
- Latin Christendom;
- Abbasid and later Islamic polities;
- Song China where appropriate.

### Output

- 10,000–20,000 word research packet;
- periodized scorecard;
- institution causal map;
- norm–institution–practice–distribution table;
- strongest pro-Orthodox and anti-imperial interpretations;
- source-closed public claims;
- list of claims rejected or softened.

### Breaker evidence requirement

The packet must include at least five pieces of evidence that weaken the preferred thesis.

## 11. Research Packet 5 — Can “Alignment with Truth” Be Measured?

### Exact question

> Which observable mechanisms would allow a transcendent or moral truth claim to improve civilizational outcomes, and how can those mechanisms be distinguished from geography, wealth, coercion, and inherited institutions?

### Candidate mechanisms

- trust and contract reliability;
- costly honesty;
- ruler restraint;
- charity and endowment formation;
- appetite regulation;
- family duty;
- educational motivation;
- universal or trans-tribal identity;
- willingness to sacrifice for future generations;
- moral courage against one’s own group;
- repentance and correction;
- protection of vulnerable beings.

### Methods

- comparative process tracing;
- natural experiments where available;
- before/after institutional change;
- paired historical cases;
- legal and budget analysis;
- network diffusion;
- counterfactual analysis;
- mixed quantitative and qualitative scorecards.

### Required falsifiers

The hypothesis weakens if:

- the outcome predates the belief and does not change after adoption;
- a control society produces the same outcome through a different mechanism;
- the belief remains ceremonial and does not alter incentives;
- the alleged benefit is confined to insiders;
- coercion rather than conscience produces the order;
- success disappears after material advantages are controlled.

- archaeological reports;

- influencer thread;
- apologetic quote compilation;
- partisan documentary;
