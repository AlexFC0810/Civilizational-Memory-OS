# Comparative Genocide and Mass Atrocity Atlas

**Status:** Active research agenda  
**Parent:** `canon/ATROCITY_MEMORY_AND_PREVENTION_ENGINE.md`

## Research objective

Build a comparative atlas of genocides and mass atrocities that preserves historical specificity while extracting transferable prevention mechanisms.

The atlas is not a ranking of suffering, a universal stage theory, or a substitute for case-specific history.

It is a disciplined system for asking:

> **What repeatedly has to become true—socially, politically, institutionally, and psychologically—before large populations can be removed, destroyed, or abandoned?**

## Research design

Each case should be studied through six synchronized records:

1. **Human record** — victims, families, communities, ordinary life, testimony, loss.
2. **Historical record** — timeline, geography, political context, conflict, actors.
3. **Institutional record** — law, bureaucracy, media, military, police, schools, religion, economy.
4. **Mechanism record** — dehumanization, classification, fear, dispossession, confinement, killing, denial.
5. **Response record** — bystanders, rescuers, refusers, foreign powers, journalists, institutions.
6. **Memory record** — trials, memorialization, denial, diaspora, education, political reuse.

## Case packet schema

Every case packet must include:

- formal case name and alternative names;
- date range with explanation;
- geographic scope;
- targeted populations;
- principal perpetrating institutions and chains of command;
- legal classification and jurisdiction;
- major scholarly classification disputes;
- estimated victims with ranges, provenance, and methodological notes;
- prehistory and structural conditions;
- ideology and propaganda;
- key laws and administrative measures;
- operational sequence;
- gendered and age-specific violence;
- cultural destruction;
- property transfer and beneficiaries;
- foreign enabling and restraint;
- warning signals;
- rescuers, refusers, defectors, and sanctuary;
- evidence preservation;
- trials and accountability;
- denial and distortion;
- intergenerational consequences;
- lessons that transfer;
- lessons that do not transfer;
- prohibited overclaims;
- confidence and unresolved questions.

## Wave 1 anchor cases

### CGA-001 — Holocaust

Purpose:

- high-resolution study of ideological radicalization, legal exclusion, registration, property seizure, deportation, concentration, industrialized killing, collaboration, rescue, and denial;
- strong comparative reference point without making every other case a replica.

### CGA-002 — Armenian genocide

Purpose:

- empire under war pressure;
- deportation and annihilation;
- confiscation and demographic engineering;
- foreign observation and delayed accountability;
- long-term state denial and diaspora memory.

### CGA-003 — Bosnian genocide

Purpose:

- atrocity in late-20th-century Europe;
- ethnic cleansing, detention camps, sexual violence, municipal-level operationalization, siege, international protection failure, Srebrenica, trials, and denial;
- direct relevance to the Bek Lover pilot and Balkan peace work.

### CGA-004 — Genocide against the Tutsi in Rwanda

Purpose:

- rapid escalation;
- propaganda and identity administration;
- local participation and coercion;
- international abandonment;
- post-genocide justice and reconciliation.

### CGA-005 — Cambodia under the Khmer Rouge

Purpose:

- revolutionary ideology;
- class and identity purification;
- administrative classification;
- forced labor, starvation, prisons, and mass killing;
- challenges around genocide versus broader mass atrocity classification.

### CGA-006 — Herero and Nama genocide

Purpose:

- colonial conquest;
- racial ideology;
- expulsion into lethal conditions;
- camps, forced labor, and long-term reparative claims;
- continuity and discontinuity questions in later European racial violence.

### CGA-007 — Yazidi genocide

Purpose:

- religious extermination campaign;
- enslavement, sexual violence, child separation, forced conversion, massacre, displacement, and digital propaganda;
- rescue and survivor-led documentation.

### CGA-008 — Albania/Besa rescue case

Purpose:

- a counter-case focused on protection;
- Muslim and Christian rescue under occupation;
- household ethics, honor codes, local networks, risk, and postwar memory;
- test what converts ordinary people into protectors.

## Comparative modules

### Module A — Language before violence

Compare:

- vermin, disease, contamination, treason, demographic replacement, invasion, impurity, existential threat;
- official versus informal language;
- humor, rumor, euphemism, and repetition;
- how metaphor becomes policy.

### Module B — Classification and data

Compare:

- censuses;
- identity cards;
- registries;
- racial laws;
- local knowledge;
- informant networks;
- modern biometric and platform data.

### Module C — Property and beneficiaries

Study:

- confiscation;
- abandoned homes and businesses;
- land redistribution;
- employment replacement;
- looting;
- commercial collaboration;
- incentives that recruit ordinary participants.

### Module D — Gender, family, and reproduction

Compare:

- sexual violence;
- forced pregnancy or sterilization;
- child removal;
- family separation;
- targeted killing of men or community leadership;
- destruction of reproductive and cultural continuity.

### Module E — Media and narrative systems

Compare:

- newspapers;
- radio;
- film;
- schools;
- sermons;
- social media;
- recommender systems;
- synthetic media.

### Module F — Bystanders and international failure

Compare:

- intelligence warnings;
- diplomatic language;
- peacekeeping mandates;
- border closure;
- arms flows;
- geopolitical incentives;
- legal hesitation;
- “insufficient evidence” as delay mechanism.

### Module G — Rescue and refusal

Compare:

- household rescue;
- forged documents;
- sanctuary;
- official obstruction;
- military refusal;
- diplomatic intervention;
- early warning;
- organized escape;
- community-wide protection.

### Module H — Denial after the crime

Compare:

- number minimization;
- victim inversion;
- “civil war” flattening;
- legalistic evasion;
- archival destruction;
- heroic national mythology;
- false balance;
- intimidation of scholars and survivors;
- algorithmic amplification of denial.

Deliverables:

3. one timeline showing warning, acceleration, and outside response;
4. three survivor-centered stories;
5. three refusal/rescue stories;

7. a “what could still have been done?” intervention ladder;

9. six short-form scripts;
10. one transfer test using an unfamiliar hypothetical case.

## Source hierarchy

Prioritize:

1. trial judgments and evidentiary records;
2. survivor and witness testimony with provenance;
3. contemporaneous documents and archives;
4. serious case-specific scholarship;
5. demographic and forensic research;
6. reputable memorial and documentation institutions;
7. specialist comparative genocide scholarship;
8. journalism for discovery and current memory politics;
9. public summaries only as navigation aids.

No AI-generated summary, unsourced casualty figure, viral infographic, or political speech may serve as terminal evidence.

## Comparative safeguards

Every comparison must state:

- what is genuinely analogous;
- what is structurally different;
- where terminology differs;
- what evidence is stronger or weaker;
- what the comparison cannot establish;
- how it might be abused politically.

## Evaluation

The atlas succeeds when users can:

- retain the specificity of individual cases;
- recognize shared mechanisms without false equivalence;
- identify early warning signs in unfamiliar contexts;
- resist identity-wide blame;
- explain how ordinary institutions become atrocity infrastructure;
- identify intervention points before mass killing;
- recognize denial and victim inversion;
- name models of refusal and rescue;
- understand that past victimhood does not grant present impunity.

## North star

> **Remember every people in their own name. Study every machinery in a common language. Build prevention before memory becomes another funeral ritual.**
