# Religion–Secularism Claim Reset Message

## Recommended message

Josh, I think we reached real common ground on poverty, education, institutions, welfare, and material security reducing desperation. I am not walking any of that back.

But I also need to reset the evidentiary standard, because several things were stated as facts that are actually disputed, unsupported, or category errors.

Calling God a “pedophile” because Mary was supposedly 13–15 does not survive basic scrutiny. Neither the canonical Gospel nor the Qur’an gives Mary a numerical age. The conception story is explicitly miraculous, not sexual intercourse. And pedophilia is a claim about sustained sexual attraction to prepubescent children. So that label depends on an unstated age, converts a nonsexual event into sex, and then applies a psychiatric category without evidence. You can reject the miracle; that does not make the insult historically or logically valid.

The same standard applies to Aisha. The Qur’an gives no age. Later Sunni hadith collections report six/nine, but modern source-critical scholarship disputes the provenance of that report. The honest verdict is not “definitely nine,” and it is not “definitely disproven.” It is historically contested. Even if someone accepted the traditional report, one marriage claim would still not establish a persistent sexual preference fitting the definition of pedophilia.

Jesus being “an allegory” is also not the mainstream historical position. Scholars debate almost everything about his life and theology, but the existence of a Jewish preacher who was crucified under Pontius Pilate is accepted across nearly the entire specialist field. Reza Aslan’s *Zealot* is one disputed reconstruction of that historical figure, not a settled verdict that Jesus was leading a violent regime-change movement.

Muhammad did lead a political community and armed forces. “Military commander” is factual. “Warlord” is usually a loaded modern compression that erases prophet, teacher, judge, treaty-maker, reformer, and statesman and pre-decides the moral verdict before examining any specific campaign. I am willing to examine every battle and execution individually; I am not willing to let one label substitute for that work.

On slavery, precision cuts both ways. The Qur’an does not contain a modern sentence banning all slavery everywhere overnight. Muslim societies also failed to complete abolition and continued slave systems. That must be admitted. But reducing the Qur’anic architecture to “not abolitionist” hides the manumission contracts, freeing slaves as virtue and expiation, zakat funding for liberation, protection against forced prostitution, moral equality, and restrictions on enslavement. The strongest claim is not that Islam instantly abolished slavery. It is that it created an unusually strong manumission and reform vector inside a world structurally built on slavery—and later Muslims did not fully realize that vector.

The Qur’an’s preservation also cannot be dismissed as “oral tradition means anyone can say anything.” It had a dual transmission architecture: public recitation and mass memorization cross-checked against written manuscripts and distributed communities. Early manuscripts support very early stabilization of the consonantal text. There are recognized reading and orthographic variants, so I will not make a brittle “zero variation of any kind” claim. But “easy to tamper with” is equally inconsistent with the evidence.

Finally, “secularism and mass production made us peaceful” is too simple. Material abundance, education, law, state capacity, medicine, welfare, commerce, democratic accountability, moral movements, changing norms, surveillance, and coercive power all interact. Secular societies can be peaceful; explicitly secular regimes have also produced censorship, persecution, camps, and mass killing. Religious societies have produced both mercy and atrocity. Atheists can be profoundly moral, but atheism itself is only a position on gods; empathy, human rights, pacifism, and anti-exploitation come from additional ethical commitments such as humanism, liberalism, socialism, or anarchism.

So here is the standard I think we should both accept:

- exact source, not screenshot compression;
- defined term, not disgust labels;
- scripture separated from later report, law, empire, and custom;
- ideal compared with ideal, practice with practice, corruption with corruption;
- multiple causes instead of one-variable history;
- and a clear statement of what evidence would change our minds.

I am not asking for religion to be protected from criticism. I am asking for it to receive the same intellectual charity you give communism: distinguish the ideal from corrupt implementation, then test whether the blueprint itself contains mechanisms for justice, restraint, protection of the weak, and self-correction.

That leaves us with a better question than “religion or secularism?”

> Which complete civilizational system best combines material competence, moral truth, protection of the vulnerable, restraint of power, inner conscience, institutional accountability, community, meaning, and the ability to correct its own corruption?

That is the question I am actually trying to answer.

## One-paragraph compressed version

Josh, I agree that poverty reduction, education, welfare, and functioning institutions reduce desperation, but several anti-religious claims were presented far more confidently than the evidence allows. Mary’s age is not given in the canonical texts, miraculous conception is not sex, and “pedophile” is therefore a category error. Aisha’s six/nine report is in later Sunni hadith, not the Qur’an, and its historical provenance is disputed; neither certainty nor a clinical label follows. Jesus-as-allegory is fringe, Aslan’s revolutionary Jesus is one contested portrait, and “warlord” is a loaded reduction of a prophet-statesman who also commanded armies. The Qur’an did not issue a modern instant abolition statute, but it created substantial manumission, protection, and liberation mechanisms that “pro-slavery” language erases; later Muslim failure must also be admitted. Qur’anic preservation combined writing, public recitation, mass memorization, and communal correction, with remarkable early stability and bounded variants. And secularism or mass production alone did not cause peace: material capacity, institutions, norms, law, conscience, welfare, coercion, and history interact. Let’s use one standard—exact sources, defined terms, symmetrical comparisons, and multicausal analysis—rather than comparing communism’s ideal to religion’s worst implementation.
