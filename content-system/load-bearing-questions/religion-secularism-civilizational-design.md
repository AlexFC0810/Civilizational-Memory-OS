# Load-Bearing Questions: Religion, Secularism, and Civilizational Design

**Purpose:** Replace endless point-by-point debate with questions that expose causal gaps, asymmetrical standards, category errors, and missing mechanisms.

---

## Correlation and causality

1. When poverty, war, education, inequality, state capacity, colonial history, sanctions, and reporting quality are controlled for, what independent effect does religiosity still have?
2. Are we measuring sincere conviction, identity, clerical power, state religion, or religious literacy?
3. Does religion cause insecurity, or does insecurity increase religiosity?
4. Are low reported crime rates evidence of virtue, trust, surveillance, fear, underreporting, or some mixture?
5. What mechanism actually produces the outcome?

---

## Symmetry and fairness

6. Are we comparing religion’s worst implementation to secularism’s best ideal?
7. If communism may evolve beyond Stalin, why must Islam be frozen at the worst practice of medieval empires?
8. If corrupt clergy do not define Christianity, do corrupt party officials define communism?
9. Are we judging the blueprint, the builder, the building, or the corruption mode?
10. Would we accept the same standard if it were applied to our own worldview?

---

## Historical baseline

11. Compared with the society it entered, what did the system restrain, protect, normalize, fail to correct, and set in motion?
12. Which moral ideas feel obvious today only because earlier generations made them normal?
13. What was the realistic alternative available at the time?
14. Does historical context explain a practice, excuse it, or only help us evaluate its trajectory?
15. Which reformers later used the tradition’s own principles against its failures?

---

## Violence and order

16. Every civilization reduces violence somehow. What mechanism does this one use, and what does it cost?
17. Is peace produced by conscience, trust, welfare, surveillance, shame, policing, fear, or coercion?
18. Would the society remain orderly if cameras, police, and reputational pressure disappeared?
19. What produces people who restrain themselves when they could escape punishment?
20. Does the system merely manage behavior, or form character?

---

## Material abundance

21. Did industrialization create morality, or make pre-existing moral claims easier to implement?
22. Why did industrial capacity produce both welfare states and mechanized mass killing?
23. What tells a society whether surplus should fund hospitals, palaces, weapons, or public goods?
24. Is technology a moral direction or a power amplifier?
25. Which layer is being mistaken for the whole civilization?

---

## Slavery and abolition

26. Does the source command permanent slavery, merely regulate it, or create a contractionary transition architecture?
27. What were the legitimate inputs into slavery, and what exits existed?
28. If inputs narrow while manumission is continuously rewarded and funded, what is the long-run direction?
29. Are all historical systems called “slavery” structurally identical?
30. Which system gave the enslaved the strongest realistic path to freedom, family protection, property, education, or status?
31. Did later rulers preserve slavery by following the blueprint or by neutralizing its liberation logic?
32. Could abolition have occurred immediately without alternative labor, welfare, prison, and POW systems?
33. Does practicality explain gradualism without morally legitimizing ownership?

---

## Democracy and power

34. Is democracy valuable because majorities are always right, or because it allows peaceful correction and gives the weak voice?
35. What must a majority never be allowed to vote away?
36. At what point does money convert democracy into auction politics?
37. If wealth buys law, media, candidates, and attention, do the poor still possess meaningful political power?
38. What moral and institutional guardrails keep democracy from becoming oligarchy or mob rule?

---

## Secularism and inherited moral capital

39. Did a mature secular society create its norms from nothing, or inherit and transform older moral capital?
40. Which norms came from religion, local custom, labor movements, philosophy, feminism, constitutionalism, and material development?
41. Does removing clerical domination remove every moral inheritance transmitted through religious civilization?
42. Can inherited trust persist after its original carriers disappear?
43. What institutions now reproduce the norms that churches, mosques, families, guilds, or local communities once carried?
44. Is secularization a cause of current trust, an effect of security, or both?

---

## Morality without God

45. Can atheists be moral? Obviously yes. Can a civilization reproduce costly moral duty across generations without transcendence?
46. What makes a right fundamental rather than socially convenient?
47. If rights come from the state, can the state revoke them?
48. If morality is consensus, what makes a genocidal consensus wrong?
49. What binds a person when evil is profitable, hidden, legal, and popular?
50. Can secular humanism provide a durable answer, and what institutions transmit it?

---

## Worldview evaluation

51. Which worldview explains the greatest amount of reality with the fewest arbitrary assumptions?
52. What evidence would genuinely change your mind?
53. Which observations are expected under your worldview but surprising under the rival model?
54. Are unresolved mysteries being investigated or used as an escape hatch?
55. Are scientific-miracle claims robust, or dependent on translation tricks and retrofitting?
56. Does the worldview explain consciousness, reason, mathematics, morality, beauty, evil, suffering, and meaning as one coherent pattern?

---

## Civilizational intelligence

57. Can the civilization admit error without collapsing?
58. Can low-status people correct high-status people?
59. Does truth reach power, or is the feedback loop captured?
60. What does the system optimize after its original founders are gone?
61. What protects the weak when compassionate leaders are absent?
62. What preserves memory without turning history into mythology?
63. Can the civilization survive its own technology?
64. What does it treat as sacred, and what does it sacrifice to that sacred object?

---

## Construction questions

65. If we rewound history to the year 600, what minimum stack would reliably produce a compassionate, scientifically advanced, prosperous civilization a thousand years later?
66. What should be taken from science, democracy, religion, socialism, markets, philosophy, and art?
67. What corruption mode must be designed against in each layer?
68. What inner-accountability mechanisms complement law and enforcement?
69. What outer-accountability mechanisms restrain claimed divine, political, economic, or expert authority?
70. What would a civilization optimized for truth, mercy, competence, family, freedom, beauty, and correction actually look like?

---

## Closing question

> **Which civilizational operating system best explains reality, forms people who remain just under pressure, protects the weak, constrains power, corrects corruption, and preserves meaning without sacrificing truth?**
