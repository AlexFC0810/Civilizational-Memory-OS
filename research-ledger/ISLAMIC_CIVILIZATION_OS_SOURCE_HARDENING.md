## Evidence Grades

### A-Level
- Qur’an.
- Authenticated hadith with exact collection/reference.
- Primary historical sources with source/transmission caveats.
- Peer-reviewed scholarship.
- Major academic-press monographs.
- Specialist historians or domain experts.

### B-Level
- Reputable encyclopedias.
- University/museum/library explainers.
- Reputable scholarly summaries.
- Well-cited secondary sources.

### C-Level
- Popular articles.
- General documentaries.
- Public lectures.
- Apologetics material.
- Polemical works.

### D-Level
- Viral claims.
- Memes.
- Unsourced quotes.
- Social media screenshots.
- Emotionally useful but unverified stories.

# Priority Claim Cards

## 1. Niyyah: Actions Are Judged by Intentions

**Layer:** Inner World  
**Status:** Source-hardened starting point  
**Evidence grade:** A

### Draft claim
Islam places intention at the core of action: the same outward act can have different moral/spiritual meaning depending on why it is done.

### Why it matters
This gives the civilization an inner audit layer. It prevents external performance from being treated as sufficient.

### Primary sources
- Sahih al-Bukhari 1 — https://sunnah.com/bukhari:1

### Caveats / counterpoints
- Avoid reducing the whole ethical system to intention alone. Intention matters, but action, lawfulness, harm, and rights also matter.

### Safe public wording
Islamic teaching places extraordinary weight on intention; Sahih al-Bukhari opens with the hadith that deeds depend upon intentions.

### Unsafe wording
Islam only cares about intention, not outcome.

## 4. Waswasah: Intrusive Whispers Are Not Identity

**Layer:** Inner World / Mental-Spiritual Protection  
**Status:** Needs tafsir hardening  
**Evidence grade:** A for Qur’anic anchor; B/C for psychological translation until sourced carefully

### Draft claim
Waswasah gives a spiritual framework for separating intrusive whispers from identity and truth.

### Why it matters
This is extremely useful for anxiety, despair, shame, lust, suspicion, and self-harm thoughts when combined with real care and safety.

### Primary sources
- Surah An-Nas — https://quran.com/114

### Sources still needed
- Classical tafsir on “al-waswas al-khannas.”
- Modern Muslim mental health scholarship.
- Clinical material on intrusive thoughts, rumination, and suicidal ideation.

### Caveats / counterpoints
- Never use waswasah to dismiss serious mental health risk.
- Do not imply that all mental illness is demonic whispering.
- In crisis, pair spiritual framing with practical safety and professional support.

### Safe public wording
Waswasah can help a person distinguish intrusive thoughts from truth or identity, but serious self-harm thoughts require immediate safety support and should not be spiritualized away.

### Unsafe wording
Suicidal thoughts are just waswasah; just pray more.

## 5. Medina as Covenant/Society-Building

**Layer:** Prophetic Era / Social Order  
**Status:** Needs careful source caveats  
**Evidence grade:** B until primary-source chain/caveat card is built

### Draft claim
The Medina period shows Islam moving from persecuted moral community to covenantal society with worship, welfare, law, defense, market norms, and intergroup agreements.

### Why it matters
This is the root of studying Islam as public moral architecture, not merely private belief.

### Starting sources
- Britannica overview: Constitution of Medina — https://www.britannica.com/topic/Constitution-of-Medina
- Early sira sources to verify: Ibn Ishaq/Ibn Hisham, Abu Ubayd, later historians.

### Caveats / counterpoints
- The Constitution of Medina text and form require caveats; scholars discuss whether it was one document or multiple agreements.
- Avoid presenting the document as a modern constitution in a simplistic sense.

### Safe public wording
The Medina period contains early covenantal and political arrangements associated with the Prophet’s leadership; the exact textual history of the so-called Constitution of Medina should be handled with scholarly caveats.

### Unsafe wording
The Prophet created the first modern democratic constitution.

## 9. Bimaristan as Institutionalized Mercy

**Layer:** Institution / Golden Age / Healthcare  
**Status:** Needs source-hardening  
**Evidence grade:** B currently; A/B with specialized scholarship

### Draft claim
Medieval Islamic bimaristans were hospital institutions that combined treatment, teaching, specialization, and public welfare, often supported through endowments.

### Why it matters
They show moral values becoming durable healthcare architecture.

### Starting sources
- PMC article on Islamic Golden Age medicine — https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3621228/
- Academic sources to add: Ahmed Ragab, Michael Dols, Emilie Savage-Smith, Peter Pormann.

### Caveats / counterpoints
- Origin claims are disputed.
- Avoid saying “Islam invented hospitals.”
- Distinguish leprosaria, mobile care, hospitals, teaching hospitals, and later institutional forms.

### Safe public wording
Medieval Islamic societies developed influential bimaristan institutions that combined care, medical learning, and public welfare, though precise origin and “first hospital” claims require careful historical qualification.

### Unsafe wording
Muslims invented the modern hospital.
