# Global Mercy Dawah Localization Playbook

**Status:** Operational doctrine v1  
**Date:** 2026-07-17  
**Purpose:** Prevent literal translation, cultural arrogance, covert proselytism, and theological distortion when carrying mercy-first Islamic education into Chinese, Spanish, Arabic, Russian, French, and future languages.

---

## 1. Core doctrine

> **Do not translate words. Translate the moral recognition pathway.**

The project should begin with a truth the audience can already perceive:

- vulnerable life matters;
- cruelty in secret is still wrong;
- power creates responsibility;
- children must be formed before harm occurs;
- compassion must become law and institution.

Then show how Islam:

- names the truth;
- grounds it in God;
- trains it through story and ritual;
- constrains power through law;
- preserves accountability in the unseen;
- calls believers to embody it.

The sequence is:

> conscience → recognition → explanation → formation → institution → invitation

Not:

> tragedy → emotional pressure → conversion demand

---

## 2. The two-direction mission

### Outward dawah

Help non-Muslims understand Islam as a mercy-and-accountability architecture rather than a foreign ethnic identity or arbitrary rule system.

### Inward tajdīd

Call Muslims to rebuild visible mercy in homes, streets, farms, markets, slaughterhouses, schools, and government.

Every language program should contain both.

A campaign that praises Islamic mercy but never audits Muslim practice becomes propaganda.

A campaign that criticizes Muslim practice but hides the Islamic standard loses the source of renewal.

## 3. Three content modes

### Mode A — Universal mercy

No theological prerequisite.

Use for:

- public animal protection;
- child formation;
- bystander action;
- law;
- platform safety;
- memorials.

Example:

> A civilization is measured by what it refuses to do to beings entirely in its power.

### Mode B — Islamic explanation

Explicitly explain:

- animals as communities;
- tawḥīd;
- raḥmah;
- taqwā;
- amānah;
- iḥsān;
- Prophetic stories;
- judgment.

Example:

> Islam teaches that the creature may be under your care, but you remain under God.

### Mode C — Muslim accountability

Directed toward Muslim audiences.

Example:

> Calling something halal does not end the question. Did the animal’s full life reflect iḥsān?

Do not mix all three modes into every post.

Audience clarity produces trust.

---

## 4. Localization workflow

### Step 1 — Define the moral objective

What should the audience:

- feel;
- understand;
- do;
- investigate;
- build?

### Step 2 — Identify local moral vocabulary

Find concepts that already carry legitimacy.

### Step 3 — Map convergences and differences

Do not falsely claim local traditions and Islam teach identical things.

### Step 4 — Draft natively

A native writer should produce the copy from the objective and source material, not merely edit machine translation.

### Step 5 — Theological review

Check Qur’anic, hadith, and doctrinal claims.

### Step 6 — Cultural and political review

Identify phrases likely to sound imperial, insulting, illegal, sectarian, or manipulative.

### Step 7 — Welfare/scientific review

Verify empirical animal claims.

### Step 8 — Small audience test

Test comprehension, emotional response, trust, and perceived coercion.

### Step 9 — Revise

Preserve truth over literal consistency across languages.

### Step 10 — Publish with correction pathway

Make it easy for native readers and scholars to flag problems.

## 5. Chinese-language pathway

### Strategic posture

Do not describe China as spiritually empty or morally deficient.

China contains:

- Confucian humaneness and benevolence;
- Mencian compassion;
- Buddhist concern for sentient beings;
- Daoist non-domination;
- folk religious and ancestral practice;
- Muslim communities with long Chinese histories;
- modern animal-welfare advocates.

The invitation should say:

> Your conscience and traditions already recognize compassion. Islam offers a powerful account of why mercy remains binding in secret and how it becomes a complete moral discipline.

### Useful bridge concepts

- 仁 (*rén*) — humaneness/benevolence;
- 慈悲 — compassion/mercy;
- 天 / 天道 — Heaven or moral order, with caution because it is not equivalent to Allah;
- 敬畏 — reverence/awe;
- 责任 — responsibility;
- 守护 — guardianship/protection;
- 众生 — sentient beings/living beings, often Buddhist resonance;
- 正义 — justice.

### Avoid

- “China has no religion.”
- “Chinese culture does not care about animals.”
- “Islam must civilize China.”
- foreign-nationalist framing;
- direct political provocation;
- covert religious messaging presented as neutral education.

### Content sequence

1. Wang Wang dignity-centered memorial.
2. Child protection and bystander action.
3. Chinese moral traditions of compassion.
4. Comparative mercy traditions.
5. Explicit Islamic animal stories.
6. Invitation to explore tawḥīd, raḥmah, and accountability.

### Review requirements

- mainland Simplified Chinese native writer;
- Chinese Muslim theological reviewer where possible;
- animal-welfare advocate;
- legal/policy sensitivity review;
- verify Wang Wang’s authoritative Chinese name.

## 6. Spanish-language pathway

### Strategic posture

Spanish-speaking cultures are diverse and cannot be treated as one Catholic bloc.

Relevant inheritances may include:

- Catholic mercy and stewardship;
- Indigenous relationships to animals and land;
- secular animal-rights movements;
- family-centered moral language;
- growing curiosity about Islam;
- anti-clerical skepticism in some contexts.

### Useful bridge concepts

- misericordia;
- compasión;
- custodia;
- responsabilidad;
- creación;
- conciencia;
- justicia;
- rendición de cuentas;
- dignidad de la vida.

### Core bridge line

> **La criatura puede estar bajo tu cuidado, pero tú sigues estando bajo el juicio de Dios.**

### Avoid

- assuming all Spanish speakers share one theology;
- presenting Islam only in contrast to Christianity;
- disrespect toward Jesus, Mary, saints, or local traditions;
- overly literal Arabic terms without explanation;
- legal language imported from one country to another.

### Content sequence

1. compassion and protection;
2. Abrahamic continuity;
3. Jesus, mercy, and stewardship where appropriate;
4. Islamic animal narratives;
5. tawḥīd and accountability;
6. Muslim self-audit.

### Review requirements

Use regional review for:

- Mexico;
- Spain;
- Latin American Southern Cone;
- Caribbean;
- U.S. Spanish.

One neutral version may launch first, but regional adaptation should follow.

## 7. Arabic-language pathway

### Strategic posture

Arabic audiences generally do not need a basic introduction to Islamic vocabulary.

The primary need is reactivation and implementation.

The governing question is:

> **If Islam is mercy, where is that mercy visible in our treatment of vulnerable life?**

### Core themes

- al-Raḥmān and al-Raḥīm;
- the Bismillah Test;
- raḥmah toward animals;
- amānah;
- iḥsān beyond technical halal;
- street animals;
- working animals;
- transport and slaughter;
- childhood formation;
- Muslim hypocrisy and renewal without humiliation.

### Core line

> **لسنا بحاجة إلى إثبات أن الإسلام رحمة فقط؛ نحن بحاجة إلى أن نجعل الرحمة مرئية في أفعالنا ومؤسساتنا.**

Working meaning:

> We do not only need to prove that Islam is mercy; we need to make mercy visible in our actions and institutions.

### Avoid

- treating Arabic as one culture;
- sectarian framing;
- attacking ordinary people without addressing systems;
- praising the past without contemporary audit;
- implying all local practices are Islamic;
- using weak hadith without review.

### Review requirements

Separate review for:

- Gulf;
- Levant;
- Egypt;
- Maghreb;
- Iraq;
- Sudan/Horn contexts where relevant.

Use Modern Standard Arabic for core assets, with dialect adaptations for creators.

## 8. Russian-language pathway

### Strategic posture

Likely high-value themes:

- strength under discipline;
- mercy as controlled power rather than weakness;
- conscience under state and group authority;
- hidden accountability;
- Orthodox and Islamic moral inheritances;
- care for animals as evidence of character;
- resistance to sentimental but ineffective messaging.

### Useful bridge concepts

- милосердие — mercy;
- сострадание — compassion;
- ответственность — responsibility;
- совесть — conscience;
- попечение / опека — care/guardianship depending on context;
- страх Божий — fear of God, requiring careful theological framing;
- справедливость — justice.

### Avoid

- geopolitical messaging;
- treating Russian speakers as politically uniform;
- competition with Orthodoxy as the primary frame;
- equating mercy with weakness;
- machine-translated Islamic terminology.

### Content sequence

1. strength that restrains itself;
2. conscience when authority approves;
3. animal stories;
4. Abrahamic comparison;
5. Islamic accountability;
6. institutional protection.

High-value themes:

- universal dignity;
- rights and duties;
- critique of hypocrisy;
- distinction between revelation and institutional capture;
- sentience and law;
- Islam as intellectual and ethical architecture;
- mercy without obscurantism.

### Useful bridge concepts

- miséricorde;
- compassion;
- responsabilité;
- dignité du vivant;
- conscience;
- garde / intendance / responsabilité de dépositaire depending on context;
- justice;
- reddition de comptes.

### Avoid

- defensive grievance as the primary tone;
- implying criticism of Muslim practice is anti-Islam;
- presenting secular people as morally empty;
- importing English culture-war phrases;
- using religious tragedy as a conversion weapon.

### Content sequence

1. universal ethical question;
2. historical and comparative analysis;
3. Qur’anic ontology;
4. blueprint versus implementation;
5. invitation to investigate Islam;
6. Muslim renewal.

- Indonesian;
- Turkish;

## 11. Translation governance

Each language needs:

- lead native editor;
- secondary reviewer;
- Islamic source reviewer;
- welfare/science reviewer;
- cultural-risk reviewer;
- version history;
- correction log;
- glossary.

Arabic terms should not be translated inconsistently.

Create language-specific glossaries for:

- tawḥīd;
- raḥmah;
- taqwā;
- khashyah;
- khawf;
- amānah;
- iḥsān;
- ḥisāb;
- sharīʿah;
- ummah;
- fitrah.

Sometimes transliteration plus explanation is better than one misleading equivalent.

## 12. Anti-exploitation rules

- Never hide explicit Islamic intent inside supposedly neutral welfare content.
- Never make aid conditional on religious engagement.
- Never imply a victim’s death proves Islam in a deductive sense.
- Never pressure grieving audiences to convert.
- Never attack a nation using one atrocity.
- Never circulate graphic footage to increase conversion reach.
- Never overstate scientific or historical claims.
- Never erase other traditions’ moral achievements.
- Never praise Islamic doctrine without auditing Muslim practice.

## 13. Content adaptation matrix

For every asset, define:

| Dimension | Question |
|---|---|
| Audience | Who is this for? |
| Mode | Universal, Islamic explanation, or Muslim accountability? |
| Moral doorway | Which existing intuition or tradition is the entry point? |
| Islamic source | Which Qur’anic or Prophetic material grounds it? |
| Action | What should the audience do next? |
| Risk | What could sound insulting, manipulative, or false? |
| Reviewer | Who must approve before launch? |
| Outcome | What protection or learning will be measured? |

---

## 14. Quality test

A localized asset succeeds when native readers say:

- “This understands how we speak.”
- “This respects our moral inheritance.”
- “This taught me something real about Islam.”
- “This did not manipulate the victim’s suffering.”
- “I know what to do next.”
- “Muslims are applying the same standard to themselves.”

It fails when it merely preserves the English sentence structure.

## 15. Canonical lines

> **Localization is not translation. It is rebuilding the path from conscience to meaning inside another moral language.**

> **Do not begin by demanding that people admire Islam. Begin with a truth their conscience already recognizes.**

> **The strongest dawah is not a slogan translated into six languages. It is mercy made legible, credible, and useful inside six civilizations.**

> **A language should launch only when someone native is ready to steward the trust.**
