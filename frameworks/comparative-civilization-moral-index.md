# Comparative Civilization Moral Index

**Status:** Framework seed  
**Use case:** Comparative meta-history, religion, political philosophy, civilizational benchmarking  
**Core function:** Evaluate civilizations and moral systems relative to historical baseline, not utopian abstraction.

---

## 1. Core thesis

Civilizations should not be judged only by whether they achieved moral perfection. No civilization has.

They should be judged by:

> What did they restrain relative to their baseline? What did they protect? What did they normalize? What did they fail to correct? What trajectory did they create?

A tradition can be imperfect in practice and still represent a major moral upgrade relative to its time.

---

## 2. Why comparative benchmarking matters

Without comparison, critique becomes lazy.

A critic may point to an ancient law and say “barbaric,” without asking:

- What was the surrounding baseline?
- Was this law restricting an existing worse practice?
- Did it create rights where none existed?
- Did it make the vulnerable more visible?
- Did it constrain rulers, warriors, creditors, patriarchs, or slaveholders?
- Did it create a reform trajectory?

Comparison does not excuse evil. It clarifies civilizational direction.

## 3. Scoring axes

Use a 0–5 scale for each axis when useful.

0 = absent or actively harmful  
1 = minimal / inconsistent  
2 = partial / class-limited  
3 = meaningful but incomplete  
4 = strong relative to baseline  
5 = exceptional / transformative relative to baseline

### 3.1 Accountability of rulers

Does the ruler answer to law, God, custom, courts, prophets, scholars, assemblies, or public accountability?

Key question:

> Is the king above morality, or judged by a higher order?

### 3.2 Protection of vulnerable people

Treatment of:

- widows;
- orphans;
- poor;
- strangers;
- slaves;
- captives;
- children;
- elderly;
- disabled;
- minorities;
- debtors.

Key question:

> Who becomes morally visible?

### 3.3 Rules of war

Evaluate:

- noncombatant protection;
- prisoner treatment;
- siege conduct;
- rape prohibition;
- crop/property destruction;
- religious sites;
- proportionality;
- treatment of defeated populations.

Key question:

> Does the society restrain violence when it has military advantage?

### 3.4 Legal restraint and due process

Evaluate:

- evidence standards;
- proportionality;
- class equality;
- judicial independence;
- arbitrary punishment;
- torture;
- corruption;
- appeal mechanisms;
- necessity and intent.

Key question:

> Is law a shield against power or a weapon of power?

### 3.5 Sexual ethics and family protection

Evaluate:

- restraint of predatory sexuality;
- consent norms relative to historical context;
- protection of women and children;
- divorce rights;
- inheritance;
- family obligations;
- exploitation;
- coercive control.

Key question:

> Does sexual order protect dignity or become domination?

### 3.6 Slavery trajectory

Evaluate whether the system:

- normalizes slavery;
- regulates slavery;
- restricts enslavement;
- humanizes treatment;
- creates manumission pathways;
- undermines the institution;
- abolishes it;
- rebrands it under another form.

Key question:

> Does the system move humans toward liberation or stabilize domination?

### 3.7 Knowledge culture

Evaluate:

- literacy;
- preservation of texts;
- translation;
- scholarship;
- philosophy;
- science;
- law;
- universities;
- libraries;
- debate culture;
- intellectual humility.

Key question:

> Does the civilization preserve and expand humanity’s truth-seeking capacity?

### 3.8 Moral universalism

Evaluate whether moral concern applies to:

- only tribe;
- only citizens;
- only class/elite;
- co-religionists;
- strangers;
- enemies;
- all humans.

Key question:

> How far does the moral circle extend?

### 3.9 Anti-corruption mechanism

Evaluate mechanisms such as:

- prophets;
- scholars;
- courts;
- public rebuke;
- constitutional limits;
- councils;
- free press;
- religious obligation;
- divine judgment;
- institutional audit;
- reform movements.

Key question:

> How does the system correct itself when power captures it?

### 3.10 Transcendent accountability

Evaluate whether hidden crimes remain accountable through:

- God;
- afterlife;
- karma;
- ancestral judgment;
- cosmic justice;
- historical memory;
- conscience.

Key question:

> Can evil escape if humans never discover it?

### 3.11 Institutional failure mode

Identify how the system gets corrupted:

- empire capture;
- priestly capture;
- scholar capture;
- oligarchy;
- bureaucracy;
- nationalism;
- market capture;
- ideological purity spirals;
- cult of personality;
- militarization.

Key question:

> What is this system’s predictable corruption pathway?

### 3.12 Internal reform capacity

Evaluate whether the system contains tools to rebuke itself:

- return to scripture;
- prophetic critique;
- repentance;
- legal reform;
- constitutional amendment;
- scholarly disagreement;
- public dissent;
- decentralization;
- renewal movements.

Key question:

> Can the system name and correct its own betrayal?

## 6. Key warnings

### Do not compare ancient reality to modern slogans only

Modern ideals matter, but historical comparison must ask whether a tradition improved on its actual baseline.

### Do not use “relative to baseline” to excuse everything

Some actions can be morally grave even if common in their time. Baseline comparison contextualizes; it does not erase moral judgment.

### Do not flatten civilizations

Every civilization contains internal diversity, class differences, regional variation, reform movements, hypocrisy, and contradictions.

### Do not confuse law on paper with society in practice

Measure both ideals and outcomes.

### Do not confuse elite texts with lived reality

A beautiful text may not govern the masses. A corrupt society may still preserve a powerful moral seed.

---

## 7. Highest-level comparative question

> Which moral operating system best restrains power, desire, greed, violence, and corruption while preserving truth, mercy, family, freedom, dignity, and accountability?

---

## 8. Compressed synthesis

> Comparative meta-history asks not whether any civilization was perfect, but whether it raised humanity above its baseline, protected the vulnerable, restrained domination, expanded moral concern, and built mechanisms to correct its own corruption.
