# Khaldunian Historical Plausibility Gate

**Status:** Operational framework v0.1  
**Parent:** `scholar-instruments/IBN_KHALDUN_INSTRUMENT_CARD.md`  
**Purpose:** Prevent repetition, prestige, sacred labeling, or chain-of-transmission alone from substituting for social, material, institutional, and incentive plausibility.

## Core rule

> **A report is not historically secure merely because it was transmitted. A social theory is not true merely because it sounds plausible. Textual provenance and civilizational possibility must interrogate one another.**

This gate supplements—not replaces—hadith criticism, manuscript study, archaeology, philology, legal method, demographic history, quantitative analysis, and domain expertise.

## The twelve checks

### G1. Exact claim

Reduce the story to concrete propositions.

- Who acted?
- What happened?
- When and where?
- At what scale?
- Through which institution or mechanism?
- What causal conclusion is being drawn?

**Fail condition:** a moral label or civilizational slogan substitutes for a testable claim.

### G2. Source and date

- What is the earliest securely datable witness?
- Is the source contemporary, near-contemporary, or retrospective?
- Is it eyewitness, hearsay, literary construction, legal precedent, sacred memory, or political narrative?
- What are the manuscript and edition limits?

**Fail condition:** later canonization is silently treated as contemporary documentation.

### G3. Independence

- How many genuinely independent witnesses exist?
- Do multiple versions branch from one common source?
- Are local or familial chains late expansions?
- Does verbal similarity suggest dependence?

**Fail condition:** multiplicity of later copies is counted as multiplicity of early witnesses.

### G4. Narrator incentives

Test:

- sectarian loyalty;
- dynastic patronage;
- court access;
- legal-school competition;
- family prestige;
- regional pride;
- defense of inherited office;
- polemical need;
- commercial incentive;
- fear of authority.

**Fail condition:** social position is ignored because a narrator has a prestigious title.

### G5. Material capability

Could the actors actually do what is alleged?

- population;
- food and water;
- transport;
- weapons;
- communications;
- literacy;
- administrative reach;
- revenue;
- storage;
- time;
- geography;
- disease environment.

**Fail condition:** the story requires capacities unavailable to the actors.

### G6. Scale and number

- Are troop counts, casualties, wealth, distances, dates, and construction rates plausible?
- Was the number symbolic, rounded, rhetorical, or copied?
- What logistical footprint should the claim leave?

**Fail condition:** sensational numbers survive only because they are memorable.

### G7. Social mechanism

What causal pathway connects intention to outcome?

- command chain;
- kinship;
- market;
- taxation;
- law;
- bureaucracy;
- religious authority;
- coercion;
- patronage;
- family transmission;
- educational reproduction.

**Fail condition:** “the civilization believed X” replaces an actual mechanism.

### G8. Comparative conditions

- Are two societies being compared at equivalent stages, environments, and scales?
- Is one civilization’s ideal compared to another’s practice?
- Are later categories projected backward?
- Is a local pattern universalized?

**Fail condition:** anachronism or asymmetric comparison carries the verdict.

### G9. Narrative function

Ask what the story does:

- legitimizes a ruler;
- glorifies an ancestor;
- humiliates an opponent;
- creates legal precedent;
- proves divine favor;
- sanctifies a boundary;
- excuses violence;
- explains defeat;
- produces communal cohesion;
- markets outrage.

**Fail condition:** narrative usefulness is mistaken for historical proof.

### G10. Institutional afterlife

- When did the story become useful to institutions?
- Who taught, copied, funded, canonized, or enforced it?
- Did later practice reshape the remembered origin?
- Is an institution retrojecting itself into the founding age?

**Fail condition:** a mature institution’s self-description is treated as a transparent account of its origin.

### G11. Counterevidence and silence

- Which expected sources omit the event?
- What independent account conflicts?
- What material trace is absent?
- Could the silence be explained by genre, loss, taboo, or irrelevance?

**Fail condition:** silence is either ignored or treated as automatic disproof.

### G12. Falsifier and confidence

State:

- what evidence would strengthen the claim;
- what evidence would weaken it;
- what remains unknown;
- confidence by proposition rather than one global label.

**Fail condition:** the claim has no imaginable falsifier or is protected by identity.

## Special applications

### A. Prophetic-history claims

Add:

- revelation/hadith/sira distinction;
- earliest community memory;
- common-link analysis;
- legal or sectarian usefulness;
- whether later norms were projected backward.

### B. Civilizational achievement claims

Add:

- named institution and date;
- predecessors and inheritance;
- funding mechanism;
- workforce and training;
- continuity versus isolated example;
- real access and social distribution.

### C. Atrocity and conquest claims

Add:

- combatant/noncombatant distinction;
- primary narrative genre;
- number inflation risk;
- archaeological/demographic footprint;
- command versus breakdown of discipline;
- local event versus system-wide pattern.

### D. Decline narratives

Add:

- decline of what variable;
- baseline and time window;
- regional heterogeneity;
- manuscript survival and archive bias;
- conquest, plague, trade-route, climate, fiscal, and institutional shocks;
- persistence hidden outside capital cities.

### E. Modern propaganda

Add:

- original clip or transcript;
- edit history;
- algorithmic incentive;
- sponsor or political network;
- repeated phrasing across outlets;
- emotional payload versus evidence.

## Relationship to truth-constrained advocacy

The Truth Court uses this gate internally.

Defense Counsel then asks:

1. Which required proposition failed the gate?
2. Which burden belongs to the accuser?
3. What later memory or institution is being laundered into the root?
4. What concise line makes the evidentiary failure legible?

Example compression:

> **Repeated later is not the same as witnessed earlier. Show the first source, the independent lines, and the mechanism that makes the story possible.**

## Guardrails

Do not use “social impossibility” as a magic dismissal.

- Rare events occur.
- Human beings violate incentives.
- records are lost;
- institutions can achieve surprising coordination;
- models can reflect the researcher’s assumptions.

The gate produces calibrated probability, not metaphysical certainty.

## Canonical maxim

> **Transmission tells us that a story traveled. Plausibility analysis asks whether the world described could have produced it. Historical judgment requires both.**
