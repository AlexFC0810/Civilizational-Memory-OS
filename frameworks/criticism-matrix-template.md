# Criticism Matrix Template

**Status:** Reusable template  
**Use case:** Abrahamic criticism atlas, ideology audits, historical objections, apologetics/polemics analysis  
**Core function:** Convert criticism into structured truth-seeking instead of reactive debate.

---

## 1. Core thesis

Not all criticisms are equal.

Some criticisms are historically serious and morally necessary.  
Some are emotionally powerful but poorly sourced.  
Some are true at one layer but falsely applied to another layer.  
Some attack human corruption and claim to have refuted the original seed.

The Criticism Matrix forces clarity.

---

## 2. Matrix fields

| Field | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Claim | What is being alleged? |
| Tradition / target | Christianity, Islam, Judaism, Abrahamic faiths, secular ideology, institution, empire, etc. |
| Source layer | Scripture, hadith, fiqh, church tradition, tafsir, empire, culture, modern practice, etc. |
| Primary source | Exact text, manuscript, historical record, report, law code, or event. |
| Strongest good-faith version | The critique stated in its most serious and fair form. |
| Weak polemical version | The overclaimed, emotionally loaded, or misleading form. |
| Historical baseline | What was normal in the surrounding context? |
| Interpretive range | How have serious interpreters understood this? |
| Best response | The strongest honest answer without dodging. |
| What remains unresolved | Tensions that should not be hand-waved. |
| Confidence level | High / medium / low. |
| Research needed | Sources, scholars, primary texts, or comparative data to verify. |

## 4. Example: Aisha’s age

## 1. Claim

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ married Aisha when she was six and consummated the marriage when she was nine.

## 2. Tradition / target

Islam / Prophet Muhammad ﷺ / hadith transmission.

## 3. Source layer

Hadith report tradition, not Qur’an.

## 4. Primary source

Traditional hadith reports, especially in Sahih al-Bukhari and related collections. Requires source ledger verification before publication.

## 5. Strongest good-faith version

Canonical Sunni hadith collections contain reports giving Aisha’s age as six at marriage and nine at consummation. Modern moral intuitions view this as deeply troubling, and the report has been used by critics as a central objection to the Prophet’s moral example.

## 6. Weak polemical version

“Muhammad definitely abused a child; therefore Islam is false.”

## 7. Historical baseline

Ancient and late antique marriage norms differed from modern norms, but baseline context alone does not resolve the historical question of the report’s reliability.

## 8. Interpretive range

Traditional Sunni acceptance exists. Modern Muslim and academic critiques challenge the report’s historicity through isnad/matn analysis, chronology, and transmission criticism. Joshua Little’s work is a major research node.

## 9. Best response

The 6/9 claim is hadith-based, not Qur’anic. The report exists in canonical collections, so it should not be denied. But its historicity is contested. The strongest careful position is that Aisha’s exact age is historically uncertain, and polemical certainty exceeds the evidence.

## 10. What remains unresolved

How mainstream Muslim communities should handle canonical reports whose historicity or moral implications are contested.

## 11. Confidence level

Medium pending full source review.

## 12. Research needed

- Joshua Little thesis.
- Traditional hadith sources.
- Asma chronology arguments.
- Early legal usage or absence.
- Academic responses to Little.

## 5. Example: theft punishment and starvation

## 1. Claim

Islam would cut off the hand of a starving person who steals bread.

## 2. Tradition / target

Islam / Qur’an / Sharia / hudud law.

## 3. Source layer

Qur’an plus jurisprudence, not a single verse in isolation.

## 4. Primary source

Qur’an 5:38 and classical legal discussions. Requires source ledger verification before publication.

## 5. Strongest good-faith version

The Qur’an mentions hand cutting for theft, which is morally shocking to modern readers and can appear harsh or barbaric if applied mechanically.

## 6. Weak polemical version

“Islam kills thieves” or “Islam cuts off starving people’s hands for bread.”

## 7. Historical baseline

Ancient and premodern societies often used severe punishments. The relevant question is whether Islamic law constrained arbitrary punishment through evidentiary standards, thresholds, necessity, and public responsibility.

## 8. Interpretive range

Classical Islamic jurisprudence treated theft hadd as subject to conditions. Necessity, famine, coercion, ownership ambiguity, thresholds, and evidentiary requirements matter.

## 9. Best response

No legal text contains all jurisprudence in one sentence. Justice is not mechanical literalism. The starving-person case is exactly where necessity and social failure become relevant, and the moral burden shifts toward society and governance.

## 10. What remains unresolved

Modern application, state legitimacy, abuse risk, and whether corporal punishment should exist today.

## 11. Confidence level

Medium-high pending source review.

## 12. Research needed

- Qur’an 5:38.
- Reports on Umar and famine.
- Classical fiqh thresholds.
- Modern maqasid and hudud scholarship.

---

## 6. Debate posture

The right tone:

> I agree this is a serious criticism. I do not want to dodge it. Let’s identify the source layer, the strongest version of the objection, the historical context, the internal interpretive range, and what remains unresolved.

This posture builds trust because it is neither defensive nor submissive to weak polemics.

---

## 7. Compressed synthesis

> The Criticism Matrix turns objections into disciplined inquiry. It lets us accept legitimate criticism, reject overclaiming, separate source layers, and preserve truth without collapsing into apologetics or polemics.
