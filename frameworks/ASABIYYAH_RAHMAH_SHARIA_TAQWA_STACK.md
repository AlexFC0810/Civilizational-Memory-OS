# ʿAsabiyyah–Raḥmah–Sharīʿah–Taqwā Stack

**Status:** Reusable framework v1  
**Date:** 2026-07-17  
**Use case:** Civilizational analysis, Islamic political theory, group cohesion, moral formation, social movement design, animal ethics, institutional architecture

---

## 1. Core formula

> **ʿAsabiyyah supplies collective power.  
> Raḥmah determines whom that power can see and protect.  
> Sharīʿah constrains what that power may do.  
> Taqwā restrains it when nobody is watching.**

This framework integrates Ibn Khaldun’s analysis of group solidarity with Islamic moral and legal architecture.

It corrects two common errors:

1. treating social cohesion as automatically moral;
2. treating mercy as sufficient without collective capacity, law, and internal accountability.

---

## 2. ʿAsabiyyah: collective capability

ʿAsabiyyah is the capacity of a group to:

- trust;
- sacrifice;
- coordinate;
- endure;
- mobilize;
- defend;
- conquer;
- build;
- survive stress.

Sources of ʿasabiyyah may include:

- kinship;
- shared hardship;
- religion;
- ideology;
- common enemy;
- neighborhood;
- military discipline;
- organizational identity;
- shared mission;
- mutual dependence.

ʿAsabiyyah is morally neutral.

High cohesion may power:

- liberation;
- rescue;
- mutual aid;
- conquest;
- organized crime;
- genocide;
- institution-building;
- authoritarianism.

Diagnostic question:

> **What does this group’s solidarity enable it to do—and to whom?**

---

## 3. Raḥmah: moral visibility and protective direction

Raḥmah expands the circle of concern beyond strategic reciprocity.

It asks:

- Who is vulnerable?
- Who can suffer?
- Who is dependent?
- Who lacks leverage?
- Who is unseen by the group?
- Who is outside the tribe?
- Who is beneath the system’s power?

Raḥmah does not abolish boundaries or justice.

It changes the purpose and manner of power.

It prevents the group from concluding:

> Not ours, therefore disposable.

Diagnostic question:

> **Whose pain becomes visible to this group, and whose remains invisible?**

---

## 4. Sharīʿah: durable external constraint

Here Sharīʿah is used in the broad sense of divinely oriented moral-legal guidance, not as a slogan for state coercion.

Its function is to translate moral vision into:

- duties;
- prohibitions;
- procedures;
- rights;
- standards;
- evidentiary rules;
- boundaries;
- accountability;
- institutional practices.

Sentiment varies.

Law attempts to preserve protection when sentiment weakens.

A complete analysis must distinguish:

- revelation;
- Prophetic interpretation;
- jurisprudence;
- school differences;
- state law;
- custom;
- enforcement;
- historical outcome.

Diagnostic question:

> **What can the group not lawfully do, even when it wants to and possesses the power?**

---

## 5. Taqwā: internal constraint in the unseen

No law observes everything.

Taqwā operates where:

- enforcement is absent;
- evidence is weak;
- the victim cannot testify;
- the group approves wrongdoing;
- self-interest rewards betrayal;
- a loophole exists;
- the ruler controls the court.

Taqwā is the internal guard against convenience morality.

Diagnostic question:

> **What restrains the person when wrongdoing is profitable, secret, and socially rewarded?**

---

## 6. Failure combinations

### ʿAsabiyyah without raḥmah

Produces:

- tribal domination;
- chauvinism;
- conquest;
- collective cruelty;
- loyalty over truth;
- strong protection for insiders and danger for outsiders.

### Raḥmah without ʿasabiyyah

Produces:

- moral sentiment without capacity;
- compassion unable to defend victims;
- fragmented advocacy;
- exhaustion;
- symbolic concern without institution.

### Sharīʿah without raḥmah

Produces:

- legalism;
- loophole ethics;
- punitive culture;
- technical compliance without moral perception;
- law used to dominate rather than protect.

### Sharīʿah without taqwā

Produces:

- hidden violation;
- corruption;
- selective enforcement;
- ritualized hypocrisy;
- obedience only under inspection.

### Taqwā without law

Produces:

- reliance on unusually virtuous individuals;
- inconsistent protection;
- inability to scale;
- victims dependent on private conscience.

### Raḥmah without justice

Produces:

- enabling aggression;
- refusal to restrain abusers;
- sentimentality;
- abandonment of victims in the name of softness.

### ʿAsabiyyah captured by state or leader

Produces:

- personality cult;
- sacred nationalism;
- outsourcing of conscience;
- interpretation monopolized by power.

## 7. Fifth and sixth layers: institution and correction

The four-part stack needs two implementation layers.

### Institution

Converts principle into durable protection:

- schools;
- courts;
- charities;
- endowments;
- professional standards;
- welfare systems;
- reporting pathways;
- market rules.

### Correction

Detects capture and failure:

- independent scholarship;
- audit;
- dissent;
- appeals;
- transparency;
- historical memory;
- source review;
- empirical evidence;
- repentance and reform.

Expanded formula:

> **ʿAsabiyyah → Raḥmah → Sharīʿah → Taqwā → Institution → Correction**

## 8. Application to animal ethics

### ʿAsabiyyah

Can a community mobilize to rescue, educate, enforce, and reform?

### Raḥmah

Does the animal’s suffering become visible despite lacking status?

### Sharīʿah

What conduct is forbidden or required in care, work, transport, and slaughter?

### Taqwā

What restrains abuse behind closed doors?

### Institution

Are there inspections, reporting systems, welfare standards, schools, and enforcement?

### Correction

Can new welfare evidence improve the practice? Can failures be admitted without treating criticism as an attack on Islam?

---

## 9. Application to war

### ʿAsabiyyah

Does the society possess the cohesion to defend itself?

### Raḥmah

Are enemy civilians, captives, and the wounded morally visible?

### Sharīʿah

What methods remain prohibited even under existential stress?

### Taqwā

What restrains soldiers and leaders where evidence is absent?

### Institution

Are orders, training, investigation, and accountability aligned?

### Correction

Can the society investigate its own side without treating truth as treason?

---

## 10. Application to movements and organizations

A movement should be audited:

- Is it cohesive enough to execute?
- Does it care only for members?
- What may it not do for the cause?
- What restrains private corruption?
- Are values embedded in systems?
- Can criticism reach leadership?

A mission-driven organization with high ʿasabiyyah but weak correction can become cultic.

A compassionate organization with weak ʿasabiyyah can remain ineffective.

## 11. Application to Peludito / House of Mercy

### Required ʿasabiyyah

- multilingual creator network;
- researchers;
- educators;
- animal-welfare professionals;
- legal advocates;
- donors;
- technical builders;
- faith communities.

### Required raḥmah

- protect all vulnerable animals, not only culturally favored pets;
- preserve dignity of victims;
- avoid sadism toward offenders;
- include child rehabilitation;
- refuse ethnic blame.

### Required Sharīʿah / normative constraint

- truth standards;
- no graphic exploitation;
- no unverifiable claims;
- no covert conversion pressure;
- no selective compassion;
- no donor capture.

### Required taqwā

- do not exaggerate when exaggeration would help reach;
- do not use tragedy for status;
- do not conceal Muslim failures;
- do not treat noble purpose as moral immunity.

### Required institution

- source ledger;
- native review;
- curriculum;
- reporting system;
- law tracker;
- outcome dashboard.

### Required correction

- independent review;
- public corrections;
- welfare and scholarly panels;
- conflict disclosure;
- victim-centered metrics.

## 12. Scoring rubric

Rate each layer from 0–5.

### ʿAsabiyyah

0 = fragmented, no capacity  
5 = high trust and execution capacity

### Raḥmah

0 = outsiders and weak beings invisible  
5 = broad, disciplined moral concern

### Sharīʿah / normative constraint

0 = power defines permission  
5 = clear constraints binding insiders and leaders

### Taqwā

0 = conduct depends entirely on detection  
5 = strong internal accountability in private

### Institution

0 = values remain slogans  
5 = values embedded in durable systems

### Correction

0 = criticism punished, failures hidden  
5 = truth can reach power and trigger reform

A high total does not prove perfection.

It identifies whether collective capacity has been morally directed and made durable.

## 13. Canonical questions

> What does this group protect when protection is costly?

> Who remains outside its circle of concern?

> What is it forbidden to do for victory?

> What restrains members when nobody is watching?

> What institutions preserve the standard after founders die?

> Can truth correct the group without being treated as betrayal?

---

## 14. Compressed synthesis

> **Civilization needs cohesion strong enough to act, mercy broad enough to see beyond the tribe, law strong enough to constrain power, conscience strong enough to survive secrecy, institutions strong enough to outlive individuals, and correction strong enough to resist capture.**
