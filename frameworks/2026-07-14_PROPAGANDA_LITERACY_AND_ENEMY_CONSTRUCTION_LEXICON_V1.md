# Propaganda Literacy and Enemy-Construction Lexicon — V1

> **STATUS: NONCANONICAL FRAMEWORK PROPOSAL**  
> This is a source-audit and public-literacy candidate. It distinguishes established concepts from project-specific working terms. No allegation of coordination, intent, genocide, incitement, or criminal responsibility becomes established merely because a term appears here.

## Mission

The public should not depend on one skilled debater to rebut every future smear.

The stronger objective is:

> **Give people names for the recurring manipulation patterns so they can recognize the machinery themselves.**

Naming a pattern converts:

> “Something about this feels manipulative”

into:

> “I can identify the selection, category jump, threat inflation, double standard, and permission being manufactured.”

This is conceptual self-defense, not tribal counter-propaganda.

## Governing guardrail

> **Do not invent a hidden mastermind when visible incentives and institutions are sufficient to explain the pattern.**

Manufactured consent can arise through aligned structures—ownership, sourcing, access, advertising, flak, political incentives, audience reward, and algorithmic amplification—without a single secret command center.

Function does not prove private intent.

## The enemy-making pipeline

```text
Selection
  choose the most inflammatory event, speaker, clip, or symbol
      ↓
Fusion
  merge source, actor, government, criminal, civilization, and population
      ↓
Inflation
  make the exceptional appear typical, coordinated, and inevitable
      ↓
Securitization
  redefine a social or religious population as an existential emergency
      ↓
Moral exclusion
  weaken ordinary demands for individuality, evidence, rights, and proportionality
      ↓
Permission
  make surveillance, censorship, exclusion, collective punishment, war, or dispossession feel necessary
```

Master line:

> **Propaganda does not begin by asking you to hate an entire population. It begins by teaching you to treat every event involving one member as evidence about every other member.**

## Established concepts

### 1. Manufacturing consent / propaganda model

A structural model associated especially with Edward S. Herman and Noam Chomsky. Media output can become systematically favorable to powerful interests through ownership, advertising, dependence on official sources, organized flak, and a dominant enemy ideology.

Correct use:

- explains recurring structural selection and framing;
- does not require direct censorship or a conspiracy meeting;
- should be tested against counterexamples and media disagreement.

Public question:

> **Who owns the platform, who funds it, who supplies its routine facts, who can punish dissent, and which enemy frame organizes the story?**

### 2. Agenda-setting

Media may not determine exactly what people think, but can strongly influence what they repeatedly think about.

Audit:

- Which events receive saturation?
- Which comparable events receive silence?
- Which questions never enter the frame?

### 3. Framing

The selection of language, context, causal story, actors, and moral emphasis through which an event is interpreted.

Examples:

- “security operation” versus “collective punishment”;
- “clash” versus identifying who attacked whom;
- “religious threat” versus a named actor and policy.

### 4. Priming

Repeated coverage teaches audiences which criteria to use when judging a group or policy.

If Muslims are repeatedly encountered only through terrorism, migration panic, or foreign war, those categories become the default evaluation criteria even when they are irrelevant.

### 5. Moral panic

A condition, event, or group becomes defined as a threat to society; unusual examples are amplified until they appear typical, and moral entrepreneurs demand control measures.

Key diagnostic:

> **Was the untypical made typical?**

### 6. Securitization

A political or social issue is converted into an existential security threat, allowing extraordinary measures to be presented as necessary.

Public question:

> **When did a disagreement, immigrant, believer, or minority become an “emergency” for which normal rights no longer apply?**

### 7. Essentialism

Treating a vast and internally diverse population as though every member expresses one fixed essence.

Example:

> “Muslims are…”

without specifying which Muslims, where, when, which school, which source, which institution, or what measured prevalence.

### 8. Out-group homogeneity bias

The tendency to perceive members of another group as more alike than members of one’s own group.

Asymmetry:

- “Our side contains individuals, denominations, politics, contexts, and exceptions.”
- “Their side is one thing.”

### 9. Scapegoating

Assigning broad social anxieties, decline, crime, war, or disorder to a convenient population that cannot plausibly explain the whole problem.

### 10. Collective guilt

Assigning responsibility to people because they share identity with an offender rather than because they participated in the offense.

Master question:

> **What did this individual do—not what identity can you attach to them?**

### 11. Dehumanization and moral exclusion

Dehumanization diminishes the perceived humanity, individuality, emotions, or moral worth of a population. Moral exclusion places people outside the circle to whom ordinary fairness and protection are owed.

Warning signs:

- population described as infestation, disease, flood, animals, or demographic weapon;
- children treated primarily as future threats;
- civilian identity presumed fraudulent;
- suffering described as deserved, staged, or strategically irrelevant.

### 12. Attribution asymmetry

The same behavior is interpreted differently depending on group identity.

Example:

- a Christian offender is treated as an individual who betrayed Christianity;
- a Muslim offender is treated as Islam revealing itself.

Public question:

> **Why does your side receive individuality while the other side receives essence?**

### 13. Selection bias / cherry-picking

Retrieving only examples that support the preferred conclusion while ignoring the comparison universe.

Historical form:

- battles without hospitals;
- rulers without scholars;
- punishments without legal restraints;
- failed modern states without civilizational chronology;
- one extremist without population base rates.

### 14. Availability cascade and repetition effects

A claim feels prevalent or true because vivid examples are repeatedly available in memory and socially repeated.

Audit:

- How common is it?
- Compared with what base rate?
- Are the examples independent, or is one event being recycled?

### 15. Gish gallop

A rapid pile of claims whose combined rebuttal requires far more time than their assertion.

Response:

> **Freeze one claim. Name the source. Finish it before the next accusation receives borrowed credibility.**

### 16. Motte-and-bailey

A speaker advances a dramatic claim, then retreats to a modest and defensible version when challenged, later returning to the dramatic version.

Example pattern:

- Bailey: “We are at war with Muslims.”
- Motte: “I only mean we should discuss extremism.”

### 17. Moving goalposts and unfalsifiable threat theories

Evidence standards change after the original burden is met.

Unfalsifiable version:

- violent Muslim = proof of aggression;
- peaceful Muslim = proof of deception;
- integrated Muslim = proof of infiltration;
- disagreement = proof of concealment.

A theory that treats every possible observation as confirmation is not functioning as an evidence-based theory.

### 18. Worthy and unworthy victims

A concept used in propaganda analysis: some victims receive names, biographies, photographs, grief, presumed credibility, and sustained coverage; others become anonymous aggregates, disputed numbers, passive constructions, or strategic inconveniences.

Audit:

- Who receives a face?
- Whose death has an actor?
- Whose numbers are reflexively doubted?
- Whose suffering is constantly followed by a justification clause?

### 19. Agent deletion and passive voice

Language removes the responsible actor:

- “People died.”
- “A hospital was hit.”
- “Violence erupted.”

The grammatical form may conceal causation, agency, or responsibility.

### 20. Atrocity propaganda

True, false, exaggerated, decontextualized, or selectively presented accounts of atrocities used to mobilize hatred, war, collective punishment, or exceptional measures.

Important boundary:

> Naming atrocity propaganda does not mean every atrocity report is false. It means atrocity claims require evidence precisely because their emotional and political power is enormous.

### 21. Black propaganda, false flag, and literal black flags

These are different.

- **Black propaganda:** content falsely presented as coming from a source other than its real creator.
- **False-flag operation:** an operation designed to appear as though another actor carried it out.
- **Literal black flag:** a symbol whose meaning depends on culture and context; it may signify mourning, religious memory, militancy, piracy, or something else.

Do not use “black flag” as a generic synonym for secret manipulation.

Required question:

> **Do you mean a false flag, black propaganda, or an actual black-colored flag—and what evidence establishes its meaning in this case?**

## Project-specific working terms

These terms should be marked as project language unless a direct scholarly lineage is later established.

### Source collapse

Merging Qur’an, hadith, sīrah, fiqh, empire, state policy, culture, criminal conduct, and ordinary believers into one undifferentiated source called “Islam.”

### Civilizational montage

Editing unrelated centuries, dynasties, actors, and events into one emotionally continuous trailer while deleting chronology and causation.

### Threat-class construction

Turning an identity category into a population presumed dangerous before individual evidence is considered.

### Asymmetric literalism

One side’s extreme rhetoric receives context, metaphor, humor, or retreat; the other side’s gestures, hopes, symbols, or rhetoric are interpreted in the most literal and threatening possible way.

### Symbolic threat inflation

Assigning the most militarized or apocalyptic meaning to a symbol without establishing its language, tradition, speaker, time, or local context.

### Evidence-to-permission pipeline

The process by which a selected event is transformed into public permission for actions against a much wider population.

## The TRACE diagnostic

Teach audiences to run five questions:

### T — Target the exact claim

What precisely is being alleged?

### R — Resolve the source and responsible actor

Qur’an, hadith, jurist, government, organization, criminal, or individual?

### A — Audit evidence, prevalence, and comparison

How many cases? What denominator? Compared with whom, when, and under what conditions?

### C — Catch the category jump

How did one event become evidence about a religion, civilization, race, immigrant population, or two billion people?

### E — Examine the permission being prepared

What emotion, policy, exclusion, surveillance, censorship, deportation, war, or indifference is the frame asking the audience to accept?

Master line:

> **Do not ask only, “Is this claim true?” Ask, “What category jump does it perform, and what permission is it preparing?”**

## Palestine and Gaza legal-language precision

The moral case must not rely on a false legal claim.

Current source-safe formulation for the July 2026 record:

> **A United Nations Human Rights Council-appointed independent Commission of Inquiry concluded in September 2025 that Israel is committing genocide in Gaza. The International Court of Justice genocide case remains pending on the merits; the Court issued binding provisional measures in 2024. Israel rejects the genocide allegation and the Commission’s conclusion.**

Do not say:

- “The ICJ ruled that Israel committed genocide.”
- “Every UN body has legally adjudicated genocide.”
- “Disagreement over the legal classification erases the documented civilian catastrophe.”

The propaganda-literacy relevance is not to instrumentalize Palestinian suffering as a debate prop. It is to examine whether Muslim and Palestinian civilians are individualized, believed, and protected by the same standards applied to favored victims.

## Public-teaching rule

Do not dump twenty definitions into a debate.

Use:

1. name one technique;
2. define it in one sentence;
3. point to the exact move that just occurred;
4. ask one burden-shifting question;
5. return to the human consequence.

Example:

> **“That is securitization: you took a religious population and reclassified it as an emergency. Name the specific actor and plan before asking people to surrender ordinary standards of evidence and rights.”**

## Final doctrine

> **A rebuttal saves one argument. A concept can protect an audience from the next hundred arguments.**

> **Teach people to see the machinery, not merely disagree with its latest output.**
