# Conquest Is Not Conversion — A Causal Map for Religious Expansion Claims

**Status:** Proposal / research framework candidate  
**Date:** 2026-07-13  
**Write policy:** Suggestion branch only; not canonical until reviewed and merged  
**Related systems:** Load-Bearing Truth Kernel · Historical Calibration · Transcript-to-Immunity

## Purpose

Public arguments routinely collapse several different historical processes into one sentence:

> “Islam spread by the sword.”

That sentence may refer—often without distinction—to:

- a scripture authorizing war;
- a ruler launching a campaign;
- an army conquering territory;
- a state governing non-Muslims;
- legal or economic pressure;
- forced conversion;
- voluntary conversion;
- intermarriage;
- trade and missionary activity;
- elite adoption;
- language change;
- cultural assimilation;
- a population becoming majority Muslim centuries later.

These are not the same event.

> **Before judging how a religion spread, separate the spread of armies, states, institutions, identities, and beliefs.**

---

## The three verbs

The highest-leverage distinction is between three verbs:

### 1. Conquered

A military force defeated another political authority and acquired territory.

### 2. Ruled

A state established administration, taxation, law, security, hierarchy, and relations among religious communities.

### 3. Converted

A person or population adopted a religion, whether through conviction, family formation, patronage, status incentives, social pressure, coercion, or force.

A region can be:

- conquered without being rapidly converted;
- ruled for centuries while retaining large non-Muslim populations;
- converted without foreign conquest;
- politically lost while remaining religiously Muslim;
- linguistically Arabized without becoming immediately Muslim;
- Islamized without becoming Arabized.

> **A sword can change a government in a day. It cannot by itself explain why millions of families reproduce a religious identity across centuries.**

## The causal stack

Use this stack for every “spread by the sword” claim.

```text
NORMATIVE TEXT AND THEOLOGY
            ↓
LEGAL AND POLITICAL INTERPRETATION
            ↓
STATE INCENTIVES AND ELITE STRATEGY
            ↓
MILITARY CAMPAIGN / TREATY / ANNEXATION
            ↓
ADMINISTRATIVE RULE
            ↓
STATUS, TAX, SECURITY, AND SOCIAL INCENTIVES
            ↓
MISSIONARY, TRADE, SCHOLARLY, AND FAMILY NETWORKS
            ↓
INDIVIDUAL AND HOUSEHOLD CONVERSION
            ↓
INTERGENERATIONAL IDENTITY CHANGE
            ↓
DEMOGRAPHIC MAJORITY
```

Every arrow is a research question rather than an assumption.

## Required questions

### Normative layer

- What does the Qur'an explicitly say?
- Which passages concern a specific war, treaty violation, defense, or general principle?
- Which later doctrines were derived from those passages?
- Is a juristic doctrine being presented as a direct Qur'anic sentence?

### Military layer

- Who initiated the campaign?
- What polity, dynasty, army, and century are involved?
- Was the territory taken by battle, surrender treaty, alliance, rebellion, or succession?
- What happened to civilians, captives, religious buildings, property, and local elites?

### Governance layer

- Which communities retained worship, institutions, property, and internal law?
- Which legal disabilities, taxes, exclusions, or humiliations existed?
- Did policy vary by ruler, place, period, and denomination?
- Were administrative systems preserved or replaced?

### Conversion layer

- How quickly did the population become Muslim?
- What evidence supports the demographic estimate?
- Were conversions individual, household, elite-led, communal, incentivized, coerced, or forced?
- Did conversion occur during the conquest, generations later, or without conquest?

### Comparison layer

- Compared with which contemporary empire or conquest regime?
- Are Muslim states being compared with modern liberal ideals while rivals are compared with their own ideals?
- Are exceptions being treated as the norm—or norms used to erase exceptions?

## Named failure modes

### Conquest–conversion collapse

Military acquisition of territory is treated as proof that the population was forcibly converted.

### Dynasty continuity illusion

Rashidun, Umayyad, Abbasid, Fatimid, Seljuk, Ayyubid, Ottoman, Safavid, Mughal, and modern Muslim actors are treated as one continuous intentional agent.

### Chronological compression

Centuries of shifting borders, civil wars, trade, treaties, migration, and local agency are compressed into one uninterrupted attack.

### Retrospective defense laundering

A later war is declared morally defensive by aggregating every earlier conflict involving people with the same broad religious label.

### Civilizational guilt inheritance

A modern individual is assigned responsibility or presumed intent because a premodern state sharing their religion conducted conquest.

### Ideal–implementation asymmetry

One tradition is judged by scripture or saints while the other is judged by its worst ruler, army, or criminal.

### Conversion monocause

One cause—usually violence or peaceful preaching—is used to explain a process that varied across regions and centuries.

### Archive fatalism

“History is written by the victors” is used to dismiss all records rather than analyzing provenance, incentives, survival, genre, and corroboration.

## The history-is-written-by-victors correction

Power shapes archives. Victors often commission monuments, chronicles, school curricula, and official memory. Defeated communities may lose libraries, institutions, languages, and the ability to circulate their account.

But the slogan is not a method.

For the Crusades and many medieval conflicts, historians can compare:

- Latin chronicles and letters;
- Greek and Byzantine narratives;
- Arabic chronicles and biographies;
- Syriac and Armenian accounts;
- Jewish testimony;
- legal documents;
- treaties;
- pilgrimage and travel accounts;
- archaeology, architecture, coins, and inscriptions.

Losers, minorities, bystanders, and rival factions also wrote history.

> **History is power-shaped, not victor-monopolized.**

The correct response to bias is source criticism and triangulation—not permission to believe the preferred side without evidence.

## Balanced truth conditions

A defensible synthesis must be able to say all of the following when supported:

1. Early Muslim states expanded through major military conquests.
2. Those conquests involved real warfare, death, displacement, captivity, taxation, and hierarchy.
3. Conquest did not usually produce immediate mass conversion across the entire conquered population.
4. Islamization often took generations or centuries and followed different mechanisms by region.
5. Some Muslim rulers and movements used coercion or forced conversion.
6. Other large Muslim populations emerged substantially through trade, scholarship, family networks, Sufi orders, and elite adoption without Arab conquest.
7. Non-Muslims under Muslim rule were neither always equal nor always facing conversion or death.
8. Historical Muslim wrongdoing cannot be erased by Qur'anic ideals or Golden Age achievements.
9. Historical imperial conduct cannot be transferred automatically into the intentions of a modern Muslim citizen.

## Public retrieval questions

> **Are you talking about the spread of Muslim rule—or the spread of Muslim belief?**

> **If conquest automatically meant forced conversion, why did major conquered regions retain Christian, Jewish, Zoroastrian, Hindu, or other populations for centuries?**

> **Which dynasty, which country, which century, and which conversion mechanism?**

> **Did Islam reach this population through an army, a trader, a ruler, a scholar, a marriage network, a state incentive, or coercion?**

> **What evidence connects a medieval empire's campaign to this modern Muslim's intentions?**

---

## Master synthesis

> **Islam spread through more than one mechanism. Muslim states often spread by conquest; Muslim belief often spread more slowly through governance, incentives, trade, scholarship, family networks, local adoption, and sometimes coercion. “Islam spread by the sword” is therefore too broad to be a serious historical verdict, while “Islam never spread through force” is also false. The truthful question is: which layer, mechanism, region, and century?**
