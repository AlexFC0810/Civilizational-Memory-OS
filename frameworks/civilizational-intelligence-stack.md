# Civilizational Intelligence Stack

**Status:** Canonical framework seed  
**Purpose:** Model civilization as an interacting stack rather than a single ideology, institution, or economic variable.

---

## Core definition

> **Civilizational Intelligence is the capacity of a society to perceive reality, preserve wisdom, coordinate action, constrain domination, protect the vulnerable, correct error, and transmit meaning across generations.**

A civilization can be wealthy, literate, militarily strong, and technologically advanced while remaining civilizationally unintelligent.

The decisive question is not how much power it possesses.

It is whether it can govern that power without losing truth, mercy, legitimacy, or the ability to correct itself.

---

## The five-layer stack

### Layer 0 — Reality

The non-negotiable substrate:

- physics;
- biology;
- ecology;
- energy;
- mortality;
- geography;
- scarcity;
- disease;
- human cognitive limits.

A civilization that denies reality accumulates hidden debt.

### Layer 1 — Material capacity

The production substrate:

- agriculture;
- industry;
- infrastructure;
- logistics;
- energy;
- medicine;
- housing;
- transport;
- communications;
- technology.

Material capacity determines what a society can do, not what it should do.

### Layer 2 — Institutions

The coordination substrate:

- law;
- courts;
- state;
- markets;
- property;
- welfare;
- schools;
- universities;
- military;
- media;
- unions;
- associations;
- religious institutions.

Institutions convert values and resources into repeated behavior.

### Layer 3 — Culture

The social substrate:

- family;
- trust;
- honor;
- shame;
- language;
- ritual;
- identity;
- norms;
- memory;
- habits;
- expectations;
- models of adulthood.

Culture determines what people do before law intervenes.

### Layer 4 — Meaning

The ultimate-orientation substrate:

- religion;
- philosophy;
- purpose;
- sacred limits;
- transcendence;
- sacrifice;
- hope;
- death;
- ultimate accountability;
- conceptions of the good.

Meaning answers why people should endure cost for truth, justice, family, strangers, and future generations.

---

## The four civilizational questions

Every civilization must answer:

1. **What is true?** — Epistemology.
2. **What is good?** — Ethics.
3. **Who decides?** — Governance.
4. **Why sacrifice?** — Meaning.

A system that answers only one or two is incomplete.

Science can help answer what is empirically true but cannot alone decide ultimate purpose.

Markets can coordinate production but cannot alone determine what must never be sold.

Democracy can distribute voice but cannot alone establish what a majority must never do.

Religion can supply sacred limits and meaning but can become oppressive when interpretation and power lack accountability.

---

## Stack failure modes

### Material failure

Scarcity, famine, infrastructure collapse, disease, and economic desperation destabilize higher layers.

### Institutional failure

Law becomes selective, democracy becomes auction politics, religion becomes clerical monopoly, and markets become extraction.

### Cultural failure

Trust collapses, family formation weakens, honor detaches from virtue, and atomization expands.

### Meaning failure

People possess comfort without purpose, freedom without direction, and power without sacred limits.

### Reality failure

Propaganda, dogma, ideology, or elite capture blocks corrective information.

---

## Cross-layer rule

> **No layer can replace the whole stack.**

Common reduction errors:

- economics explains everything;
- religion explains everything;
- technology solves everything;
- democracy guarantees justice;
- science supplies morality;
- material abundance creates meaning;
- culture alone overcomes scarcity;
- law alone creates virtue.

Civilizational strength comes from alignment across layers.

---

## Diagnostic protocol

For any civilization, movement, state, ideology, or institution, ask:

### Reality

- What facts can the system not admit?
- How does evidence reach decision-makers?
- What happens to dissent?

### Material

- What does the system produce?
- Who receives the surplus?
- What vulnerabilities does scarcity create?

### Institutions

- Who holds power?
- What checks exist?
- How are failures corrected?
- Can money purchase law or legitimacy?

### Culture

- What behavior is rewarded?
- What is normalized?
- How is trust reproduced?
- What kind of adult is the culture trying to create?

### Meaning

- What is treated as sacred?
- What justifies sacrifice?
- What must never be done, even if profitable?
- What does the system believe human beings are for?

## Civilizational intelligence scorecard

A high-intelligence civilization should be able to:

- distinguish reality from propaganda;
- preserve memory without worshipping the past;
- integrate new evidence;
- constrain rulers, markets, clergy, and majorities;
- protect the weak when protection is costly;
- generate material abundance without making money ultimate;
- reproduce trust and family stability;
- maintain pluralism without dissolving truth;
- form conscience before enforcement;
- correct errors without total collapse;
- transmit meaning without coercive monopoly;
- survive its own technology.

## Master line

> **Every civilization is an information-processing and moral-coordination system attempting to stay aligned with reality while transmitting wisdom across generations under conditions of scarcity, conflict, power, and imperfect knowledge.**
