## 3. Babylonian Talmud chronology

### Transcript claim

The Talmud was compiled during the Babylonian oppression/exile following the ancient Babylonian conquest.

### Evidence

Modern scholarship generally dates the Babylonian Talmud's formation and redaction to late antiquity, especially the fifth and sixth centuries CE in Sasanian Babylonia, not the sixth-century BCE Babylonian exile. Relevant scholarship includes:

- Shai Secunda, *The Iranian Talmud: Reading the Bavli in Its Sasanian Context* (University of Pennsylvania Press, 2014).
- Monika Amsler, *The Babylonian Talmud and Late Antique Book Culture* (Cambridge University Press, 2023).
- Yishai Kiel, *Sexuality in the Babylonian Talmud: Christian and Sasanian Contexts in Late Antiquity* (Cambridge University Press, 2016).

### Verdict

**Contradicted as chronology.**

### Replacement

> The Babylonian Talmud developed in Babylonia, but mainly in the late-antique Sasanian world many centuries after the Babylonian exile. Its historical environment matters; the dates must not be collapsed.

## 7. Jesus's languages and the “20–40%” translation claim

### Transcript claim

Jesus primarily spoke Aramaic and texts moved through Aramaic, Greek, Latin, and English; translation loses 20–40% of original meaning.

### Audit

Scholars broadly agree that Jesus spoke Aramaic. He likely used Hebrew in religious settings and may have possessed some Greek. The canonical Gospels were composed in Koine Greek; they are not simply surviving English translations of an original complete Aramaic Gospel corpus moving through one linear chain.

Translation can lose distinctions or add ambiguity, but no general scientific rule establishes a universal 20–40% loss of meaning between languages.

### Verdict

**Mixed; numerical claim unsupported.**

### Replacement

> Jesus most likely taught primarily in Aramaic, while the canonical Gospels were written in Greek. Translation and cultural distance can change nuance, which is why original-language scholarship matters. There is no defensible universal percentage of meaning lost.

## 13. Albania and Jewish rescue

### Transcript claim

Albanians—Muslim and Christian—protected Jews during World War II; Albania had no Jewish casualties and ended with more Jews than before.

### Evidence

Albania proper is an extraordinary case. Local Muslims and Christians hid Jewish refugees, often connected to the Albanian honor code of *besa*. Albania's Jewish population increased during the war. Yad Vashem recognizes numerous Albanian rescuers.

However, “zero casualties” obscures the fate of Jews in Kosovo and other territories incorporated into wartime Greater Albania. AP reporting notes that nearly half of roughly 500 Jews in Kosovo died after arrests and deportation, even as Albanian rescuers helped many escape.

Relevant sources:

- Yad Vashem, *Besa: A Code of Honor—Muslim Albanians Who Rescued Jews During the Holocaust*.
- U.S. Holocaust Memorial Museum materials.
- AP, “Kosovo inaugurates ‘Wall of Honor’…” (2023).
- Norman Gershman, *Besa: Muslims Who Saved Jews in World War II*.

### Verdict

**Strong achievement; geographic scope must be precise.**

### Replacement

> Albania proper became one of Europe's most extraordinary rescue cases: Muslim and Christian Albanians sheltered Jews, and the Jewish population there was larger at the end of the war. The broader wartime territories, especially Kosovo, suffered real Jewish deportations and deaths and should not be erased.

## 21. Jewish life under Islam

### Transcript claim

Muslims always protected Jews, never expelled them, restored them to Jerusalem, enabled a golden age in Spain, and prevented their extinction.

### Evidence

The comparative macro-history contains strong truths:

- early Islam created a much larger civilizational space in which Jewish communities recovered demographically and institutionally;
- historian David Wasserstein has provocatively argued that “Islam saved Jewry” in this macro-historical sense;
- Jewish intellectual and political life flourished in several Muslim societies;
- Jews often experienced comparatively better conditions under Muslim rule than under medieval Latin Christianity;
- al-Andalus produced major Jewish scholarship, medicine, philosophy, poetry, and translation.

But legal subordination, taxation, social restrictions, violence, persecution, forced conversion, and expulsions also occurred in particular Muslim regimes and periods.

Mark R. Cohen's *Under Crescent and Cross* rejects both the utopian “interfaith paradise” and the counter-myth of uniquely unrelenting Islamic persecution.

### Verdict

**Powerful comparative thesis; universal wording false.**

### Replacement

> Across major periods of the medieval world, Muslim societies often gave Jewish communities more security, autonomy, and intellectual opportunity than contemporary Christian Europe. That history includes real legal inequality and episodes of persecution; neither paradise nor permanent oppression describes the whole record.

## 23. Al-Andalus “invitation,” conquest, and Golden Age

### Replacement

> The Muslim conquest of Iberia succeeded partly through Visigothic fragmentation and local alliances. The societies that followed produced major periods of Muslim, Jewish, and Christian scholarship and became one of several crucial channels through which knowledge reached Latin Europe.
