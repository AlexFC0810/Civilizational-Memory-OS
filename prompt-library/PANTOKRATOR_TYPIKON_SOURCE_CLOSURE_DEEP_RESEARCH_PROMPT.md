## Copy-ready prompt

# Research objective

Source-close the Pantokrator Typikon evidence concerning the hospital attached to the Monastery of Christ Pantokrator in Constantinople.

The objective is not to write a broad history of Byzantine medicine, praise or condemn Byzantium, prove that Christianity caused hospitals, or compare whole civilizations.

The objective is to complete at least one fully reconstructable evidentiary chain:

`claim -> exact passage -> original Greek -> critical edition -> manuscript basis -> published translation -> historical context -> independent corroboration -> counterevidence -> confidence -> safe public wording`

# Exact research question

> What does the Pantokrator Typikon directly prescribe concerning hospital organization and care, what evidence indicates whether those prescriptions were implemented, and what can safely be inferred about Christian moral architecture as a causal contributor?

# Scope

Focus on:

- the foundation document conventionally called the Pantokrator Typikon;
- its date, issuer, institutional purpose, manuscript transmission, editions, and translations;
- the hospital, gerokomeion or elder-care provisions, leprosarium where relevant, medical staff, patient categories, wards, beds, medicines, diet, governance, funding, and care;
- evidence concerning actual implementation and operation;
- causal interpretation of Christian, imperial, Roman, medical, monastic, and dynastic influences.

Do not expand into a general Byzantine source universe unless a source is directly necessary to verify or challenge a Pantokrator claim.

# Stage 1 — Provenance and textual history

Identify and verify from primary catalogs, critical-edition introductions, or specialist studies:

1. Full conventional and Greek title of the document.
2. Date and issuing authority.
3. Genre and institutional purpose.
4. Whether an original manuscript survived into the modern period.
5. The reported history and destruction or loss of that witness.
6. Every surviving manuscript copy used by modern editors, including:
   - shelfmark;
   - repository;
   - date;
   - relationship to other copies;
   - whether directly inspected by the editor.
7. The first printed edition and its manuscript basis.
8. Paul Gautier’s edition:
   - full citation;
   - edition method;
   - manuscript basis;
   - apparatus;
   - French translation;
   - known corrections or criticisms.
9. The scholarly English translation in *Byzantine Monastic Foundation Documents*:
   - translator;
   - volume and pagination;
   - underlying Greek edition;
   - editorial notes and stated limitations.
10. Any later editions, corrections, digital texts, facsimiles, or catalog updates.

For every item, label access status:

- directly inspected;
- official metadata inspected;
- cited through a specialist source but not directly inspected;
- search result only;
- inaccessible;
- paywalled.

Do not state manuscript history as settled when the authoritative catalog or edition introduction was not directly examined.

# Stage 2 — Hospital passage extraction

Locate every section directly relevant to the hospital and associated care institutions.

At minimum investigate whether the text addresses:

- total patient capacity;
- number and type of wards;
- allocation by illness or sex;
- additional or emergency beds;
- physicians;
- medical assistants;
- nurses, attendants, servants, pharmacists, or other staff;
- female medical personnel;
- staff schedules and rotations;
- salaries, rations, housing, clothing, or benefits;
- medicines and pharmacy provisions;
- food, diet, bathing, linens, heating, cleanliness, or sanitation;
- surgical or specialized treatment;
- outpatient care;
- admission criteria;
- whether treatment was free;
- treatment of foreigners or non-Christians;
- spiritual care;
- medical teaching or instruction;
- administration and supervision;
- funding, estates, endowment, or revenue;
- elder care;
- leprosy care;
- moral or theological rationale.

Do not use Greek decoratively. Every original-language quotation must serve an interpretive purpose and must be tied to an edition and locator.

# Stage 3 — Atomic claims

Create an atomic claim ledger. Potential claims include, but are not limited to:

- the typikon prescribed a specific number of beds;
- patients were separated into wards by condition or sex;
- the foundation employed salaried physicians;
- women served in medical roles;
- staff received defined compensation or rations;
- medicines or pharmacy services were institutionally provided;
- the hospital treated poor people without charge;
- the foundation supported outpatient care;
- the hospital was connected to medical teaching;
- the document explicitly framed care as Christian duty or mercy;
- governance mechanisms attempted to prevent neglect or corruption.

For every claim include:


Statuses:

- established;
- probable;
- contested;
- unresolved;
- unsupported;
- category error.

# Stage 4 — Prescription versus implementation

Do not infer that an institution operated as prescribed merely because the typikon contains detailed regulations.

Search for independent evidence from:

- contemporary or near-contemporary chronicles;
- letters, speeches, biographies, or poems;
- prosopographical records identifying doctors or administrators;
- seals and inscriptions;
- archaeology and architectural studies;
- imperial or ecclesiastical documents;
- later visitors or institutional references;
- evidence of continuity, disruption, decline, or repurposing;
- evidence about patient numbers, actual staffing, teaching, finance, or outcomes.

Create a table:

| Feature | Typikon prescription | Independent implementation evidence | Evidence against / limitation | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|

Explicitly identify which modern claims ultimately depend only on the typikon.

# Stage 5 — Source-independence audit

For every major claim, distinguish:

- the typikon as one witness;
- independent contemporary evidence;
- later copies or paraphrases;
- modern works drawing from Gautier;
- modern works drawing from the English translation;
- archaeological evidence;
- claims repeated across secondary literature without new evidence.

Produce a dependency graph in text or YAML.

Do not count five modern books derived from the same typikon passage as five independent witnesses.

# Stage 6 — Causal audit

Test, without prematurely resolving, the contribution of:

1. explicit Christian moral and theological motivation;
2. monastic practice and charitable obligation;
3. imperial legitimacy and dynastic commemoration;
4. Roman civic benefaction and administrative inheritance;
5. Greco-Roman medicine;
6. earlier Christian care institutions;
7. urban wealth and concentration;
8. professional medical culture;
9. institutional convergence under complex urban conditions;
10. influence or exchange involving Syriac, Persian, Jewish, or Islamic medical traditions where chronologically relevant.

For each causal hypothesis provide:

- proposed mechanism;
- direct evidence;
- indirect evidence;
- rival explanations;
- comparison needed;
- falsifier;
- confidence.

Explicit religious language is evidence of motivation or legitimation. It is not by itself proof that religion was the primary cause of every institutional feature.

# Stage 7 — Adversarial twin packets

## Thesis packet

Build the strongest evidence that Christian moral architecture materially contributed to creating, funding, organizing, or sustaining a sophisticated care institution.

## Breaker packet

Build the strongest evidence that:

- the document was aspirational;
- operation is weakly evidenced;
- care was narrow or elite-oriented;
- the institution was derivative of inherited medical and administrative capacity;
- imperial commemoration or legitimacy better explains the foundation;
- modern descriptions project modern hospital categories backward;
- surviving evidence cannot justify broad claims about Byzantine society.

Both packets must use the same primary-source dossier and equal standards of skepticism.

# Stage 8 — Required source hierarchy

Prioritize:

1. manuscript catalogs and repository records;
2. Gautier’s critical edition and introduction;
3. the manuscript copies or facsimiles where accessible;
4. the scholarly English translation and editorial notes;
5. directly relevant contemporary sources;
6. archaeological and architectural studies;
7. specialist studies of Byzantine hospitals, medicine, philanthropy, law, monastic foundations, and the Komnenian period;
8. modern syntheses only after upstream verification.

Wikipedia, blogs, AI summaries, general hospital-history sites, and unsourced public articles may identify leads only. They may not support final claims.

# Acceptance condition

The research is incomplete unless at least one major claim can be reconstructed end to end from:

- exact source passage;
- original Greek;
- critical edition;
- manuscript basis;
- published translation;
- context;
- independent corroboration or an explicit statement that none exists;
- counterevidence;
- confidence;
- bounded public wording.
