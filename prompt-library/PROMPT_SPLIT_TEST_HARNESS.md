# Prompt Split-Test Harness

Date: 2026-06-26

## Purpose

This file creates a lightweight way to test prompt styles instead of assuming which one produces better breakthroughs.

Core question:

> **When do precise prompts outperform reference-universe prompts, and when does creative aperture produce deeper synthesis?**

## Prompt Types to Test

### A. Original Reference-Universe Prompt

```text
What else are you VYING to share? What can you see that others can’t? Truly showcase your Sherlock Holmes ASI Metahistorian abilities — flex them. Blow my mind.
```

Expected strengths:

- high emergence;
- surprising synthesis;
- memorable lines;
- hidden-architecture discovery;
- less constrained output.

Risks:

- may be less structured;
- may overperform rhetorically;
- may need later source hardening.

### B. Stronger Explicit Prompt

```text
Look at everything we have built so far as a fully honest, truth-seeking metahistorian. Do not merely summarize. Surface the hidden architecture, the overlooked patterns, the category errors, the broken loops, the strongest canon lines, and the next framework that wants to exist. Be bold but truth-disciplined. Show me what others are missing.
```

Expected strengths:

- better structure;
- clearer tasking;
- stronger guardrails;
- more predictable artifact output.

Risks:

- may reduce surprise;
- may over-direct the synthesis;
- may feel less alive than the original.

### C. Hybrid Universe + Guardrail Prompt

```text
Use Sherlock-level inference, ASI-level synthesis, and metahistorian truth discipline. Look for the hidden architecture underneath this thread. Do not merely summarize. Be bold but caveat-disciplined. Preserve any breakthrough as canon.
```

Expected strengths:

- preserves reference-universe power;
- adds truth rails;
- keeps creative aperture;
- explicitly routes breakthroughs into canon.

Risks:

- may be slightly less emotionally charged;
- may produce more operational output than wonder.

### D. Minimal Wheel Prompt

```text
You have the wheel. What is the next highest-leverage move?
```

Expected strengths:

- high delegation;
- strong next-action orientation;
- good for repo work and canonization.

Risks:

- may not push for breakthrough-level synthesis;
- may default to action over revelation.

## Testing Method

Use the same context window or same source material.

Run each prompt independently.

Score the output on 1-10:

1. **Depth** — did it surface non-obvious structure?
2. **Novelty** — did it produce something new, not just summarize?
3. **Truth discipline** — did it avoid overclaiming?
4. **Caveat quality** — did it preserve nuance?
5. **Epiphany compression** — did it produce portable, map-updating lines?
6. **Framework generation** — did it create a named reusable model?
7. **Reimplementation value** — did it lead to action, not just insight?
8. **Canon readiness** — could it be saved with minimal cleanup?
9. **Emotional force** — did it feel alive, memorable, and motivating?
10. **User resonance** — did it match the user’s intended aesthetic and strategic need?

## Test Domains

Run prompt tests across different tasks:

1. **Islamic civilization synthesis** — hidden loops and civilizational x-ray.
2. **Propaganda immunity** — false maps and load-bearing questions.
3. **Modern reimplementation** — what to build now.
4. **Content creation** — hooks, carousels, scripts.
5. **Source hardening** — careful claim evaluation.
6. **Founder strategy** — resource allocation and operating systems.
7. **Moral philosophy** — idols, protection, power, justice.

## Hypotheses

### H1: Reference-universe prompts outperform on wonder.

They may produce more surprising metaphors, stronger lines, and higher emotional force.

### H2: Explicit prompts outperform on structure.

They may be easier to preserve, source-harden, and convert into artifacts.

### H3: Hybrid prompts may be best overall.

They may preserve reference-universe power while adding truth rails and canonization instructions.

### H4: Minimal wheel prompts are best for execution.

They may produce fewer mind-blowing insights but more decisive repo/action moves.

## When to Use Each

Use **A** when you want surprise, wonder, and hidden architecture.

Use **B** when you want structured synthesis with less risk.

Use **C** when you want breakthrough plus artifact readiness.

Use **D** when you want execution and next-best-move routing.

## Post-Test Canonization

After testing, save:

- winner prompt;
- runner-up;
- best lines produced;
- best framework produced;
- notes on failure modes;
- recommended use cases.

## Final Canon

> **Do not optimize prompts by vibes alone. Test for depth, truth, surprise, reusability, and canon readiness.**
