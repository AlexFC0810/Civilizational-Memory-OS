# Deep Research Prompt — Byzantine Source Universe

**Use:** First Deep Research activation for the Prophetic Civilizational Science program  
**Mode:** Reconnaissance and provenance only  
**Do not request or accept a final civilizational verdict in this run.**

---

## Copy-ready prompt

# Research objective

Build a source-first research universe for the case study:

**Byzantium: Christian Creed, Roman Power, and the Adherence Gap**

The purpose of this run is **not** to decide whether Byzantium was the most successful empire, whether Orthodoxy is true, whether Byzantium was morally superior, or whether it flourished because it was “aligned with truth.”

The purpose is to identify, classify, and evaluate the primary-source corpora, critical editions, manuscript or artifact witnesses, translations, datasets, archives, and major specialist disputes required to answer those questions responsibly later.

# Core future research question

> Which Byzantine institutions and outcomes are best explained by Christian moral architecture, which by Roman inheritance and material conditions, and where did Byzantine practice betray or expose limits in its Christian blueprint?

Do not answer this question conclusively in this reconnaissance run.

# Scope

Cover the Eastern Roman / Byzantine Empire across its major periods, while explicitly identifying periodization disputes and avoiding treatment of the empire as one static unit.

Minimum periods to distinguish:

- late Roman / early Byzantine Christianization;
- Justinianic era;
- middle Byzantine and Macedonian period;
- Komnenian period;
- Palaiologan period.

Primary geographic focus:

- Constantinople;
- Anatolia;
- Balkans;
- Syria and Egypt while under Byzantine control;
- relevant frontier and successor contexts.

# Required research domains

Map source corpora and scholarship for:

1. imperial identity and Roman continuity;
2. law and administration;
3. taxation and state capacity;
4. economy, trade, and gold coinage;
5. military organization, diplomacy, geography, walls, and logistics;
6. church–state relations and imperial theology;
7. hospitals, hospices, orphanages, monasteries, poor relief, and charitable funding;
8. medical care and institutional medicine;
9. education, libraries, manuscripts, scholarship, and knowledge transmission;
10. slavery, servitude, manumission, and labor;
11. treatment of Jews, pagans, heretics, and rival Christian communities;
12. war practices, prisoners, sieges, conquest, and civilian protection;
13. political blinding, mutilation, exile, execution, dynastic conflict, and succession;
14. gender, family, inheritance, property, and ordinary social life;
15. art, architecture, liturgy, ritual, and moral formation;
16. internal critics, saints, theologians, reform movements, and correction mechanisms;
17. plague, demography, environmental shocks, and external invasion;
18. competing explanations for Byzantine durability and decline.

# Source priority

Prioritize and clearly separate:

## Tier 0 — Artifacts and records

- manuscript images;
- inscriptions;
- coins and numismatic catalogs;
- archaeological records;
- laws, charters, accounts, tax records, institutional documents, and administrative texts.

## Tier 1 — Critical primary-source editions

- critical or diplomatic editions;
- major documentary corpora;
- edition title, editor, publisher, date, volume, and passage system;
- underlying manuscript or artifact basis where available.

## Tier 2 — Primary-source translations

- identify the original edition behind the translation;
- flag translations that are old, partial, ideologically shaped, or lack notes;
- list multiple translations for load-bearing sources when possible.

## Tier 3 — Specialist scholarship

- university-press monographs;
- peer-reviewed articles;
- archaeological and numismatic studies;
- institutional histories;
- specialist scholarship representing competing interpretations.

Public summaries, Wikipedia, blogs, interviews, apologetic works, and AI outputs may be used only to discover upstream sources. Do not treat them as terminal evidence.

# Trusted-site behavior

Prioritize or restrict browsing where practical to:

- major university presses;
- JSTOR, Project MUSE, Persée, Cairn, Brepols, De Gruyter, Brill, Cambridge, Oxford, Dumbarton Oaks;
- official manuscript libraries and digital humanities corpora;
- museum and numismatic databases;
- archaeological project sites;
- Internet Medieval Sourcebook only as a lead to editions;
- institutional repositories containing theses or scholarly editions;
- Google Books, HathiTrust, Internet Archive, Gallica, Europeana, and similar repositories for scans;
- relevant Greek, Latin, Syriac, Arabic, Armenian, Coptic, Georgian, Hebrew, and Slavic corpora.

The report must state when a source was located only through a search snippet or secondary citation and could not be directly inspected.

# Original-language requirements

For every major corpus, identify:

- original language;
- standard critical edition;
- whether a searchable digital text exists;
- whether manuscript images or facsimiles exist;
- major modern translations;
- relevant lexica, grammars, or concordances;
- known textual or translation disputes that could affect historical claims.

Priority languages include:

- Byzantine and Koine Greek;
- Latin;
- Syriac;
- Arabic;
- Armenian;
- Coptic;
- Georgian;
- Church Slavonic where relevant;
- Hebrew for Jewish sources.

Do not imply that an original-language text resolves interpretation automatically. Flag passages requiring specialist philology, paleography, legal history, or manuscript expertise.

# Required source-registry schema

For every high-value source or corpus, provide:

| Field | Required content |
|---|---|
| Source ID | Stable short identifier |
| Work / artifact / corpus | Full title |
| Author / issuer | Person or institution |
| Date composed or issued | Include uncertainty |
| Earliest surviving witness | Date and identity if known |
| Language | Original language |
| Genre | Chronicle, law, sermon, charter, coin, etc. |
| Geographic scope | Where it applies or was produced |
| Critical edition | Full bibliographic details |
| Manuscript/artifact basis | Witnesses or catalog details |
| Translation(s) | Full details and edition dependence |
| Digital access | Stable repository or database |
| Evidentiary value | Which research domain it informs |
| Bias / incentive / limitation | Genre, patronage, audience, survival issues |
| Independence | Whether it is an independent witness or derivative |
| Access status | Inspected, metadata only, paywalled, inaccessible |
| Priority | Essential, important, supplementary |

# Historiography map

For every major research domain, identify:

- dominant interpretation;
- major revisionist or competing interpretations;
- representative specialists;
- the specific evidence driving disagreement;
- what would resolve or narrow the dispute;
- whether popular narratives lag behind current scholarship.

# Mandatory anti-bias controls

1. Do not give Orthodox apologetic sources automatic authority on Byzantine success.
2. Do not give secular or materialist scholarship automatic authority on the causal irrelevance of religion.
3. Do not attribute every charitable institution to Christianity without tracing Roman, Jewish, Persian, monastic, civic-benefaction, and administrative precedents.
4. Do not attribute every atrocity to “Roman corruption” without testing Christian legal, theological, and institutional authorization or resistance.
5. Do not compare Byzantine ideals with another civilization’s worst practices.
6. Do not treat elite art, theology, or manuscripts as proxies for ordinary welfare.
7. Do not treat many citations derived from one source as independent evidence.
8. Do not treat absence from surviving records as proof of nonexistence without preservation analysis.
9. Do not rank Byzantium as “most successful” without defining the objective function.
10. Include sources and arguments that weaken the idea that Orthodoxy caused Byzantine durability.

# Required counterevidence

Identify at least:

- five major bodies of evidence that could support an Orthodox civilizational thesis;
- five major bodies of evidence that weaken or complicate it;
- five cases where material or institutional explanations may dominate;
- five cases where Christian moral teaching plausibly changed institutions or behavior;
- five cases where Byzantine practice sharply departed from Christian norms.

These are research leads, not final verdicts.

# Prohibited outputs

Do not produce:

- a final score for Byzantium;
- a claim that Orthodoxy is true or false;
- a claim that Byzantium was the greatest or most moral empire;
- a generalized “rise and fall” story;
- a public-facing essay;
- unattributed quotations;
- quotations without passage and edition information;
- a bibliography that does not distinguish directly inspected works from cited-but-uninspected works.

# Required final deliverable

Produce a structured reconnaissance report containing:

1. Executive map of the source terrain.
2. Periodization and terminology cautions.
3. Source registry in the required schema.
4. Domain-by-domain primary-source map.
5. Critical editions and translation map.
6. Original-language and philology dependencies.
7. Archive, corpus, manuscript, numismatic, and archaeological access map.
8. Historiography and dispute map.
9. Source-independence and dependency warnings.
10. Missing evidence and inaccessible-source log.
11. Ranked acquisition queue: the first 25 sources or corpora to obtain and inspect.
12. Recommended division into later bounded Deep Research packets.
13. Explicit list of claims that remain unsafe.
14. Machine-readable appendix in Markdown tables or YAML suitable for import into GitHub.
