# The First Permission — Anime Microdrama Pilot

**Status:** Execution-ready experiment  
**Parent architecture:** `canon/CIVILIZATIONAL_STORYWORLD_DISTRIBUTION_ENGINE.md`  
**Strategic objective:** Test whether an original fictional anime-style microdrama can teach atrocity mechanisms across tribal and ideological boundaries, create repeat viewing, route audiences to source-grounded history, and produce creator/institutional distribution.

## Hypothesis

A fictional serialized story that preserves the causal machinery of atrocity while changing names, flags, and identities will:

1. reduce immediate tribal defensiveness;
2. improve emotional comprehension;
3. increase retention and sharing;
4. create stronger identification with refuser/protector roles;
5. improve recognition of the same mechanism in unfamiliar real cases;
6. generate more source engagement than a standalone lecture of equal promotional reach.

## Pilot scope

Do not produce a full season first.

Create three connected vertical episodes:

1. **The Rumor**
2. **The List**
3. **The Neighbor**

Each episode runs 60–90 seconds and ends with an unresolved moral choice.

## Story premise

In the City of Names, citizens take an oath that no person may be reduced to a group identity.

After an attack, a popular leader claims one district hides the enemy. The protagonist is a junior municipal clerk whose job initially appears morally neutral.

### Episode 1 — The Rumor

A crime committed by one masked person is attributed to an entire district. School chants, short clips, and emergency speeches convert fear into collective suspicion.

**Mechanism:** act-based guilt becoming identity-based guilt.

**Cliffhanger:** The protagonist receives a message asking whether the district registry is current.

**Audience question:** Is supplying the list neutral?

### Episode 2 — The List

Officials insist the registry will protect innocent people by separating them from dangerous ones. A colleague signs. The protagonist discovers that families, children, and religious affiliation are included.

**Mechanism:** administrative preparation and responsibility diffusion.

**Cliffhanger:** The protagonist is ordered to export the addresses before midnight.

**Audience question:** At what point did the order become dangerous?

### Episode 3 — The Neighbor

A child from the targeted district arrives at the protagonist's door while transport vehicles enter the neighborhood. The protagonist's family fears collective punishment for hiding the child.

**Mechanism:** personhood versus tribe at the point of consequence.

**Cliffhanger:** The door is struck from outside.

**Audience question:** What would a protector need in order to act?

## Visual grammar

- original anime-inspired aesthetic;
- no imitation of a named copyrighted franchise;
- vertical 9:16 composition;
- consistent character sheets and environment references;
- restrained symbolic violence;
- recurring visual motif: names transforming into colored labels;
- recurring sound motif: crowd chant becoming machine rhythm;
- warm human color palette inside relationships, colder institutional palette around the permission cascade;
- subtitles designed for muted viewing;
- visually distinct evidence/source transition after each story.

## Truth-return sequence

Every episode is paired with:

1. a 30–60 second creator debrief;
2. a source card explaining the mechanism;
3. two real historical examples;
4. one protection counter-example;
5. one prohibited overclaim;
6. one transfer question.

Example for `The List`:

- identity cards and administrative categories in Rwanda;
- municipal lists and separation practices in Bosnia;
- Albanian households and officials who protected rather than exposed people;
- warning not to claim that all registries are inherently genocidal;
- transfer question: what safeguards distinguish legitimate administration from identity-based targeting infrastructure?

## Kill criteria

Do not scale the storyworld if:

- viewers enjoy the aesthetic but fail to recognize the mechanism;
- fiction reduces rather than increases source engagement;
- historical communities consistently experience the work as exploitative;
- character inconsistency destroys immersion;
- production cost prevents a reliable cadence;
- engagement depends mainly on rage or tribal blame;
- the work cannot cross at least two major audience archetypes.

## Scale criteria

Expand to a ten-episode season only if:

- completion and return behavior are strong relative to channel baseline;
- viewers demonstrate mechanism transfer;
- creator partners voluntarily request more;
- source engagement is material;
- one institutional partner wants to pilot it;
- the visual pipeline can repeat consistently;
- ethics review finds no major unresolved harm.

## App decision

No app during the pilot.

Use existing platforms for discovery and a lightweight owned source world for continuity.

An app becomes a later option only when repeated demand exists for:

- branching decisions;
- character interaction;
- learning progression;
- multilingual story libraries;
- discussion circles;
- institutional accounts;
- personalized transfer tests.

## Definition of success

The pilot succeeds when a viewer who has never studied Bosnia, Rwanda, Armenia, or Albania can:

1. identify the first permission;
2. recognize identity-wide guilt;
3. name a possible interruption point;
4. imagine themselves as a refuser or protector;
5. voluntarily seek the historical evidence;
6. recognize the machinery again when the tribe and symbols change.

## Governing line

> **The story earns attention. The decision creates identification. The source return earns trust. The transfer test proves learning.**
