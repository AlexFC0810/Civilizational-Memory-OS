Historical Record — evidence-led reconstruction

Category firewall: institution layer

Classical Islamic law developed the waqf: a founder dedicates income-producing property — farmland, shops, mills, bathhouses — irrevocably once the endowment takes effect (the law schools differed on when irrevocability attaches), so that its revenue funds named public purposes in perpetuity, with the terms recorded in a witnessed deed (waqfiyya) under a judge's oversight; from at latest the ninth century (the oldest surviving dated deed, from 876 CE, endows a Qur'an manuscript) this instrument became a standing funding mechanism for much of the classical Islamic world's civil infrastructure — madrasas, hospitals whose endowments covered staff wages, medicines, beds, and repairs, fountains, bridges, and soup kitchens such as Hürrem Sultan's endowed Jerusalem imaret (charter dated 1556–57), with women documented among founders in named cases and Ottoman registers. The honest limits: charitable foundations predate Islam and the question of influence on waqf's legal form is unresolved; the Qur'an contains no waqf statute (jurists built the institution from hadith reports and general charity teachings); reported capacities are traditional figures rather than audits; family endowments were used to steer wealth around inheritance shares; administration was at times corrupt, and judge-approved property substitutions are reported by specialists as a channel by which ruling elites absorbed endowment assets well before the modern era; economists such as Timur Kuran argue the perpetuity rules bred long-run economic rigidity; and the modern era centralized, captured, or nationalized most of the system.
