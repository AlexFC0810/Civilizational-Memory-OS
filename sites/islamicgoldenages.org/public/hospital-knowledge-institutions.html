Historical Record — evidence-led reconstruction

Category firewall: institution layer

Across roughly the 8th–14th centuries, in cities including Baghdad, Cairo, Damascus, and Cordoba, rulers and elites built durable, endowment-funded institutions for knowledge and healing: a sustained translation movement that corrected and advanced Greek science (while remaining selective by genre and occasionally altered by individual translators), waqf-funded bimaristan hospitals — modeled partly on the earlier Sasanian Gundeshapur hospital — with documented open-admission language and specialized wards (including for mental illness), and named scholars — Ibn al-Haytham in optics, al-Khwarizmi in algebra (whose own astronomy drew on Indian sources) — whose work later fed into European science through named chains of transmission. This tradition also faced real internal opposition, including the destruction of philosophy/science books from Cordoba's royal library by a Muslim successor ruler. None of this means these institutions were first, unique, or flawlessly delivered; it means the record of what was built and transmitted is real and cannot be waved away as "nothing."
