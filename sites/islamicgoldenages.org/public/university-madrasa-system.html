Historical Record — evidence-led reconstruction

Category firewall: institution layer

Between the 9th and 12th centuries, Muslim rulers, viziers, and private endowers scaled endowed higher learning into standing institutions: al-Qarawiyyin in Fez (a ninth-century mosque foundation, dated 857–859 by a late tradition that a rival rediscovered inscription — naming a different founder in 877 — and modern scholarship both contest; a state university since 1963), al-Azhar in Cairo (built 970–972 under the Isma'ili Shia Fatimids, designated for higher learning in 988, later the Sunni world's preeminent seat, with a modern university statute in 1961), and the Nizamiyya network the Seljuk vizier Nizam al-Mulk replicated across Baghdad, Nishapur, Isfahan, and other cities from 1065 — institutions legally constituted as waqf charitable endowments whose income paid professors and gave students room, board, and stipends, with the ijaza, a license issued by an individual teacher, as the credential. This was endowed higher education at scale, inherited from older Near Eastern and Mediterranean precedents and then scaled — not a university in the strict sense: the universitas, a chartered self-regulating corporation of masters and students (Bologna 1088, Paris c. 1150, Oxford 1167), is a distinct Latin-European legal form the madrasa did not have, and whether the waqf-funded college shaped Europe's colleges remains a genuinely open scholarly question.
