# Epistemic Triangulation and Reality Audit

**Status:** Proposed repository protocol  
**Canonical upstream doctrine:** `AlexFC0810/doctrine-hub/doctrine/EPISTEMIC_SOVEREIGNTY_AND_REALITY_TRIANGULATION.md`  
**Local purpose:** Adapt the cross-portfolio doctrine to civilizational history, religion, propaganda immunity, moral comparison, creator intelligence, and public truth transmission.

> **Civilizational Memory OS must not confuse the archive of powerful institutions with the full record of reality. It must also not confuse excluded, defeated, traditional, or dissident voices with automatic truth.**

---

## 1. Repository-level epistemic law

Every major claim must survive triangulation across the relevant evidence worlds before it earns canon.

The repository does not begin with:

- Western academic consensus;
- anti-Western reaction;
- state archives;
- religious apologetics;
- colonial archives;
- modern moral fashion;
- inherited communal memory;
- AI synthesis;
- or founder intuition.

It begins with an exact claim, a causal terrain map, a plural source map, explicit authority layers, competing explanations, confidence, and breaker evidence.

**Reality is the terminal judge. GitHub preserves the evolving case.**

---

## 2. Claim decomposition before source collection

Separate claims by type:

- **Event claim:** Did this occur?
- **Chronology claim:** When and in what sequence?
- **Text claim:** What does the source actually say?
- **Transmission claim:** How did the report reach us?
- **Attribution claim:** Which authority layer owns the statement or action?
- **Causal claim:** Why did the event, institution, or pattern emerge?
- **Prevalence claim:** How common was it?
- **Comparative claim:** Better or worse than what contemporaneous benchmark?
- **Moral claim:** What value or violation is being asserted?
- **Institutional claim:** What rules and incentives governed practice?
- **Outcome claim:** What did the system repeatedly produce?
- **Memory claim:** What story did a community preserve, and why?
- **Propaganda claim:** Who benefits from this framing, omission, or compression?
- **Prescriptive claim:** What should be built or done now?

No source can answer every claim type merely because it is prestigious or primary.

## 3. The civilizational evidence portfolio

For major investigations, seek the relevant combination of:

1. **Foundational texts** — scripture, constitutions, charters, legal codes, treaties.
2. **Transmitted corpora** — hadith, chronicles, oral traditions, collected reports.
3. **Contemporary records** — letters, court records, tax ledgers, waqf deeds, inscriptions, diplomatic reports.
4. **Material evidence** — archaeology, architecture, coins, manuscripts, environmental and demographic evidence.
5. **Institutional evidence** — hospitals, schools, courts, guilds, welfare systems, animal institutions, markets, military orders.
6. **Practice evidence** — what communities and states actually did over time.
7. **Outcome evidence** — literacy, health, mobility, minority survival, violence, prosperity, knowledge production, ecological or animal outcomes.
8. **Internal critique** — dissenters, reformers, jurists, theologians, philosophers, poets, minorities, women, workers, enslaved people, and conquered peoples.
9. **External observers** — rivals, travelers, diplomats, colonizers, merchants, missionaries, refugees, and neighboring civilizations.
10. **Communal memory** — stories, rituals, reputations, warnings, and moral exemplars preserved across generations.
11. **Modern scholarship** — source criticism, historiography, archaeology, social science, quantitative reconstruction.
12. **Counter-scholarship** — revisionist, decolonial, sectarian, nationalist, anti-religious, apologetic, and rival-civilizational interpretations.
13. **Living practitioner knowledge** — archivists, scholars, religious authorities, craftspeople, community elders, and institutional descendants.
14. **AI synthesis** — useful for mapping and comparison, never terminal evidence.

The portfolio should reveal convergence, disagreement, silence, destruction, and survival bias.

## 4. Power and archive audit

Archives are produced by power and damaged by power.

For each source ecosystem, ask:

- Who had the literacy, money, authority, and physical security to create records?
- Which records were preserved, copied, translated, classified, looted, censored, or destroyed?
- Which languages became academically legible and which remained inaccessible?
- Which institutions funded the dominant historiography?
- Which categories were imposed by colonial, nationalist, sectarian, or modern bureaucratic systems?
- Which actors had incentives to exaggerate, sanitize, demonize, or universalize?
- Which populations appear mainly through the records of enemies or rulers?
- What absence is evidence, and what absence is merely archival loss?

Power analysis does not invalidate an archive. It defines its blind spots and the weight its claims can carry.

---

## 5. Authority-layer discipline

Never substitute the wrong defendant.

Use the repository authority stack:

`foundational text → transmitted corpus → commentary → jurisprudence → institution/state → movement → community practice → individual`

For every “Islam says,” “Christianity did,” “the West believes,” or equivalent claim:

- identify the exact layer;
- identify whether the layer is representative, contested, exceptional, or later;
- distinguish rule from violation;
- distinguish ideal from median practice;
- distinguish origin from later identity;
- distinguish theological doctrine from state interest;
- distinguish the founder, scripture, school, empire, and modern movement.

No layer is immune from criticism. No layer may be silently collapsed into another.

## 6. Geographic and intellectual diversity rule

The source map must not default to Anglo-American or contemporary Western academic mediation when primary, regional, or rival traditions exist.

For contested civilizational claims, seek where relevant:

- original-language sources;
- scholarship from the civilization under study;
- neighboring and rival civilizational sources;
- Global South scholarship;
- European and North American scholarship;
- sectarian and school-diverse interpretations;
- archaeological and material evidence;
- traditional scholarly lineages;
- modern critical methods;
- sources produced before and after colonial disruption.

Diversity is used to reduce correlated blindness, not to manufacture artificial balance.

A source earns weight through directness, competence, independence, explanatory power, and outcome fit—not geography alone.

---

## 7. Consensus, canon, and received history

Treat academic consensus as a high-value map of what a field currently coordinates around. Do not treat it as metaphysical closure.

Audit:

- the source base shared by the field;
- assumptions inherited across scholars;
- inaccessible languages or archives;
- disciplinary incentives;
- funding and political sensitivity;
- whether dissent can be published and rewarded;
- major historical reversals in the field;
- whether the consensus concerns facts, interpretation, or moral judgment.

Similarly, treat inherited communal canon and traditional memory as high-value evidence of moral identity, transmission, and long-run selection—but not automatic event-level proof.

The system preserves both:

- **event historicity**, and
- **civilizational memory significance**.

They may be different claims.

---

## 8. Fruit and outcome evidence

Civilizational claims cannot be evaluated only through texts or elite intentions.

Track what systems repeatedly produced:

- treatment of the weak;
- treatment of religious, ethnic, and political minorities;
- women’s property, education, safety, and agency;
- slavery, servitude, war, taxation, and legal inequality;
- knowledge creation and preservation;
- public health and welfare;
- animal treatment;
- ecological stewardship;
- corruption control;
- social mobility;
- institutional durability;
- capacity for self-correction;
- capacity to restrain rulers and violent actors;
- capacity to survive contact with power.

The fruit test does not replace causal analysis. Outcomes may reflect geography, technology, inherited capital, war, demography, or external extraction. The causal model must separate the operating system from the terrain.

---

## 9. Testimony, oral history, and lived memory

Testimony is evidence. Grade it rather than excluding it.

High-value testimony includes:

- identifiable witness position;
- temporal proximity;
- independent corroboration;
- stable retelling before political incentives changed;
- details against the witness’s interest;
- material or documentary alignment;
- clear distinction between witnessed event, hearsay, interpretation, and inherited memory.

Low-value testimony includes:

- repeated dependence on one origin;
- retrospective identity construction;
- political scripting;
- impossible chronology;
- dramatic detail that grows over time;
- absence of expected corroboration where records should exist;
- selective survival of only morally useful stories.

Oral traditions may preserve real structures, migrations, traumas, moral judgments, and relationships even when exact dates or event details remain unresolved.

---

## 10. Strongest-rival requirement

Every major synthesis must identify the strongest rival model—not a weak opposing quote.

The rival model must receive:

- its strongest sources;
- its strongest causal explanation;
- its strongest moral case;
- the evidence that most threatens the current thesis;
- the conditions under which it would become the preferred model.

The goal is not neutral mush. After composition under evidence, the system may issue an opinionated verdict.

A verdict becomes stronger when the rival survives inside the record rather than being deleted.

Use the shared claim states:

- **Established**
- **Strongly supported**
- **Probable**
- **Plausible**
- **Promising frontier**
- **Contested**
- **Unsupported**

For historical claims, also state:

- geographic scope;
- chronological scope;
- authority layer;
- representativeness;
- source survival limits;
- whether the claim concerns norm, institution, median practice, exceptional case, or memory.

## 12. Mandatory Reality Audit packet

Every consequential canonization packet should contain:

### A. Exact claim set

Separate event, causal, prevalence, comparative, moral, institutional, and prescriptive claims.

### B. Authority and actor map

Who said, transmitted, interpreted, enforced, violated, preserved, or weaponized the claim?

### C. Causal terrain

Incentives, institutions, material constraints, theology, law, technology, ecology, trauma, power, and path dependency.

### D. Evidence portfolio

Primary, material, institutional, practice, outcome, testimony, traditional, academic, and rival evidence.

### E. Power/archive map

What was preserved, destroyed, translated, funded, excluded, or made illegible?

### F. Comparative benchmark

Equivalent unit, contemporaneous baseline, median versus exceptional case, and modern second-layer comparison.

### G. Strongest rival

Best opposing model and breaker evidence.

### H. Verdict

Claim state, scope, confidence, assumptions, and unresolved terrain.

### I. Public boundary

What can be said forcefully? What must remain internal? What phrasing would overclaim?

### J. Revision trigger

Which new source, result, or contradiction forces reconsideration?

## 13. Truth-to-force firewall

The Truth & Peace Engine may compress, dramatize, sequence, visualize, and adapt.

It may not:

- convert a plausible claim into an established fact;
- erase scope or chronology;
- universalize an exceptional case;
- attribute an institution’s act directly to scripture without authority mapping;
- suppress the strongest counterevidence;
- present AI augmentation as a human source;
- use an emotionally effective claim that failed the Reality Audit.

Maximum justified force remains the standard.

---

## 14. Failure-conversion loop

When an operator, critic, new source, or deployment result exposes a failure:

1. identify the epistemic failure class;
2. preserve valid work;
3. reopen the source and causal maps;
4. update the claim state and confidence;
5. record the correction and supersession;
6. update the protocol, template, prompt, or gate;
7. propagate the correction downstream;
8. create an eval that detects recurrence.

Failure classes include:

- authority bias;
- Western-source monoculture;
- anti-Western romanticism;
- sectarian default;
- presentism;
- source survival blindness;
- institutional-intent confusion;
- norm/practice collapse;
- exceptional-case universalization;
- testimony dismissal;
- testimony credulity;
- mechanism neglect;
- outcome neglect;
- incentive neglect;
- public-force overreach;
- sterile caution that hides meaningful truth.
