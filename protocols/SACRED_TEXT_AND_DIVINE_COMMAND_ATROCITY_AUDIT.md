# Sacred Text and Divine-Command Atrocity Audit

**Status:** Canonical protocol  
**Purpose:** Evaluate claims that a sacred text, prophet, deity, church, jurist, ruler, or religious tradition authorizes collective violence, extermination, forced removal, or inherited guilt—without apologetic evasion, anti-religious flattening, or allowing perpetrators to define a tradition by quoting it selectively.

## Founding principle

Claims of divine authorization for mass violence are among the highest-risk claims human beings can make.

They combine:

- ultimate authority;
- moral certainty;
- identity fusion;
- promised reward;
- immunity from ordinary criticism;
- permission to override empathy;
- the possibility that a human interpreter has captured God's voice.

Therefore:

> **The higher the claimed authority, the higher the burden of authentication, context, coherence, and moral scrutiny.**

A ruler cannot make an atrocity sacred merely by attaching God's name to it.

A critic cannot prove a living tradition is genocidal merely by isolating one difficult passage without auditing canon, text history, genre, interpretation, application, and the tradition's full moral architecture.

## The Catastrophic Divine-Command Rule

When a person claims that God commands killing, expulsion, enslavement, forced conversion, or destruction of a civilian population:

1. the claim receives no presumption of obedience;
2. innocence and individual guilt remain the default;
3. collective punishment is presumed illegitimate;
4. the claimant bears the strongest available burden of proof;
5. every textual, historical, interpretive, and institutional layer must be exposed;
6. refusal remains morally protected while the claim is unresolved;
7. no contemporary actor may reactivate an ancient identity category against living people;
8. a claimed command may not silently override clearer prohibitions on murder, injustice, excess, or inherited guilt.

> **No alleged voice of God should receive less scrutiny when it commands catastrophe than a human ruler would.**

## The audit sequence

### 1. State the exact operational claim

Do not audit vague statements such as:

- “The Bible is violent.”
- “Islam commands war.”
- “God gave us the land.”
- “Those people are cursed.”

Rewrite the claim atomically:

- Which population?
- Which action?
- Which time period?
- Which conditions?
- Which source?
- Is the claim descriptive, normative, historical, theological, or operational today?

### 2. Identify the authority layer

Separate:

- foundational scripture;
- textual variant;
- later canon;
- commentary;
- hadith or tradition;
- jurisprudence;
- church doctrine;
- ruler's decree;
- ethnic custom;
- nationalist mythology;
- wartime propaganda;
- modern extremist reinterpretation;
- individual violation.

The most common manipulation is **authority laundering**: moving a claim from a lower layer to a higher layer without proof.

### 3. Establish the text

Ask:

- What are the earliest manuscripts?
- Which textual witnesses exist?
- What variants matter?
- What is the relationship between original language and translations?
- Which words are disputed?
- Is the quotation complete?
- Is the passage present across all canons?
- Has a translation imported a stronger meaning than the source permits?

### 4. Establish the canon

Distinguish:

- translation differences;
- manuscript variants;
- book-order differences;
- chapter/verse numbering;
- canonical inclusion or exclusion;
- liturgical versus doctrinal authority.

“Many versions” is not by itself proof of deliberate corruption.

A canon is a historically formed collection. Its formation, exclusions, and institutions must be studied directly.

### 5. Date composition, compilation, and redaction

Ask:

- When is the event portrayed as occurring?
- When was the account first composed?
- When did it reach its current form?
- Which political, exilic, imperial, priestly, sectarian, or community pressures surrounded composition?
- Does the text preserve older layers inside later framing?
- Who benefited from the final narrative?

Historical-critical explanation is not automatically disproof of revelation, but it can expose human mediation and political use.

### 6. Determine genre and rhetorical world

Is the passage:

- law;
- narrative;
- poetry;
- apocalypse;
- liturgy;
- prophetic condemnation;
- ancient war rhetoric;
- royal ideology;
- origin story;
- symbolic vision;
- retrospective theology?

Ancient victory accounts may use totalizing language even when later verses or archaeology imply survival. That may affect historical interpretation, but “hyperbole” must not become an automatic escape from morally difficult wording.

### 7. Test historical implementation

Ask:

- Did the described event occur as written?
- What does archaeology show?
- Do external records corroborate it?
- Did later communities treat the command as executable law, one-time narrative, allegory, moral warning, or obsolete history?
- Has the passage been reactivated against living populations?

A fictionalized or exaggerated conquest story can still be morally dangerous if later readers operationalize it.

### 8. Reconstruct the strongest internal interpretation

Before criticizing, identify how serious believers understand the text.

Possible interpretations include:

- unique and non-repeatable command;
- judicial punishment;
- ancient military hyperbole;
- allegory against sin;
- progressive revelation;
- covenant-specific law;
- human misunderstanding recorded in scripture;
- descriptive history rather than command;
- morally difficult divine command accepted as mystery.

Do not attack a caricature. Do not accept a harmonization merely because it is available.

### 9. Audit internal moral coherence

Compare the difficult passage with the tradition's strongest anchors concerning:

- sanctity of life;
- individual guilt;
- enemy civilians;
- justice against one's own side;
- mercy;
- limits in war;
- treatment of prisoners;
- noncombatants;
- children;
- coerced belief;
- repentance;
- truthfulness;
- ruler accountability.

Classify the relationship:

- coherent;
- bounded exception;
- unresolved tension;
- later interpretive override;
- contradiction;
- possible human interpolation or redaction;
- category mismatch.

### 10. Apply the Collective-Guilt Test

Ask:

- Is punishment assigned to individuals for acts they committed?
- Are children or descendants treated as guilty by identity?
- Does membership itself make innocence impossible?
- Does the target have a route to peace, exit, trial, or protection?
- Does the text preserve noncombatant status?

Any identity-based denial of possible innocence is an atrocity-risk marker.

### 11. Apply the Capture Test

Ask:

- Who is invoking the passage?
- What power, land, property, legitimacy, or revenge do they gain?
- Is the interpretation challengeable?
- Are alternative scholars censored?
- Has nation replaced God as the true sacred object?
- Is the ancient enemy being mapped onto living people?
- Does the interpretation excuse the speaker's side from universal rules?

### 12. Apply the Prophet–Ruler Separation Test

Do not assume that every act of a later empire, monarchy, church, caliphate, party, or modern state represents the founding prophet or revelation.

Also do not assume that founders are exempt from scrutiny merely because later followers corrupted the system.

Map:

`revelation → founder → earliest community → compiler/canonizer → interpreter → ruler → institution → modern activist`

### 13. Apply the Moral Breaker Test

Ask what evidence would change the verdict.

Examples:

- an earlier manuscript;
- evidence of redaction;
- archaeological contradiction;
- a different lexical meaning;
- proof the command was never historically practiced;
- proof it was repeatedly operationalized;
- a consistent internal prohibition;
- a tradition-wide consensus limiting the passage;
- modern reactivation by influential institutions.

### 14. Produce a layered verdict

Possible outputs:

- direct foundational authorization;
- textually present but historically bounded;
- difficult text with serious interpretive dispute;
- later tradition rather than foundation;
- ruler or nationalist appropriation;
- historically unverified narrative;
- likely literary hyperbole;
- morally incompatible with clearer governing anchors;
- unresolved;
- falsely attributed;
- operationally dangerous modern reuse.

No single verdict should silently answer every layer.

## Comparative textual architecture

### Qur'an

Historically, current manuscript evidence strongly supports an early standardized Uthmanic consonantal text and unusually high continuity with the Qur'an used today. The oral-recital tradition and early manuscripts create a dense transmission system.

The honest historical boundary is:

- early stabilization does not mean zero variants;
- canonical readings (*qira'at*) exist;
- early orthography lacked later dots and vowel marks;
- the Sana'a palimpsest's lower text preserves a rare non-Uthmanic textual witness;
- “divinely preserved and never edited by man” is a theological claim, while manuscript history can establish early standardization and continuity, not divine causation.

Preservation does not remove interpretation risk. A stable text can still be selectively quoted, mistranslated, or subordinated to later authority.

### Hebrew Bible / Old Testament

The Hebrew Bible is a library formed over centuries rather than one book delivered at one moment.

Its textual world includes:

- the Masoretic tradition;
- Septuagint witnesses;
- Samaritan Pentateuch;
- Dead Sea Scroll manuscripts;
- later translations;
- differing Jewish, Catholic, Protestant, Orthodox, and Ethiopian canons.

These differences must be specified rather than compressed into “thousands of Bibles.” Many modern “versions” are translations of substantially overlapping canons; canonical and textual differences are real but structured.

The existence of multiple witnesses can reveal textual development and also allow comparison and correction. It does not by itself prove a conspiracy or invalidate every moral teaching.

### New Testament and Christianity

Christian scripture includes a stable 27-book New Testament canon across major modern traditions, while Old Testament canons vary. Interpretation also depends on claims about:

- Jesus and Torah;
- covenant;
- fulfillment;
- church authority;
- apostolic teaching;
- progressive revelation;
- how Christians read difficult Israelite war texts through Jesus.

Christian critics and defenders must separate the teachings attributed to Jesus from ancient Israelite narrative, later church doctrine, empire, and modern nationalism.

## The Qur'anic atrocity firewall

A Qur'an-sovereign audit should place difficult claims against a governing cluster including:

- 5:32 — the moral enormity of unjustly taking or saving life, explicitly framed as an ordinance for the Children of Israel;
- 6:164 and parallels — no bearer bears another's burden;
- 17:33 — do not take the life God has made sacred except with right, and do not exceed limits even in retaliation;
- 2:190 — fight those who fight you and do not transgress;
- 4:135 — uphold justice even against yourselves and close relations;
- 60:8 — kindness and justice toward those who do not fight or expel;
- 2:256 — no compulsion in religion.

A claimant attempting to authorize identity-wide killing must explain how the claim survives this whole architecture rather than merely producing one fragment.

## The difficult Old Testament question

Texts associated with *herem* or “the ban,” especially in Deuteronomy, Joshua, and 1 Samuel, can depict or command the destruction of whole populations, including noncombatants.

Serious audit must ask:

- what the earliest textual forms were;
- when Deuteronomistic material was composed and redacted;
- whether conquest accounts function as royal or identity-forming literature;
- what archaeology indicates about a sweeping conquest;
- whether totalizing language reflects ancient war rhetoric;
- how Jewish and Christian traditions limited, allegorized, historicized, or defended the passages;
- whether the passages have been used to legitimate colonialism, ethnic cleansing, or modern territorial claims;
- whether internal biblical themes of individual responsibility, prophetic justice, mercy, and protection create unresolved tension or interpretive control.

This must be investigated without converting ancient Canaanite narratives into hostility toward living Jews or Christians.

## Prohibited shortcuts

Do not say:

- “The Bible has versions, therefore it is fake.”
- “The Qur'an is preserved, therefore every Muslim interpretation is correct.”
- “A perpetrator quoted scripture, therefore the religion caused the atrocity.”
- “The foundational text prohibits murder, therefore religious institutions were innocent.”
- “Archaeology questions the event, therefore the morally dangerous narrative does not matter.”
- “It was hyperbole,” without proving genre and reception.
- “God commanded it,” as though the human claim authenticates itself.
- “Ancient people had different morals,” as though historical context resolves present moral judgment.

## Public-output standard

Every public treatment should distinguish at minimum:

1. **What the text says.**
2. **How securely we possess that text.**
3. **When and why it reached that form.**
4. **What serious believers think it means.**
5. **What people historically did with it.**
6. **What moral problem remains.**
7. **What prevents modern reactivation.**

## Canonical close

> **A sacred text should not be protected from scrutiny by calling doubt impious, and a living people should not be condemned by treating their most difficult text as their permanent essence.**

> **The question is not only whether a violent sentence exists. It is who wrote it, how it traveled, what authority it holds, how it fits the whole moral architecture, who benefits from its interpretation, and whether it can still make an innocent person guilty by identity.**
