# Animal Mercy Hadith and Institutions Ledger

**Status:** living evidence ledger  
**Parent canon:** `canon/ANIMAL_MERCY_CONSTELLATION.md`  
**Rule:** distinguish report existence, traditional grading, historical event confidence, legal reception, and institutional practice.

## Confidence dimensions

Each entry should eventually receive separate grades for:

1. **Corpus certainty** — is the wording securely present in the cited collection?
2. **Traditional authenticity** — how did hadith critics grade it?
3. **Historical-event confidence** — how confidently can modern historical method infer the event occurred?
4. **Reception importance** — how influential was it in Muslim moral/legal discourse?
5. **Implementation evidence** — did it become law, enforcement, or institution?

A report can be high in corpus certainty and reception while remaining uncertain as direct seventh-century history.

## Tier A — high corpus certainty / major reception

### AMH-001 — The thirsty dog and the forgiven woman

- **Corpus:** Sahih al-Bukhari 3321; Sahih Muslim 2245a.
- **Core content:** a prostitute gives water to a dog dying of thirst and is forgiven.
- **Moral architecture:** compassion is not limited by the rescuer's status or the animal's low ritual/social status; one act of mercy can carry immense spiritual weight.
- **Deployment line:** “A socially condemned woman is remembered through mercy to a thirsty dog.”
- **Do not claim:** this proves the incident occurred exactly as narrated by modern historical standards; this resolves all Islamic tensions concerning dogs.
- **Research:** map commentary, sermon use, legal derivations, gender/class significance, and dog-related counter-traditions.

### AMH-002 — Reward in serving every living being

- **Corpus:** Sahih al-Bukhari 2363.
- **Core content:** after a man waters a thirsty dog, the Prophet is asked whether reward exists for serving animals; the answer extends reward to every living being.
- **Moral architecture:** the dog story is generalized beyond one favored species.
- **Research:** translation of `kull kabid ratbah`; juristic scope; dangerous animals; wild animals; conflicting permissions to kill.

### AMH-003 — The woman who confined and starved a cat

- **Corpus:** Sahih al-Bukhari 3318; parallel material in Sahih Muslim.
- **Core content:** a woman is punished for confining a cat while neither feeding it nor releasing it to seek food.
- **Moral architecture:** ownership and control generate affirmative duties; omission can be cruelty; release is required when care is not provided.
- **Potential legal principle:** `care for the dependent or relinquish control`.
- **Research:** fiqh on compelled feeding, sale, release, owner liability, and court enforcement.

### AMH-004 — Ihsan in slaughter

- **Corpus:** Sahih Muslim 1955a.
- **Core content:** goodness/excellence is enjoined in all things; when slaughter is permitted, the blade should be sharpened and the animal spared avoidable suffering.
- **Moral architecture:** necessity or permission does not cancel welfare duties.
- **Counterevidence required:** modern veterinary debate over stunning, restraint, handling, industrial line speed, and whether classical rules achieve their intended welfare under modern conditions.

### AMH-005 — Living beings must not be used as targets

- **Corpus:** Sahih Muslim 1957a.
- **Core content:** prohibition on making a living being a target.
- **Moral architecture:** entertainment, training, or spectacle does not by itself justify inflicted suffering.
- **Research:** legal reception concerning hunting, military training, blood sports, and pest control.

## Tier B — important transmitted reports requiring deeper source audit

### AMH-006 — The weeping camel

- **Corpus:** Sunan Abi Dawud 2549.
- **Core content:** a camel weeps; the Prophet calms it and rebukes its owner because it is kept hungry and worked heavily.
- **Moral architecture:** hunger and overwork are morally legible complaints; ownership is stewardship; an animal can function narratively as a complainant.
- **Caution:** source page and traditional grading notes require specialist review; do not present as equal in historical confidence to multiply attested Bukhari/Muslim reports without a chain-and-parallel audit.
- **Research:** all variants, earliest common link, fiqh reception, artistic/literary afterlife, and whether jurists treated visible exhaustion as actionable evidence.

### AMH-007 — The grieving mother bird and returned chicks

- **Corpus:** Sunan Abi Dawud 2675; graded sahih by al-Albani on the cited edition.
- **Core content:** companions take a bird's chicks; the mother displays distress; the Prophet orders them returned. The same report condemns burning an ant colony.
- **Moral architecture:** parental attachment and distress matter; small animals are not outside moral attention.
- **Research:** chain history, species identification, legal reception, nest-taking, egg collection, habitat disturbance, and dangerous-insect exceptions.

## Institutional-claim ledger

### AMI-001 — Hisba protection of working and market animals

- **Claim:** market inspectors could regulate loading, treatment, slaughter, and animal-related commerce.
- **Status:** plausible and likely sourceable, but not yet closed.
- **Needed:** named hisba manuals, exact clauses, jurisdiction, enforcement evidence, and comparison with actual court records.

### AMI-002 — Waqf feeding stations for cats, dogs, birds, and other animals

- **Claim:** charitable endowments funded food and water for animals.
- **Status:** repeatedly asserted; evidence quality varies.
- **Needed:** original waqf deeds or reliable editions, founder, date, location, revenue asset, named beneficiaries, duration, and administrative records.

### AMI-003 — Gurabahane-i Laklakan / injured-stork hospice in Bursa

- **Claim:** an Ottoman institution cared for injured storks and possibly other animals.
- **Status:** strong cultural memory and modern documentation; original institutional history and funding mechanism require archival verification.
- **Needed:** earliest attestation, physical site history, waqf deed if any, operating dates, species served, staffing, medical function, and whether “first animal hospital” is defensible.
- **Unsafe wording:** “The Ottomans created the world's first free animal hospital.”

### AMI-004 — Free animal hospitals

- **Claim:** Islamic or Ottoman cities operated hospitals that treated animals without charge.
- **Status:** unresolved as a general civilizational claim.
- **Needed:** distinguish veterinary schools, military remount hospitals, market clinics, private charitable care, bird hospices, and modern retrospective labels.

### AMI-005 — Sanctuaries for retired working animals

- **Claim:** endowments maintained horses, camels, donkeys, or other animals after they ceased to be economically useful.
- **Status:** high-value but not source-closed.
- **Needed:** deeds, account books, beneficiary rules, admission criteria, lifetime-care evidence, scale, longevity, and evidence that animals were not merely reassigned, sold, or slaughtered.
- **Priority:** highest. This directly tests the Post-Use Obligation.

### AMI-006 — Urban bird architecture and feeding culture

- **Claim:** Ottoman buildings included birdhouses and urban practices supporting birds.
- **Status:** physical evidence is likely strong for architecture; welfare function, prevalence, and causal religious claims require calibration.
- **Needed:** architectural surveys, dates, geographic prevalence, intended use, maintenance evidence, and comparison with European dovecotes/nesting structures.

### AMI-007 — Veterinary institutions

- **Claim:** Islamic societies developed veterinary knowledge and later formal veterinary schools/hospitals.
- **Status:** broad claim is defensible; free-public-welfare framing is not automatic.
- **Needed:** distinguish military/economic animal medicine from intrinsic-welfare institutions; map Abbasid, Mamluk, Ottoman, Persian, Indian, and modern developments.

## Comparative cells

### AMC-BYZ-001 — Byzantine working animals at Yenikapi

- **Provisional evidence:** archaeological reporting describes young working horses with severe skeletal and mouth damage; animals no longer able to work were slaughtered and processed. The same site also includes evidence of a dog's broken foot having healed after treatment.
- **Use:** demonstrates why comparative verdicts must be dimension-level rather than civilizational caricatures.
- **Needed:** underlying osteoarchaeological publications, sample dates, representativeness, and comparison with Ottoman-period remains.

### AMC-EUR-001 — Modern European statutory welfare

- **Provisional evidence:** nineteenth-century anti-cruelty legislation, later veterinary regulation, slaughter conventions, and modern charities create stronger formal enforcement than most premodern systems.
- **Counterevidence:** retired horses may still be sold, slaughtered, abandoned, or rehomed according to economic value; formal welfare law is not a universal retirement guarantee.

## Canonical close

> Hadith can show what a civilization taught itself to notice. Law can show what it believed owners owed. Archives and bones show what actually happened.
