# Islamic Animal Ethics — Primary Source Ledger Seed

**Status:** Research ledger seed; not yet source-complete  
**Date:** 2026-07-17  
**Purpose:** Prevent future agents from collapsing Qur’an, hadith, fiqh, historical practice, modern welfare science, and public advocacy into one layer.

---

## 1. Research rule

> **No public claim becomes load-bearing until its source layer, wording, authenticity/status, interpretation, counterevidence, and safe public formulation are visible.**

This ledger is intentionally conservative.

It preserves promising claims while marking what still requires verification.

## 2. Source hierarchy

### Layer A — Qur’an

Highest Islamic revelatory authority.

Track:

- Arabic text;
- major translation differences;
- immediate context;
- classical tafsīr;
- modern interpretation;
- limits on public inference.

### Layer B — Hadith

Track:

- collection;
- hadith number across editions;
- isnād/authenticity grading;
- variant wording;
- legal and ethical interpretation;
- whether report is mutawātir, mashhūr, āḥād, or otherwise classified where relevant.

### Layer C — Fiqh and legal tradition

Track:

- school;
- jurist;
- period;
- legal category;
- disagreement;
- enforcement history;
- distinction between minimum law and recommended excellence.

### Layer D — Historical implementation

Track:

- documented practices;
- courts;
- market inspection;
- waqf/endowment;
- veterinary literature;
- animal hospitals or shelters where evidenced;
- violations and breaker cases.

### Layer E — Modern welfare science

Track:

- species;
- method;
- outcome measured;
- study quality;
- uncertainty;
- religious compatibility questions.

### Layer F — Public advocacy

Track:

- safe wording;
- prohibited overclaim;
- audience;
- citation requirement;
- emotional risk;
- political/cultural sensitivity.

## 3. Qur’anic claim cards

### Q-AE-001 — Animals as communities

**Primary reference:** Qur’an 6:38  
**Working claim:** Terrestrial animals and birds are described as communities like human communities.  
**Safe public wording:** “The Qur’an describes animals and birds as communities like us.”  
**Interpretive caution:** Do not claim the verse establishes modern legal personhood or identical human/animal status.  
**Research needed:** Classical tafsīr survey; semantic range of *umam amthālukum*; modern animal-ethics scholarship.  
**Confidence:** High on text; medium on extended philosophical inference.

### Q-AE-002 — Creation glorifies God

**Primary references:** Qur’an 24:41; 17:44  
**Working claim:** Birds and creation possess modes of glorification humans may not fully understand.  
**Safe public wording:** “The Qur’an places nonhuman creation inside a God-oriented order rather than treating it as spiritually empty matter.”  
**Caution:** Avoid claims about specific animal consciousness not established by the verses.  
**Confidence:** High on text; medium on philosophical extension.

### Q-AE-003 — Sacrifice and taqwā

**Primary reference:** Qur’an 22:37  
**Working claim:** Flesh and blood do not reach God; taqwā does.  
**Safe public wording:** “The Qur’an explicitly rejects the idea that God needs blood; the moral center is reverence and God-consciousness.”  
**Caution:** Do not imply sacrifice has no ritual or social function beyond inward intention.  
**Research needed:** Tafsīr on context and sacrificial distribution.  
**Confidence:** High.

### Q-AE-004 — Solomon and the ant

**Primary references:** Qur’an 27:18–19  
**Working claim:** Prophetic power becomes aware of small creatures it could crush unknowingly and responds with gratitude and moral consciousness.  
**Safe public wording:** “The Qur’an gives a model of power noticing the vulnerable life it might harm without intending to.”  
**Caution:** This is a moral inference from narrative, not a standalone legal rule.  
**Confidence:** High on narrative; medium on systems application.

### Q-AE-005 — The she-camel of Thamūd

**Primary references:** Qur’an 7:73; 11:64; 26:155–159; 54:27–31; 91:11–15  
**Working claim:** A protected animal and her access to water become part of a covenantal test; organized violation reveals rebellion and collective complicity.  
**Safe public wording:** “The Qur’an contains an animal-centered civilizational warning in which cruelty toward a protected she-camel exposes a community’s arrogant refusal of limits.”  
**Caution:** Do not flatten the miraculous sign into a generic modern animal-rights case.  
**Confidence:** High on text; medium on generalized application.

### Q-AE-006 — Corruption, crops, and living stock/progeny

**Primary reference:** Qur’an 2:205  
**Working claim:** Destructive corruption includes ruining cultivation and living stock/progeny.  
**Safe public wording:** “The Qur’an places destruction of the biological foundations of life within the language of corruption.”  
**Caution:** Translation of *al-ḥarth wa al-nasl* requires careful treatment; do not claim the verse is exclusively about animals.  
**Confidence:** High on verse; medium on exact English formulation.

### Q-AE-007 — Justice beyond tribe and hatred

**Primary references:** Qur’an 4:135; 5:8  
**Working claim:** Justice remains binding against self-interest, kin interest, and enemy hatred.  
**Animal-ethics relevance:** Establishes the broader architecture in which power and group interest do not define justice.  
**Safe public wording:** “Islamic justice is designed to remain binding when injustice would benefit your own side.”  
**Confidence:** High.

### Q-AE-008 — Atom’s weight accountability

**Primary references:** Qur’an 99:7–8  
**Working claim:** Even the smallest good and evil remain visible in final accounting.  
**Animal-ethics relevance:** Small acts toward vulnerable life are not morally trivial.  
**Safe public wording:** “The Qur’an refuses the idea that small hidden acts disappear from moral reality.”  
**Confidence:** High.

### Q-AE-009 — Hidden knowledge and recording

**Primary references:** Qur’an 50:16–18; 57:4; 67:12  
**Working claim:** Hidden conduct and inward whispering remain known to God.  
**Safe public wording:** “Human law only reaches what it discovers; Islamic accountability includes the unseen.”  
**Caution:** Do not use this to endorse human surveillance states.  
**Confidence:** High.

### Q-AE-010 — First murder and self-justification

**Primary references:** Qur’an 5:27–32  
**Working claim:** The aggressor’s self makes murder acceptable to him; fear of God is articulated as a restraint.  
**Safe public wording:** “The Qur’an recognizes that grave wrongdoing often begins when the self manufactures permission.”  
**Animal-ethics relevance:** Applies to cruelty rationalization, though the narrative concerns human murder.  
**Confidence:** High on text; medium on extended application.

## 4. Hadith claim cards

### H-AE-001 — Woman punished for starving a cat

**Collections:** Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī; Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim  
**Working claim:** A woman is punished for confining a cat, failing to feed it, and failing to release it to seek food.  
**Moral principle:** Control creates responsibility; neglect of a powerless animal is spiritually serious.  
**Safe public wording:** “The Prophet ﷺ taught that starving a confined cat could condemn a soul.”  
**Caution:** Verify exact numbering and variant wording before publication. Avoid sensational embellishment.  
**Confidence:** High authenticity; source details to be normalized.

### H-AE-002 — Water for a thirsty dog

**Collections:** Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī; Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim variants  
**Working claim:** A person receives divine forgiveness for giving water to a desperately thirsty dog. Some narrations identify the person as a woman of sinful reputation.  
**Moral principle:** Mercy toward an animal can carry immense spiritual weight; status does not monopolize goodness.  
**Safe public wording:** “A person was forgiven for giving water to a dying dog.”  
**Caution:** When specifying identity/status, cite the exact narration.  
**Confidence:** High authenticity; variant-specific details require care.

### H-AE-003 — Reward in every living being

**Collections:** Associated with dog/water narrations in Bukhārī and Muslim  
**Working claim:** Reward exists for kindness toward every living creature / moist liver, depending on translation.  
**Safe public wording:** “The Prophetic teaching generalizes reward for mercy toward living creatures.”  
**Caution:** Verify Arabic phrase, translation, and juristic scope.  
**Confidence:** High authenticity; wording review required.

### H-AE-004 — Distressed camel and owner accountability

**Collection:** Sunan Abī Dāwūd and related reports  
**Working claim:** The Prophet encounters a distressed camel and rebukes its owner regarding hunger and overwork, invoking fear of God.  
**Moral principle:** Working animals are not machines; ownership is amānah.  
**Safe public wording:** “The Prophet ﷺ confronted an owner over an animal that had been kept hungry and overburdened.”  
**Caution:** Verify grading, exact wording, and parallel narrations.  
**Confidence:** Medium-high pending hadith-critical review.

### H-AE-005 — Mother bird and young returned

**Collection:** Sunan Abī Dāwūd and related reports  
**Working claim:** Companions take a bird’s young; the mother displays distress; the Prophet orders the young returned.  
**Moral principle:** Animal attachment and distress are morally relevant.  
**Safe public wording:** “The Prophet ﷺ ordered a mother bird’s young returned when her distress became visible.”  
**Caution:** Verify species wording and grading.  
**Confidence:** Medium-high pending review.

### H-AE-006 — Burning ant colony / creatures with fire

**Collection:** Sunan Abī Dāwūd and related reports  
**Working claim:** Burning living creatures is condemned; punishment by fire belongs to the Lord of fire in common wording.  
**Safe public wording:** “Prophetic teaching condemned burning living creatures as punishment.”  
**Caution:** Verify exact context, authenticity, and legal exceptions discussed by jurists.  
**Confidence:** Medium-high pending review.

### H-AE-007 — Iḥsān in slaughter

**Collection:** Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim  
**Working claim:** God has prescribed iḥsān in all things; when killing or slaughtering, perform it well, sharpen the blade, and spare the animal unnecessary suffering.  
**Safe public wording:** “Even where slaughter is lawful, the Prophet ﷺ made excellence and minimized suffering obligatory moral concerns.”  
**Caution:** Do not translate this into the empirical claim that every current halal practice is painless.  
**Confidence:** High authenticity.

### H-AE-008 — Do not sharpen blade before animal / do not slaughter before another animal

**Collections/status:** Often cited in animal-ethics literature; exact source chain and grading must be separately verified.  
**Working claim:** Preparing the blade away from the animal and avoiding visible slaughter before another animal are part of humane guidance.  
**Safe public wording:** Use only after source verification.  
**Prohibited wording before verification:** “The Prophet explicitly said X” without exact citation.  
**Confidence:** Pending.

### H-AE-009 — Living creatures as targets

**Collections:** Ṣaḥīḥ collections and sunan contain prohibitions on using living animals as targets and cursing those who do so.  
**Moral principle:** Entertainment does not justify inflicted suffering.  
**Safe public wording:** “Prophetic teaching condemned using living creatures as targets.”  
**Caution:** Normalize exact narrations and grading.  
**Confidence:** High general claim; source specifics pending.

### H-AE-010 — Mutilation and branding on the face

**Collections:** Multiple reports  
**Working claim:** Mutilation and certain painful treatment, especially striking or branding the face, are condemned.  
**Safe public wording:** Pending source-specific review.  
**Confidence:** Medium-high.

### H-AE-011 — Horned and hornless sheep

**Collection:** Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim  
**Working claim:** Rights are restored on the Day of Resurrection even between the horned and hornless sheep.  
**Moral principle:** Asymmetrical harm among animals is not erased from final justice.  
**Safe public wording:** “The Prophetic tradition imagines justice restored even between animals.”  
**Caution:** Avoid asserting a fully elaborated modern animal-rights theory from one report.  
**Confidence:** High authenticity.

## 6. Historical implementation research agenda

Questions:

- Were owners compelled by courts to feed or sell animals?
- What roles did the muḥtasib and market inspection play?
- What veterinary manuals circulated?
- Were there documented animal endowments or feeding stations?
- How were working animals regulated in cities?
- What evidence exists from Ottoman, Mamluk, Andalusian, Abbasid, Mughal, Safavid, West African, and Southeast Asian contexts?
- What violations were common?
- How did warfare and famine alter practice?
- How did Muslim societies compare with contemporaries using the same metrics?

Truth rule:

> Do not cherry-pick exceptional benevolent anecdotes as proof of universal practice.

## 7. Modern slaughter and welfare science ledger

### Research questions

- Time to loss of consciousness by species and method.
- Pain associated with neck incision.
- Effectiveness and failure rates of stunning methods.
- Reversible stunning compatibility with halal standards.
- Restraint stress.
- Handling and transport.
- Blade sharpness and operator skill.
- False aneurysm and delayed unconsciousness in cattle.
- Confirmation of death/unconsciousness.
- Worker conditions and error rates.

### Public truth rule

Avoid:

- “Halal is always painless.”
- “Stunning is always humane.”
- “Non-stun slaughter is always torture.”

Prefer:

> “Islamic law demands minimization of suffering. Whether a method fulfills that demand must be tested empirically by species, technique, equipment, and execution.”

## 9. Public claim safety matrix

### Safe now with standard citation

- Qur’an calls animals communities.
- Qur’an says flesh and blood do not reach God; taqwā does.
- The cat and dog reports are in highly authoritative hadith collections.
- Iḥsān is commanded in slaughter.
- Islamic teaching places ownership under responsibility.

### Safe only with nuance

- Islamic civilization developed significant animal-welfare ethics.
- Islamic law contains restraints on animal use.
- Animal mercy is integrated into Islamic accountability.
- Islam’s architecture is unusually comprehensive relative to many historical baselines.

### Not safe without research

- Islam was the first religion to protect animals.
- Muslims historically treated animals better than every other civilization.
- Europe had no animal protection until a specific late date.
- Every halal slaughter causes instant unconsciousness.
- Islamic law universally prohibited every modern factory-farming harm.
- A particular viral case proves a national or religious moral deficiency.

## 10. Publication checklist

Before publishing a claim:

- [ ] Exact Qur’anic verse or hadith source confirmed.
- [ ] Translation checked.
- [ ] Hadith grading/status confirmed.
- [ ] Classical interpretation sampled.
- [ ] Modern scholarly disagreement recorded.
- [ ] Historical implementation separated from doctrine.
- [ ] Counterexample/breaker evidence reviewed.
- [ ] Empirical welfare claim independently sourced.
- [ ] Public wording does not exceed evidence.
- [ ] Native-language theological review completed.
- [ ] Graphic or emotional material is not being used to bypass uncertainty.
