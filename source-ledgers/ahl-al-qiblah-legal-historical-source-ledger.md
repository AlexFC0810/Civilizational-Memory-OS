## Research Question

Can *Ahl al-Qiblah* / “the People of the Qiblah” serve as a historically legitimate and cross-school foundation for a modern civilizational commons without concealing real differences in how the category has been used?

## Provisional Verdict

**The phrase has deep historical legitimacy as an umbrella for people located inside the Muslim confessional world, but it has not always meant theological equality, full mutual recognition, or identical communal status.**

Across different traditions, *Ahl al-Qiblah* could function as:

- a broad synonym for Muslims;
- an anti-takfir boundary;
- a heresiographical category covering disputing Muslim groups;
- a category distinguishing internal Muslim error from external unbelief;
- or a graded status assigned to Muslims outside one's own saved or fully orthodox community.

Therefore:

> **The name is historically strong, but it is not self-executing. The modern Commons requires an explicit protected covenant defining the rights, dignity, and non-domination that the inherited phrase alone does not guarantee.**

This is the first major finding of the source-universe phase.

## Confidence Scale

- **Established:** directly visible in a primary text or authoritative institutional source.
- **Strong:** supported by specialist scholarship or converging evidence.
- **Provisional:** plausible and useful for mapping, but primary-text verification remains incomplete.

## 1. Early Heresiographical Use

### Abu al-Qasim al-Balkhi (d. 319/931)

#### Evidence

Al-Balkhi authored a work titled *Maqalat Firaq Ahl al-Qiblah* (“Doctrines of the Sects of the People of the Qiblah”). Available bibliographic summaries date the work's composition to the late ninth and early tenth centuries and report later influence among Mu'tazili, Zaydi, and Ibadi intellectual networks.

#### Status

**Strong bibliographic fact; full-text interpretation pending.**

#### Significance

The title itself is evidence that a major early theologian could place multiple disputing Islamic groups inside a shared “People of the Qiblah” analytical universe.

#### Caution

Inclusion in a heresiographical universe does not prove equal orthodoxy, equal salvation, or equal legal status.

#### Source trail

- Work: *Maqalat Firaq Ahl al-Qiblah*.
- Full modern edition reportedly published in 2018; bibliographic verification and acquisition required.

### Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari (d. 324/935)

#### Evidence

Al-Ash'ari's *Maqalat al-Islamiyyin wa-Ikhtilaf al-Musallin* classifies the doctrines of Islamic groups and “those who pray.” Modern descriptions emphasize that the work surveys disputing groups as internal Islamic actors rather than beginning with their expulsion from Islam.

#### Status

**Strong; exact category boundaries require primary-text reading.**

#### Significance

The title and structure support the broader historical pattern: early Muslim heresiography could catalogue profound disagreement inside a shared confessional field.

#### Primary-text lead

- Hellmut Ritter edition available through MENAdoc; DOI references are listed in bibliographic records.

#### Caution

Later Sunni heresiographers often created sharper boundaries. Al-Ash'ari's classificatory breadth should not be projected onto every later Sunni theologian.

## 2. Sunni Creedal and Anti-Takfir Use

### Abu Ja'far al-Tahawi (d. 321/933)

#### Evidence lead

Al-'Aqidah al-Tahawiyyah includes a section conventionally translated as declaring the People of the Qiblah Muslims and rejecting takfir for sin alone, so long as a person does not declare lawful what is prohibited or reject the foundational confession.

The often-circulated Arabic line is:

> `ولا نكفر أحدا من أهل القبلة بذنب ما لم يستحله`

A responsible English rendering and manuscript-based text must be verified from a reliable edition before deployment.

#### Status

**Strong primary-text lead; edition and translation verification pending.**

#### Significance

This may be one of the strongest classical Sunni anchors for using *Ahl al-Qiblah* as an anti-takfir category.

#### Caution

- The exact boundaries of “People of the Qiblah” were debated.
- Later commentaries differ.
- The statement does not eliminate takfir for every belief or claim.
- It cannot be honestly converted into “all self-identified Muslim groups were always treated as equally orthodox.”

### Broader Sunni research queue

Primary-text work is required on:

- Abu Hanifa and early Hanafi creed;
- al-Maturidi;
- al-Ash'ari and early Ash'aris;
- al-Baqillani;
- al-Ghazali's *Faysal al-Tafriqah*;
- al-Nasafi and Maturidi creeds;
- Hanbali/Athari statements on *Ahl al-Qiblah*;
- al-Nawawi and legal treatment of innovators;
- Ibn Taymiyya's distinctions among error, innovation, and unbelief;
- and later Ottoman/Sunni legal usage.

#### Working hypothesis

Sunni traditions contain substantial anti-takfir resources, but the category's perimeter changes depending on which doctrines are treated as necessary foundations of Islam.

## 3. Ibadi Use — Critical Counterexample to Simplistic Unity

### Evidence lead

Specialist summaries of early Ibadism report that Basran Ibadis called themselves *Jama'at al-Muslimin* (“the community of Muslims”) while assigning other Muslims the status *Ahl al-Qiblah*. They could regard non-Ibadi Muslim lands as *dar al-tawhid* rather than territory of unbelief while maintaining doctrines of loyalty and dissociation.

#### Status

**Provisional-to-strong secondary lead; primary Ibadi verification required.**

#### Source leads

- Josef van Ess, *Theologie und Gesellschaft*.
- John C. Wilkinson, *Ibadism: Origins and Early Development in Oman*.
- Early Ibadi texts and the *Mudawwana* tradition.
- Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari's sections on the Ibadis.

#### Significance

This is a load-bearing warning:

> **Being called “People of the Qiblah” could mark inclusion inside the wider monotheist Muslim world while still placing the other community below one's own fully normative community.**

The category could restrain total excommunication without producing full reciprocity.

#### Institutional implication

A modern Commons cannot rely on the inherited label alone. It must explicitly define:

- equal human dignity;
- security of life, property, and worship;
- non-domination;
- freedom from forced conversion;
- strong-form representation;
- and reciprocal institutional standing.

The official Amman Message recognizes eight Sunni, Shia, and Ibadi legal schools, prohibits takfir among adherents covered by its definition, and defines qualifications for fatwa.

## 8. Semantic Map

The phrase *Ahl al-Qiblah* should not be treated as having one timeless technical definition.

### Possible historical functions

1. **Ritual marker:** people who face the Kaaba in prayer.
2. **Confessional umbrella:** Muslims broadly.
3. **Heresiographical universe:** disputing groups discussed as internal to Islam.
4. **Anti-takfir restraint:** sinners or innovators are not automatically expelled from Islam.
5. **Graded inclusion:** outsiders to the author's own community remain inside a wider Muslim field but may not receive identical theological status.
6. **Modern ecumenical charter:** a basis for mutual recognition and anti-sectarian cooperation.

### Research rule

Every use must be interpreted by:

- author;
- century;
- school;
- genre;
- legal or theological question;
- included and excluded groups;
- and practical consequences.

## 9. Strategic Name Test

### Evidence in favor

- historically deep rather than recently invented;
- tied to a shared embodied practice rather than a modern ideology;
- capacious enough to exceed the Sunni-Shia binary;
- already active in major contemporary institutional language;
- compatible with protected doctrinal difference;
- and connected to anti-takfir traditions.

### Evidence against or requiring safeguards

- historically variable boundaries;
- sometimes compatible with graded or asymmetrical inclusion;
- may be unfamiliar to ordinary English-speaking Muslims;
- can be appropriated by state unity campaigns;
- may include groups differently depending on the speaker;
- and could sound like a lowest-common-denominator identity if not paired with a strong truth architecture.

### Provisional verdict

Retain the name.

But always pair it with the constitutional definition:

> **The People of the Qiblah Civilizational Commons protects legitimate difference, equal human dignity, reciprocal security, strong-form representation, and non-domination while keeping truth claims open to evidence.**

## 10. Falsifiers

Reconsider the public name if source-hardening shows that:

- major traditions overwhelmingly understand the phrase as exclusionary or insulting;
- it cannot be translated without substantial distortion;
- current state actors have captured it so completely that institutional independence becomes illegible;
- or a broader historically grounded term can perform the mission with materially less ambiguity.

The mission is canonical. The public label remains evidence-responsive.
