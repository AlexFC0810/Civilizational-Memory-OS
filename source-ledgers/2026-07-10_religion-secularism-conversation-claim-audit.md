# Religion–Secularism Conversation Claim Audit

**Date:** 2026-07-10  
**Status:** Verification backlog  
**Purpose:** Capture factual claims raised in the conversation before they harden into public arguments.

---

## Confidence key

- **High:** broadly established, but still cite before publication.
- **Moderate:** plausible and partly supported; wording matters.
- **Contested:** serious disagreement or source-layer problem.
- **Unsupported as stated:** too sweeping, loaded, or not established.

---

## 1. Religion and social outcomes

### Claim

Highly religious countries have worse outcomes across violence, corruption, rape, poverty, literacy, health, and democracy.

**Status:** Moderate as raw correlation; unsupported as simple causation.

**Needed:**

- identify datasets and years;
- reproduce the correlations;
- control for GDP, education, region, conflict, colonial history, sanctions, inequality, state capacity, age structure, and reporting quality;
- separate country-level from individual-level relationships;
- separate religiosity types.

### Claim

America is more violent than peer countries because it is more religious.

**Status:** Unsupported as stated.

**Needed:** Compare religiosity against inequality, firearms, welfare, urban segregation, policing, healthcare, education, drug markets, and institutional trust.

## 2. Violence decline

### Claim

Humanity is dramatically less violent than 200 or 500 years ago.

**Status:** Moderate/high depending metric and period.

**Caution:** Homicide, war deaths, state violence, colonial violence, and reporting must be separated. Long-run decline literature is debated in interpretation even when some datasets show strong declines.

### Claim

Gene-culture coevolution and human self-domestication explain declining aggression.

**Status:** Contested research program, not settled causal fact.

**Needed:** Distinguish biological self-domestication over deep time from recent institutional violence decline. Avoid claiming culture “killed off alpha males” as established fact.

### Claim

Skull feminization proves culture selected against aggressive males.

**Status:** Contested and oversimplified.

**Needed:** Review human self-domestication literature, craniofacial changes, testosterone hypotheses, neural crest critiques, and alternative explanations.

## 3. China

### Claim

Communism succeeded in China.

**Status:** Definition-dependent.

**Needed:** Distinguish Maoist command economy, post-1978 market reforms, state capitalism, socialism with Chinese characteristics, industrial policy, party control, and poverty reduction.

### Claim

China reduces violence through a surveillance state.

**Status:** Moderate.

**Needed:** Separate ordinary crime rates, social control, policing, censorship, facial recognition, Xinjiang, reporting quality, gun control, urbanization, and economic development.

### Claim

Ancient China abolished slavery twice.

**Status:** Needs precise cases and duration.

**Needed:** Wang Mang reforms and any earlier/later abolition attempts; test whether bans were comprehensive, enforced, and durable.

---

## 4. Japan

### Claim

Japan’s low violence comes from shame culture, social cohesion, gun control, policing, and high trust.

**Status:** Moderate, likely multi-causal.

**Needed:** Homicide, organized crime, reporting, clearance rates, family/community norms, economic security, demographics, and criminal justice.

---

## 5. Scandinavia

### Claim

Modern Nordic trust is inherited from one thousand years of Christianity.

**Status:** Plausible contribution; unsupported as sole cause.

**Needed:** Compare pre-Christian assemblies, Lutheran literacy, state formation, peasant institutions, labor movements, welfare, equality, corruption control, and historical trust data.

### Claim

Peasants and workers killed corrupt clergy in anti-clerical revolutions that weakened religion across Scandinavia in the 1600s–1800s.

**Status:** Too sweeping / likely inaccurate as a general Nordic account.

**Needed:** Country-specific histories; distinguish Nordic reform and labor conflict from French, Spanish, Mexican, Italian, or other continental anti-clerical violence.

### Claim

1848 and the end of World War II were the two major shifts creating modern Nordic society.

**Status:** Oversimplified.

**Needed:** Country timelines and earlier/later reforms.

---

## 6. Spain and al-Andalus

### Claim

Spain was Muslim for almost 800 years.

**Status:** Directionally true only with geographic qualification.

**Caution:** Muslim rule existed in parts of Iberia from 711 to 1492; not all of present-day Spain was continuously under Muslim rule for the entire period.

### Claim

Modern Spanish trust or development is inherited from Islamic civilization.

**Status:** Plausible contribution; unsupported as single cause.

**Needed:** Roman, Visigothic, Jewish, Islamic, Christian, imperial, regional, modern democratic, and European institutional layers.

---

## 7. Islamic Golden Ages

### Claim

Islamic civilization was the intellectual, technological, and scientific capital of the world.

**Status:** Strong for specific periods and regions, but wording must be periodized.

**Needed:** Define dates, centers, fields, and comparative peers. Prefer “a leading global center” or specific stronger claims when singular dominance can be demonstrated.

### Claim

The Islamic model worked for a millennium.

**Status:** Too broad without specifying system and outcomes.

**Needed:** Separate prophetic polity, caliphates, dynasties, regions, institutions, Golden Ages, decline, colonial intervention, and modern states.

## 8. Waqf, zakat, and public goods

### Claim

Waqf was a decentralized public-goods engine funding hospitals, schools, water, roads, libraries, and welfare.

**Status:** High in broad form.

**Needed:** Regional and period examples, governance strengths, rigidity problems, gender/property dynamics, and colonial/state transformations.

### Claim

Islamic anti-usury architecture prevents debt slavery and extreme inequality.

**Status:** Normatively plausible; empirically requires testing.

**Needed:** Distinguish Qur'anic riba, classical finance, market practice, debt instruments, redistribution, and modern Islamic finance.

## 10. Indus Valley Civilization

### Claim

The Indus Valley Civilization had no slavery.

**Status:** Unsupported as certainty.

**Reason:** Archaeological inference is limited and the script remains undeciphered.

Use only as:

> Some scholars argue the archaeological record does not show the same obvious hierarchy or slavery evidence found in certain peer civilizations, but absence is not established.

## 11. Christianity and abolition

### Claim

Christianity produced abolition.

**Status:** Too broad.

**Better:** Christian abolitionists, especially Quakers and evangelicals, were important within a multi-causal abolition process also driven by enslaved resistance, secular radicals, political struggle, war, and economic change.

### Claim

The Bible directly advocates institutional abolition.

**Status:** Contested/weak as a direct-text claim.

**Needed:** Separate regulation, manumission, equality principles, Pauline texts, later abolitionist interpretation, and pro-slavery interpretation.

---

## 12. Early Christianity and communism

### Claim

Ancient Christians lived in communist societies.

**Status:** Loaded analogy.

**Better:** Some early Christian communities practiced voluntary sharing or communal property, but this is not identical to modern state communism, Marxism, or abolition of all private property.

## 13. Jesus and Reza Aslan’s *Zealot*

### Claim

Jesus was primarily a political revolutionary seeking regime change against Rome.

**Status:** Contested scholarly interpretation.

**Needed:** Compare Aslan with mainstream historical-Jesus scholarship. Crucifixion supports perceived political threat but does not settle the total meaning of Jesus’s mission.

### Claim

Jesus is an allegory based on a real person.

**Status:** Unsupported and conceptually unclear.

**Better:** The historical existence of Jesus is accepted by the overwhelming majority of relevant scholars; interpretations of Gospel theology and legendary development vary.

---

## 14. John 10:35 and scripture

### Claim

Jesus saying “scripture cannot be broken” proves endorsement of every later Old Testament canon, interpretation, and translation.

**Status:** Unsupported as stated.

**Needed:** Literary context, the scripture available to first-century Jews, canon development, textual traditions, and theological interpretation.

---

## 15. Mary’s age and miraculous conception

### Claim

Mary was explicitly 13–15 or 14 when she conceived Jesus.

**Status:** Unsupported as explicit textual fact.

**Better:** Her age is not stated in canonical scripture; teenage estimates are historical inference from marriage customs.

### Claim

The virgin conception makes God a pedophile.

**Status:** Category error as stated.

**Reason:** Pedophilia refers to sexual attraction to prepubescent children; the doctrine asserts miraculous conception, not divine sexual intercourse or desire.

A serious critique would ask about agency, consent, and divine action rather than use a sexual diagnostic label unsupported by the narrative.

---

## 16. Aisha’s age

### Claim

Muhammad married Aisha at nine.

**Status:** Traditional hadith claim; historically disputed; not Qur'anic.

### Claim

Joshua Little conclusively debunked the age-nine report.

**Status:** Overstated.

**Better:** Little presents a serious historical-critical argument that the report is late/unreliable; this challenges certainty but does not create universal scholarly consensus.

### Claim

Alternative chronology proves she was 17–19.

**Status:** Contested reconstruction.

Do not state any exact alternative age as settled without a full source audit.

---

## 17. Qur'an preservation

### Claim

The Qur'an cannot be tampered with because it is memorized.

**Status:** Overstated mechanism.

**Better:** Combined oral and written transmission, mass recitation, manuscript evidence, and early standardization support remarkable preservation; oral transmission alone is not infallible.

### Claim

The Qur'an is unchanged with zero variation.

**Status:** Needs careful qualification.

**Better:** The consonantal text is exceptionally stable, while canonical readings, orthography, and manuscript variants require honest discussion.

---

## 18. Muhammad and “warlord”

### Claim

Muhammad was objectively a warlord.

**Status:** Definition-dependent and rhetorically loaded in modern English.

**Better:** Muhammad was a prophet, community founder, statesman, lawgiver, diplomat, and military leader. Whether “warlord” is useful depends on a defined neutral criterion rather than modern connotation.

### Claim

All of Muhammad’s wars were purely defensive.

**Status:** Requires case-by-case historical analysis.

Needed:

- persecution and exile;
- raids and caravan conflict;
- Badr, Uhud, Trench;
- treaties;
- Khaybar;
- conquest of Mecca;
- Tabuk;
- source reliability and later framing.

---

## 19. Democracy

### Claim

Democracy did not exist in Muhammad’s time.

**Status:** False if universal; direct democracy existed in ancient Athens and deliberative/assembly traditions existed elsewhere.

**Better:** Modern mass constitutional democracy did not exist in seventh-century Arabia.

### Claim

American democracy died under Reagan and Clinton.

**Status:** Normative/political thesis, not a clean fact.

**Needed:** Define democracy and test lobbying, campaign finance, union decline, inequality, voter access, media concentration, and policy responsiveness.

### Claim

Money is incompatible with democracy.

**Status:** Strong rhetorical thesis requiring mechanism specification.

**Better:** Concentrated wealth and unlimited political spending can convert formal democracy into oligarchic influence or auction politics.

---

## 20. Atheism and morality

### Claim

Atheist culture has empathy, anti-censorship, and anti-war sentiment as core tenets.

**Status:** Too broad; atheism is a metaphysical position, not one culture or complete ethic.

**Better:** Many secular-humanist and atheist movements emphasize empathy, free inquiry, anti-clericalism, and opposition to oppression; other atheist regimes and individuals have not.

### Claim

You need God to be moral.

**Status:** Reject as a claim about individual behavior.

Stronger question:

> Can a civilization ground and reproduce costly moral obligation across generations without transcendence or another binding sacred framework?

---

## 21. Scientific miracles

### Claim

Scientific miracles in the Qur'an prove revelation.

**Status:** High-risk category requiring claim-by-claim audit.

Guardrails:

- use earliest plausible meaning, not modern retrofitting;
- verify Arabic semantics;
- check pre-Islamic knowledge;
- avoid vague metaphor presented as precise science;
- distinguish compatibility, suggestiveness, and prediction;
- include strongest skeptical analysis.

## 22. Publication protocol

Before any claim becomes public-facing:

1. Record exact wording.
2. Identify source layer.
3. Find primary sources.
4. Find strongest supporting scholarship.
5. Find strongest critical scholarship.
6. Mark confidence.
7. Add historical context.
8. Identify mechanism.
9. Add breaker examples.
10. Compress only after the full audit.

## 24. Core rule

> **No claim becomes load-bearing because it feels directionally right. It becomes load-bearing when its source layer, causal mechanism, confidence level, and strongest counterargument are visible.**
