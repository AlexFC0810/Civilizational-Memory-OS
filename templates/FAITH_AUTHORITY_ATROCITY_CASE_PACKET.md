## 7. Scripture, doctrine, and propaganda separation

For the relevant tradition(s), distinguish:

- foundational text;
- authoritative doctrine;
- disputed interpretation;
- inherited custom;
- ruler-made law;
- nationalist myth;
- propaganda;
- conduct directly violating the tradition.

Do not allow:

- perpetrators to define a religion simply by using its language;
- defenders to erase institutional complicity by quoting an ignored prohibition.

## 8. Literacy and information environment

- basic literacy levels;
- access to foundational texts;
- translations available;
- censorship;
- educational curriculum;
- clerical or state monopoly;
- independent press;
- rumor networks;
- radio/print/social media role;
- public ability to verify claims;
- punishment for questioning.

## 9. Refusers, rescuers, and protectors

For every case include actors from the perpetrator-associated population who refused.

For each profile:

- role and identity;
- what they noticed;
- stated motivation;
- relationship to victims;
- religious or moral beliefs;
- practical action;
- risk and cost;
- support network;
- institutional position;
- whether refusal spread;
- evidence quality.

## 10. Bystander and beneficiary map

Identify:

- silent institutions;
- foreign governments;
- media;
- commercial beneficiaries;
- property recipients;
- transport/logistics actors;
- professionals;
- diaspora actors;
- international organizations;
- actors who knew but delayed.

Do not collapse different levels of knowledge, coercion, or responsibility.

## 11. No-Camera Test

For participants at each load-bearing point ask:

> What earthly or transcendent accountability remained when law, authority, crowd, and material interest favored participation?

Assess:

- fear of God or final judgment;
- universal dignity;
- professional oath;
- legal risk;
- empathy/relationship;
- memory of prior atrocity;
- community support for refusal;
- absence of restraint.

## 13. Intervention ladder

At each stage answer:

- What remained feasible?
- Who had leverage?
- What evidence existed?
- Why was action not taken?
- What would intervention have cost?
- What did delay make impossible?

Stages:

1. identity hardening;
2. legal discrimination;
3. dehumanizing propaganda;
4. organization and arming;
5. dispossession and registration;
6. confinement/expulsion;
7. mass killing;
8. denial and impunity.

## 14. Comparative lessons

- What is specific to this case?
- Which mechanisms recur elsewhere?
- Which comparison would be misleading?
- What new prevention hypothesis emerges?
- What breaker evidence could change the conclusion?

## 15. Public-force layer

Produce:

- one transferable question;

## 16. Internal-community mirror

For communities associated with victims:

- What must be remembered?
- How can memory avoid revenge and inherited guilt?

For communities associated with perpetrators:

- What must be confessed?
- How can responsibility remain individual and institutional rather than inherited collectively?

For the relevant religious community:

- What did the tradition require?
- Where did institutions fail?
- Who preserved the standard?
- What reforms follow now?

## 17. Completion gate

- [ ] No identity-sized collective blame appears.
- [ ] Explanation is not used as excuse.

- [ ] Transfer and intervention lessons are explicit.
- [ ] Survivor testimony use is ethical and consent-aware.
- [ ] Red team completed.

## Canonical close

> **A case packet is complete only when it preserves the people, explains the machinery, identifies the points of refusal, and leaves the reader better able to recognize and interrupt the next case.**
