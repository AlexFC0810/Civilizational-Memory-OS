# RP-MC-001 — Scandinavian Inherited Moral Capital Falsification Test

- **Research theater:** Moral capital and secularization
- **Parent issue:** #15
- **Status:** queued
- **Priority:** P0

## Exact question

To what extent can high trust, low violence, welfare solidarity, and prosocial behavior in modern Scandinavian societies be explained by inherited Christian/Lutheran moral capital rather than pre-Christian institutions, geography, state formation, literacy, labor movements, equality, welfare policy, ethnic history, or other material and institutional causes?

## Why this matters

“Inherited moral capital” is plausible but can become an unfalsifiable story. This packet is designed primarily to break, bound, or operationalize the hypothesis.

## Operational definitions required

Define measurable proxies for:

- moral capital;
- interpersonal trust;
- institutional trust;
- costly prosocial behavior;
- norm transmission;
- secularization;
- religious inheritance;
- moral regeneration;
- depletion.

## Competing hypotheses

### H1 — Christian inheritance
Lutheran teaching, literacy, parish organization, conscience, neighbor duty, and universal moral status created durable norms later secularized by welfare states.

### H2 — Pre-Christian/institutional continuity
Nordic assemblies, peasant autonomy, low feudalism, landholding patterns, and local cooperation explain much of the trust before or apart from Christianity.

### H3 — State-capacity and welfare
Universal institutions, low corruption, competent administration, equality, and social insurance generate trust contemporaneously.

### H4 — Selection and ecology
Population structure, geography, war history, disease environment, and migration patterns explain key differences.

### H5 — Reciprocal causation
Trust enabled welfare institutions, which then reinforced trust; religion was one component in a feedback loop rather than the root cause.

### H6 — Measurement artifact
Survey measures, social desirability, underreporting, or narrow definitions overstate Nordic moral performance.

## Required research design

1. long-run timelines of trust, literacy, religiosity, state formation, inequality, and welfare expansion;
2. comparisons among Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Finland, and Iceland rather than treating Scandinavia as one unit;
3. within-Nordic regional comparisons;
4. comparison with culturally Lutheran but lower-trust cases;
5. comparison with high-trust non-Christian cases;
6. comparison with secular societies lacking Nordic outcomes;
7. migration and second-generation evidence where useful;
8. explicit causal diagrams and reverse-causation tests.

## Required deliverables

1. moral-capital variable dictionary;
2. historical timeline;
3. competing causal graph;
4. evidence-for/evidence-against matrix;
5. breaker-case catalog;
6. strongest Christian-inheritance case;
7. strongest institutional/material case;
8. verdict separating contribution, necessity, sufficiency, and present regeneration;
9. list of claims that current evidence cannot support.

## Breaker evidence

The inherited-moral-capital thesis should be weakened if:

- trust clearly predates major Christian/Lutheran influence;
- current institutions regenerate trust independently across populations without shared inheritance;
- culturally similar Lutheran societies diverge mainly according to material or institutional variables;
- non-Christian societies generate equally durable costly prosocial norms through alternative systems;
- long-run secularization does not produce predicted decay in family, trust, duty, or solidarity.

It should be strengthened if:

- historical transmission mechanisms can be identified and temporally precede outcomes;
- secular institutions explicitly inherit and translate Lutheran moral concepts;
- prosocial norms persist after belief decline but weaken when transmission institutions disappear;
- comparable material systems without the inheritance fail to reproduce similar moral outcomes.

## Agent instruction

Treat the hypothesis as guilty until causally clarified. The goal is not to credit Christianity by default, but to determine what was inherited, what was newly generated, and what remains renewable today.
