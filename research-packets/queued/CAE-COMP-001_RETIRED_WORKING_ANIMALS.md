## Research question

How have Islamic and comparator civilizations treated animals after those animals ceased to be economically productive, and what moral, legal, charitable, or public obligations—if any—were recognized toward them?

## Why this cell is load-bearing

Care for productive animals can be explained by self-interest. Care after productivity ends is stronger evidence of gratitude, mercy, and restraint under power asymmetry.

> The retirement question separates husbandry from moral debt.

## Priority hypotheses

### H1 — Islamic waqf retirement hypothesis

Some Islamic societies used charitable endowments to maintain animals that had lost productive value.

**Breaker:** no primary deed or account can be found; surviving evidence refers only to feeding stray birds/cats, productive veterinary care, or modern folklore.

### H2 — post-use moral debt hypothesis

Islamic ethical and legal traditions contained principles that could ground a duty to maintain or humanely release an animal after service.

**Breaker:** legal sources consistently treat end-of-use animals only as disposable property absent private charity.

### H3 — modern superiority hypothesis

Modern non-Muslim societies provide more reliable retirement welfare because of veterinary science, regulation, traceability, and specialized charities.

**Breaker:** large proportions of retired animals still enter slaughter, abandonment, or untracked resale, while historical institutions show more durable lifetime-care obligations.

### H4 — utility persistence hypothesis

Across civilizations, welfare after retirement remains correlated with the animal's remaining utility, prestige, species status, and owner's wealth.

**Breaker:** universal systems provide equal lifetime care independent of economic or symbolic value.

### H5 — industrial regression hypothesis

Modern systems improve formal welfare while increasing total suffering and disposability through breeding scale, market distance, specialization, and industrial throughput.

**Breaker:** per-animal and aggregate welfare evidence shows modern systems improve both formal protection and actual lifetime outcomes across species.

## Proposed modern institution

### Animal Service Pension

A levy, insurance contribution, waqf endowment, or industry trust accumulates during an animal's working life and funds:

- retirement assessment;
- veterinary care;
- sanctuary or qualified rehoming;
- humane end-of-life care;
- traceability and audit;
- owner-of-last-resort protection.

The principle:

> Those who profit from an animal's labor should fund the animal's life after labor.
