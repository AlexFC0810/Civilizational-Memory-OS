# RP-ST-001 — Old Testament *Herem*: Textual Origins, Historical Reality, and Moral Authority

**Status:** Queued research packet  
**Priority:** P0 within the Atrocity Memory and Prevention Engine's sacred-text track  
**Purpose:** Conduct a source-diverse, historically rigorous, morally serious investigation of biblical texts associated with *herem*—the “ban” or total destruction—without anti-Jewish or anti-Christian weaponization, apologetic evasion, or assuming that a difficult text automatically defines a living tradition.

## Central question

> **When a sacred canon depicts God commanding the destruction of an identity-defined population, what exactly are we looking at: historical memory, ancient war rhetoric, retrospective national theology, human redaction, a bounded divine judgment, or a morally unresolved claim to revelation?**

## Why this packet is necessary

Several Hebrew Bible / Old Testament passages appear to command or narrate destruction extending beyond combatants.

Priority texts include:

- Deuteronomy 7:1–6;
- Deuteronomy 20:10–18;
- Joshua 6–12;
- Numbers 31;
- 1 Samuel 15;
- related uses of *herem* and totalizing victory language.

These texts have been:

- defended as unique divine judgment;
- interpreted as ancient military hyperbole;
- allegorized as war against sin;
- historically questioned through archaeology;
- treated as evidence of human moral development inside scripture;
- used by critics to indict Judaism and Christianity;
- appropriated by colonial, nationalist, settler, and extremist actors.

A serious answer requires more than either “God said it” or “it never happened.”

## Memory-integrity boundary

This packet must never:

- treat Jews or Christians as collectively responsible for ancient texts;
- map Canaanites or Amalekites simplistically onto a living ethnic or political population;
- use biblical criticism as cover for antisemitism;
- erase Jewish and Christian traditions of limitation, reinterpretation, repentance, or moral protest;
- permit apologetics to dissolve explicit violence through vague appeals to context;
- infer textual corruption solely because a passage is morally troubling;
- infer divine command solely because a canonical text attributes the command to God.

## Research questions

### A. Lexical and textual

1. What semantic range does *herem* carry across the Hebrew Bible?
2. When does it mean destruction, dedication, confiscation, removal from ordinary use, or something else?
3. What do the Masoretic Text, Septuagint, Samaritan Pentateuch, Dead Sea Scrolls, and other witnesses preserve for the priority passages?
4. Which variants materially change agency, scope, or targets?
5. How do major modern translations render contested terms?
6. Where may translation intensify or soften the original?

### B. Composition and redaction

1. When were Deuteronomy, Joshua, Numbers 31, and 1 Samuel 15 composed and redacted according to major scholarly models?
2. What layers may derive from monarchic, Josianic, exilic, post-exilic, priestly, or later contexts?
3. Did conquest narratives help construct identity after territorial loss or imperial domination?
4. Which political, priestly, land, centralization, and boundary-making interests may be reflected?
5. What evidence supports or weakens claims of elite or state-shaped redaction?

### C. Ancient Near Eastern comparison

1. How did Egyptian, Assyrian, Moabite, Hittite, and other royal inscriptions describe victory?
2. How common was “we destroyed everyone; none remained” language when populations clearly survived?
3. Does shared rhetoric show hyperbole, genre convention, or genuine exterminatory aspiration?
4. What makes the biblical cases similar or distinctive?
5. Does literary exaggeration resolve the moral problem of attributing annihilation to God?

### D. Archaeology and historicity

1. Is there evidence for a rapid, unified conquest of Canaan matching Joshua?
2. Which cities show destruction, continuity, abandonment, or incompatible chronology?
3. What do settlement patterns imply about Israelite emergence from local Canaanite populations?
4. Are Joshua and Judges historically compatible?
5. How should absence of a total conquest affect interpretation of the text's moral authority?
6. Could a later imagined conquest still function as dangerous sacred precedent?

### E. Internal biblical moral architecture

Map difficult passages against:

- prohibitions on murder;
- Abraham's challenge concerning the righteous and wicked;
- individual versus inherited guilt, especially Deuteronomy 24:16, Jeremiah 31:29–30, and Ezekiel 18;
- prophetic condemnation of violence and injustice;
- protection of strangers;
- mercy and repentance;
- enemy treatment;
- the book of Jonah;
- later wisdom and prophetic traditions;
- New Testament teachings attributed to Jesus concerning enemies, mercy, and violence.

Questions:

1. Is there a governing moral trajectory?
2. Do later texts correct, deepen, or contradict earlier violence?
3. Is “progressive revelation” supported by the canon or imposed by later theology?
4. Can a canon contain human moral error while remaining sacred?
5. How do Jewish and Christian approaches differ?

### F. Jewish interpretation and limitation

Research:

- rabbinic treatment of the seven nations and Amalek;
- claims that ancient target identities no longer exist or cannot be identified;
- procedural or moral limitations;
- Maimonidean and other legal treatments;
- allegorical and ethical reinterpretations;
- modern Jewish theological responses after the Holocaust;
- contemporary misuse and internal Jewish criticism.

The packet must include Orthodox, Conservative, Reform, academic, Israeli, diaspora, and dissenting perspectives without assuming one represents Judaism.

### G. Christian interpretation

Research:

- early church allegorical readings;
- Augustine, Aquinas, Reformation, and modern traditions;
- dispensational, evangelical, Catholic, Orthodox, Anabaptist, liberationist, and historical-critical approaches;
- Christological claims that Jesus governs Christian reading of the Old Testament;
- defenses based on divine judgment;
- arguments for hyperbole;
- theories that Israel misunderstood God;
- the moral cost and coherence of each approach.

### H. Historical and modern reactivation

Investigate use of conquest texts in:

- Crusading rhetoric;
- European colonialism;
- settler expansion;
- wars against Indigenous populations;
- slavery or racial ideology;
- South African apartheid theology;
- modern nationalist territorial claims;
- Christian Zionism;
- extremist Jewish rhetoric;
- anti-Jewish propaganda by critics of the Bible.

For each case distinguish:

- direct citation;
- background myth;
- opportunistic analogy;
- actual policy causation;
- later polemical association.

## Competing hypotheses

### H1 — Historical divine-command account

The passages preserve a real, bounded, non-repeatable divine judgment.

Required evidence:

- reliable historical event;
- authenticated command;
- coherent reason innocent status does not apply or is not actually violated;
- principled prevention of modern reuse.

### H2 — Ancient war-hyperbole account

The language follows conventional total-victory rhetoric and does not describe literal annihilation.

Required evidence:

- robust comparative rhetoric;
- internal evidence of survivors;
- clear genre conventions;
- explanation of why divine speech uses morally catastrophic imagery.

### H3 — Retrospective national-theology account

Later authors or redactors constructed conquest narratives to explain identity, land, covenant, purity, or centralization.

Required evidence:

- dating and redaction signals;
- ideological fit with later contexts;
- archaeological tension;
- comparison with state-forming literature.

### H4 — Mixed-memory account

The texts preserve memories of local conflict, migration, settlement, and destruction later organized into total conquest.

### H5 — Human moral-error-inside-canon account

The canon preserves a community's sincere but mistaken attribution of violence to God, while later revelation or moral insight corrects it.

This is theologically available in some Christian frameworks but conflicts with strong doctrines of scriptural inerrancy.

### H6 — Deliberate political corruption account

Powerful actors altered or framed traditions to legitimate land, monarchy, priestly centralization, exclusion, or war.

This must not be accepted merely because it is possible. It requires specific textual, historical, and institutional evidence.

## Verdict structure

The final packet must produce separate verdicts on:

1. **Textual presence** — what wording is securely attested.
2. **Historical event** — what likely occurred.
3. **Composition** — when and why the account reached its form.
4. **Genre** — literal, hyperbolic, symbolic, mixed.
5. **Traditional authority** — how Jewish and Christian communities rank it.
6. **Moral coherence** — whether a defensible resolution exists.
7. **Historical use** — how it shaped behavior.
8. **Modern risk** — how it can be reactivated.
9. **Anti-capture safeguard** — what prevents identity-wide application today.
10. **Open problem** — what remains genuinely unresolved.

## Breaker evidence

The packet must state what would materially change each verdict, including:

- earlier manuscripts;
- improved dating;
- new archaeological findings;
- proof of a genre convention;
- evidence of actual extermination;
- demonstrable redaction;
- early interpretive limits;
- recurring modern operationalization;
- a coherent or incoherent internal moral resolution.

## Public-output boundary

Potentially defensible working statement:

> **The Old Testament contains texts that, on their face, attribute totalizing violence to divine command. Archaeology and literary comparison complicate whether these events occurred as narrated, but neither hyperbole nor disputed historicity automatically removes the moral problem. The serious task is to reconstruct the texts' formation, meaning, reception, and modern risk without turning living Jews or Christians into the accused population.**

Do not publicly claim before completion:

- that the passages were definitely inserted by rulers;
- that they prove Judaism or Christianity is genocidal;
- that archaeology has disproved every conquest event;
- that hyperbole fully resolves the moral issue;
- that Qur'anic preservation alone settles moral superiority;
- that all biblical violence is absent from later Christian or Jewish life.

## Canonical close

> **The hardest sacred-text question is not whether ancient people wrote violent words. It is how a community determines whether those words came from God, from human fear and power, or from both a revelation and the imperfect history through which people carried it.**

> **No tradition is protected by refusing the question, and no people should be condemned by answering it carelessly.**
