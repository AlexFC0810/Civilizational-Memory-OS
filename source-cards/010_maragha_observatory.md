# Source Card 010: The Maragha Observatory (1259)

Date: 2026-07-19

## Status

**Deployment status:** reconnaissance-closure draft through the mechanical gate; graded B at the Sonnet drafting ceiling, pending frontier adjudication (grade-A promotion is not this tier's call — see Evidence Grade for what would justify it).

**Best use:** the comparative-resilience emblem for LB-28/LB-29 ("Baghdad fell; the network rerouted"). Deploy against "the Mongols ended Islamic science" and "Islamic civilization died in 1258." Pairs with Card 005 (`005_knowledge_hospitals_libraries.md`): 005 documents what the pre-1258 knowledge infrastructure was — including the House of Wisdom, destroyed in the 1258 siege; this card documents the system's regenerative capacity in the year immediately after. Together they replace the "one golden age, then extinction" cartoon with the network map. This is not a card about the sack itself — casualty figures and destruction claims for 1258 need their own card — and it must never be used to shrink that catastrophe.

**House style:** network-not-palace framing, stated precisely: each *node* lived and died by palace patronage — Maragha included — while the claim that survived 1258 is about the wider network of scholars, texts, and institutional forms (LB-28's load). The honest emblem is "one captured scholar persuaded the new power to fund the next node" — not "Muslims rebuilt despite the Mongols." The patron was the destroyer of Baghdad; say so plainly, in the claim itself, not in a buried caveat.

## Claim

In 1259 CE — one year after Hülegü's Mongol army sacked Baghdad (February 1258), a real catastrophe that killed a large share of the city's population, destroyed its institutions including its famous libraries, and ended the Abbasid caliphate in Baghdad — construction began on a major astronomical observatory at Maragha (Marāgheh) in northwestern Iran. Its patron was Hülegü himself: the same Mongol prince whose forces had destroyed Baghdad — and a non-Muslim ruler (his religion is given as Buddhism; the Ilkhanate was not yet a Muslim polity) — authorized the project and funded it by assigning religious-endowment (waqf) revenue, a slice of the conquered civilization's own charitable-trust wealth, to build and sustain it, with his own motive reported by the sources as substantially astrological — he wanted prognostication; the astronomers ran a research program. Its director was Nasir al-Din al-Tusi, a Persian polymath who passed into Hülegü's service when the Mongols took the Ismaili fortresses in 1256 (accounts vary on whether he was at Alamut or at Maymun-Diz at the capitulation) and who is reported by the fetched sources to have been with Hülegü's forces during the Baghdad campaign — one tertiary source states this as "widely assumed" and controversial rather than directly documented. The observatory became one of the leading scientific institutions of its era: purpose-built large instruments (a mural quadrant, an armillary sphere, and an azimuth quadrant of al-Tusi's own design), a substantial library, an educational function, and a named international team — Mu'ayyad al-Din al-'Urdi of Damascus as chief instrument-builder, Muhyi al-Din al-Maghribi, Qutb al-Din al-Shirazi, and the Chinese astronomer Fao Munji, with the Syriac Christian scholar Bar Hebraeus taking up residence nearby to use its library. Twelve years of observations there produced the Zij-i Ilkhani astronomical tables (completed c. 1272, published under Hülegü's son Abaqa). For the Load-Bearing Index this card carries one load (LB-29, servicing LB-28): the sharpest single empirical emblem that the 1258 catastrophe did not end Islamic scientific civilization — one node of the scholarly network re-anchored within a year, on the conqueror's own court. (The broader network-survival generalization — plural centers, post-1258 continuation — is LB-28's load, resting on its fuller evidence base, not on this card alone.)

This does **not** prove the sack of Baghdad was minor — it was a civilizational catastrophe, and nothing at Maragha offsets the dead of Baghdad. It does not prove Islamic science was unharmed by the Mongol conquests generally; one flourishing institution is compatible with enormous net destruction elsewhere. It does not by itself prove a transmission line to Copernicus — the resemblance of late Maragha-school devices to his is real, but the route is debated among historians of science. And it does not prove the observatory was permanent: it lost patronage after its founding generation and was inactive by the early 14th century. The resilience claim — that this institutional form kept being regenerated — is carried by LB-28's post-1258 enumeration; Maragha is its sharpest dated emblem, not the whole proof, and it was never a claim about one building's lifespan.

## Source Anchors

### Wikipedia: the founding, the waqf, the team, the motive, the decline

Wikipedia summary:

> "The construction of the observatory began in 1259 and lasted between 3 and 5 years." Before that, "al-Tusi spoke to Hulagu about the need for new astronomy tables," and "due to his own interest in astrology, the Khan responded by authorizing the funding and construction of the Maragheh observatory." Funding mechanism: "al-Tusi was placed in charge of waqfs, a type of religious endowments, which Hulagu would later order him to use to build and fund the observatory." On the director's path: "In 1256, the Mongols took the Alamut castle, where al-Tusi and several other scholars had taken refuge," and "Hulagu and his men took al-Tusi with them as they then went to sack Baghdad." "Al-Tusi was the first director of the observatory." The named staff includes Mu'ayyid al-Din al-'Urdi of Damascus — "in charge of constructing the buildings...as well as manufacturing the astronomical instruments" — Muhyi al-Din al-Maghribi, Qutb al-Din al-Shirazi, "Hulagu's Chinese astronomer" Fao Munji, and Bar-Hebraeus, who took residence nearby to use the library. Decline: "A major blow came when the observatory lost its patronage after the deaths of Hulagu in 1265 and his son Abaqa in 1282"; the site "turned to ruins as a result of frequent earthquakes and a lack of funding" and "became inactive by the beginning of the 14th century." Legacy: "It was also used as a model for the later Ulugh Beg Observatory in Samarkand, the Taqi al-Din observatory in Constantinople, and Jantar Mantar observatory in Jaipur." Today: "A dome shaped cover has been built to protect the remnants of the observatory," and "A celestial globe from the observatory made around 1279 is now preserved in Dresden, Germany."

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maragheh_observatory

### Wikipedia: al-Tusi's path into Mongol service, and the hedge on his presence at Baghdad

Wikipedia summary:

> "He was captured after the capitulation of Maymun-Diz to the Mongol forces under Hulagu Khan" — note the fortress differs from the Maragheh-observatory article's Alamut — after which "Hulegu, who was himself interested in the natural sciences, treated al-Tusi with great respect, appointing him as scientific adviser." On Baghdad: "it is widely assumed Tusi was with the Mongol forces under Hulegu when they attacked and massacred the inhabitants of Baghdad in 1258," a matter the article marks as controversial. On the observatory: "Tusi convinced Hulegu Khan to construct an observatory for establishing accurate astronomical tables for better astrological predictions," with construction "Beginning in 1259." The Zij-i Ilkhani is dated as "completed in 1272." On the mathematics: "he invented a geometrical technique called a Tusi-couple, which generates linear motion from the sum of two circular motions," which "was later employed in Ibn al-Shatir's geocentric model and Nicolaus Copernicus' heliocentric Copernican model" — while on the transmission question "there is still no direct proof that Copernicus did plagiarize."

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasir_al-Din_al-Tusi

### Wikipedia: the Zij-i Ilkhani — the observatory's flagship output, with the team named again

Wikipedia summary:

> "It was compiled by the Muslim astronomer Nasir al-Din al-Tusi in collaboration with his research team of astronomers at the Maragha observatory," including "Muhyi al-Din al-Maghribi, Qutb al-Din al-Shirazi, Mu'ayyid al-Din al-'Urdi from Damascus," while "the influence of Chinese astronomy was brought by Fao Munji." "Hulagu gave permission to build a new observatory in a place of al-Tusi's choice (he chose Maragheh)." The work "included data derived from the observations made over the course of 12 years in the Maragha observatory, completed in 1272," and "the tables were published during the reign of Abaqa Khan, Hulagu's son, and named after the patron of the observatory."

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zij-i_Ilkhani

### MacTutor (University of St Andrews): the scholarly-biography corroboration, with dates and instruments

MacTutor summary:

> "Al-Tusi was in the castle of Alamut when it was attacked" in 1256, and "some claim that al-Tusi betrayed the defences of Alamut to the invading Mongols" — an unverified accusation this card reports as such. He was "with the Mongol forces under Hulegu when they attacked Baghdad in 1258." "When al-Tusi presented Hulegu with plans for the construction of a fine Observatory, Hulegu was happy to agree." "Construction of the Observatory began in 1259 west of Maragheh," and "The observatory at Maragheh became operational in 1262." Instruments included "a 4 metre wall quadrant made from copper and an azimuth quadrant which was the invention of Al-Tusi himself"; the institution "possessed a fine library with books on a wide range of scientific topics" (no volume count given). "He published Zij-i ilkhani (the Ilkhanic Tables), written first in Persian and later translated into Arabic, after making observations for 12 years." On the Tusi couple: al-Tusi "employed a theorem invented by himself which, 250 years later, occurred again in Copernicus, 'De Revolutionibus', III 4," while "Many historians claim that the Tusi-couple result was used by Copernicus after he discovered it in Al-Tusi's work, but not all agree" — some argue Copernicus could have drawn it from Proclus instead.

Source: https://mathshistory.st-andrews.ac.uk/Biographies/Al-Tusi_Nasir/

### Max Planck Institute for the History of Science: the research-institution character and the astrological purpose

MPIWG project-page summary:

> The observatory complex, "founded in 1259 in northwest Iran," was proposed by al-Tusi to Hülegü and "tasked with producing an updated series of astronomical tables (Zīj-i Īlkhānī) to be used in astrological prognostication as the needs of the court might occasion." It grew beyond that brief into a multidisciplinary complex that "hosted not only mathematicians and philosophers, but also theologians and jurists, and even poets, for long-term and short-term residencies," and "The observatory complex also offered education for young scholars, in particular in mathematics and astronomy."

Source: https://www.mpiwg-berlin.mpg.de/research/projects/maragha-observatory-complex-ilkhanid-iran

### Muslim Heritage: a tertiary account used with its errors flagged — and a witness against the "first observatory" overclaim

Muslim Heritage summary:

> The observatory was "managed by no less than Nasir Al-Din Al-Tusi (d. 1274) and Qutb Al-Din Al-Shirazi," was "Fitted with a large library (over 400,000 books)" — the same round figure the UNESCO portal hedges, here repeated as bare fact, which is exactly how a topos propagates — and functioned as "an institution for research, and an academy for scientific contacts and teaching." "It lasted until at least the beginning of the 14th century. Today, however, all that remain are the foundations of it." Usefully, it does not crown Maragha "the first observatory" — it gives that title to "Shammasiyah observatory, which Caliph Al-Mamun had built in Baghdad around 828," which is one more reason this card never asserts a Maragha-first claim as house wording.

Source: https://muslimheritage.com/astro-observatories-classic-islam-culture/

## Best Load-Bearing Question

> **Baghdad fell in February 1258. In 1259, construction of a major observatory began at Maragha — funded by Hülegü, the same prince who had destroyed Baghdad, financed through religious endowments, staffed by a named international team including a Damascene chief engineer and a Chinese astronomer, and productive enough to deliver a new set of astronomical tables by about 1272. If the Mongol conquest ended Islamic science, who built that — and why was the conqueror paying for it?**

## S-Tier Lines

> Baghdad fell in 1258. The Maragha observatory rose in 1259 — and the man paying for it was the same prince who had destroyed Baghdad.

> Within a year of Baghdad's destruction, a scholar the Mongols had themselves captured had persuaded the conqueror to fund a new major observatory — bankrolled, through waqf, from the conquered civilization's own endowment income.

> Hülegü wanted astrology. What al-Tusi built with the revenue Hülegü assigned him was an institution: waqf-financed, purpose-built instruments, an international research team, students in training, and a twelve-year observation program that produced the Ilkhanid Tables.

> Maragha itself went quiet within about half a century. The deeper fact is what returned: the capacity to build again — Samarkand in the 1420s, on Maragha's own plans, after a gap of more than a century; Istanbul in 1577.

## Honest Caveats

1. **Do not say "Muslims rebuilt despite the Mongols."** The honest version — and the stronger one — is that the scholarly network survived by capturing the new power's patronage: Hülegü authorized and funded the observatory, and al-Tusi had entered his service after the Ismaili fortresses fell in 1256. Say the patron's name plainly inside the claim, never in a footnote.
2. **Do not overstate al-Tusi's Baghdad role.** Fetched sources report him with Hülegü's forces during the 1258 Baghdad campaign; one states this as "widely assumed" and controversial rather than directly documented, and MacTutor records an unverified accusation that he betrayed Alamut's defenses. Report the service and the hedges together; do not launder him into a bystander or inflate him into the campaign's architect.
3. **Do not imply the observatory was permanent.** It lost patronage after the deaths of Hülegü (1265) and Abaqa (1282) and was inactive by the beginning of the 14th century; today its remnants sit under a protective dome. Specialist work identified via search this session (not read directly: a study titled "Marāgha Ceased to Function: The Ṭūsī Family's Intellectual Network and Īl-Khānid Political Itinerance") ties the end specifically to the founding family's hereditary hold on the observatory's waqf trusteeship and its fall from Ilkhanid court favor around 1309–10 — a patronage-and-family mechanism, flagged here at identified-not-verified grade. The resilience claim is the civilization's capacity to regenerate the institutional form — Samarkand in the 1420s "according to the plans of the Maragha observatory," Istanbul completed 1577 — not this building's lifespan. And carry the succession's own honesty: the Istanbul observatory was destroyed in 1580.
4. **Never minimize the sack of Baghdad.** Mass death, destruction of institutions including the House of Wisdom and its libraries, the end of the Abbasid caliphate in Baghdad. This card is evidence the civilization was not decapitated into extinction; it is not evidence the blow was light, and one observatory offsets none of it.
5. **Do not say "Copernicus copied al-Tusi."** The fetched record supports only this: the Tusi couple later appears in Ibn al-Shatir's models and in Copernicus's; historians dispute the transmission route ("not all agree"; "there is still no direct proof"), with Proclus proposed as an alternative source. State the resemblance, attribute the debate, stop there — or omit Copernicus entirely.
6. **Do not deploy "400,000 volumes" as fact.** The UNESCO-IAU portal itself hedges it ("is said to have contained about 400,000 volumes"); Muslim Heritage repeats it as bare fact; it is a suspiciously round topos-like number of the same genre as Cordoba's "600,000 books" (see Card 005's discipline). Say "a substantial library; traditional accounts claim about 400,000 volumes, unverified and likely inflated" — or just say "a major library."
7. **Keep date claims at fetched precision.** Construction began 1259 (uniform); operational 1262 (MacTutor); construction took 3-5 years (Wikipedia); Zij-i Ilkhani completed c. 1272. The attribution of the 1259 order to al-Tusi's own Zij preface surfaced only in search-result summaries, not in a fetched source — treat the primary basis as identified, not verified. The 657 AH equivalence is conversion, not attestation. Alamut vs Maymun-Diz as al-Tusi's fortress of capture is an unresolved variance between fetched sources.
8. **State the astrological motive; do not weaponize it in either direction.** Three fetched sources report Hülegü's purpose as astrological prognostication. That is honesty about the patron's motive, not a debunking of the science: motive and program are different layers, and the program's outputs (tables, instruments, planetary models, trained students) are documented regardless of why the patron paid. Court astrology was the era's normal funding mode for astronomy well beyond the Islamic world: Tycho Brahe prepared horoscopes for the Danish princes at their births and owed the crown an annual astrological almanac — the same patron-motive/program-output layering, three centuries later, in Lutheran Denmark.
9. **Do not assert "the world's first true observatory" as house wording.** Sayılı's institutional-first thesis about Maragha exists and may be reported as his attributed scholarly thesis; meanwhile one of this card's own fetched sources gives the "first" title to al-Ma'mun's Shammasiyah observatory (Baghdad, c. 828). "First" claims in this literature are unstable; this card's load never depends on one.
10. **Do not over-attribute the achievement to a single agent.** A non-Muslim Mongol patron, Islamic legal financing, a Persian director, a Damascene engineer, a Chinese astronomer, a Syriac Christian bishop in the library. The card's claim layer is `institution` — the founding of one institution; the "it was a network" topology claim is an outcome-layer inference carried by LB-28, not established by this one building — and none of it is a proof about any one religion, dynasty, or ethnicity's essence.
11. **Name the gap and the demolition honestly.** Maragha was inactive by the early 14th century; Samarkand's observatory was founded in the 1420s — a gap of more than a century with no comparable institution named in this card. And the succession line's endpoint carries its own hard fact: Istanbul's observatory, completed 1577, was demolished in 1580 — per the UNESCO-IAU portal, after Taqi al-Din's comet prognostication of Ottoman victory "turned out to be false, whereupon the angry Sultan Murad III. regarded the observatory as worthless, stopped funding and had the observatory demolished in 1580." Widely circulated accounts add a religious-authority objection (a Sheikh ul-Islam warning against observation); revisionist accounts dispute that as later fabrication and blame court rivalry; this card does not adjudicate between them — but in every version the destroyer was indigenous (sultan, clergy, or court), not a foreign conqueror. The same-era contrast is stated rather than hidden: Tycho Brahe's Uraniborg, funded by the Danish crown at almost the same moment, ran for roughly two decades and fed Kepler. Deliberate revival across discontinuity is the honest shape of the succession claim — and it survives these facts only if it carries them.

## Safe Wording

> In February 1258, Hülegü's Mongol army sacked Baghdad — a genuine catastrophe that killed a large share of the city's population, destroyed institutions including its famous libraries, and ended the Abbasid caliphate there. In 1259, one year later, construction of a major astronomical observatory began at Maragha in northwestern Iran — and its patron was Hülegü himself, a non-Muslim Mongol prince, who funded it by directing the conquered civilization's own religious-endowment (waqf) revenue to it, reportedly because he wanted astrological prognostication. Its director, Nasir al-Din al-Tusi, had entered Hülegü's service after the Mongols took the Ismaili fortresses in 1256 and is reported to have been with Hülegü's forces during the Baghdad campaign. The observatory assembled purpose-built instruments, a substantial library (traditional accounts claim about 400,000 volumes — an unverified and likely inflated figure), and a named international team — Mu'ayyad al-Din al-'Urdi of Damascus, Muhyi al-Din al-Maghribi, Qutb al-Din al-Shirazi, the Chinese astronomer Fao Munji — and twelve years of observations there produced the Zij-i Ilkhani astronomical tables by about 1272. The observatory itself declined within roughly half a century, inactive by the early 14th century; the institutional model it pioneered was deliberately revived by later dynasties — Samarkand's Ulugh Beg observatory, built in the 1420s to Maragha's own plans though Maragha had been inactive for more than a century by then, and Istanbul's in 1577, itself demolished in 1580 amid contested court-and-clergy politics. None of this makes the sack of Baghdad minor, and it does not settle the debated question of transmission to Copernicus; it shows something narrower and harder to dismiss — that the scholarly network survived the catastrophe, in part by capturing the conqueror's own patronage.

## Counterattack Simulation

### Counter: "It was a Mongol project, not Islamic resilience — if anything it proves conquest works."

Response:

> The patron was Mongol and the sovereign authorization was his — but the money itself was requisitioned waqf income: a slice of the conquered civilization's own charitable-endowment wealth, redirected by decree and administered by al-Tusi. Look at what it bought and who spent it: an institution designed and directed by a Persian Muslim polymath, staffed by scholars formed in Damascus, Persia, and the Maghreb tradition alongside Hülegü's Chinese astronomer Fao Munji, producing a zij — the canonical genre of Islamic astronomy — and training the next generation in it. The serious scholarly version of this counter exists and is conceded: studies of the Mongol exchange system (e.g. Thomas Allsen's *Culture and Conquest in Mongol Eurasia* — identified via search, not read directly) treat Maragha as a showcase of Chinggisid integration of conquered scientific traditions, Iran–China exchange included; the imperial apparatus supplied the integration, and the Chinese astronomy came with it. But the program's genre, working languages, instrument tradition, and training pipeline remained the Islamic astronomical tradition's own — and "conquest works" would predict replacement, not the conqueror's court adopting the conquered culture's institutional forms and personnel. Patronage itself is no anomaly to explain away — Islamic scientific institutions had always run on court patronage; what this episode demonstrates is that the scholarly tradition could survive the annihilation of its capital and re-anchor on a new court within a single year.

### Counter: "It declined within decades — some resilience."

Response:

> Correct on the building, and the card states it: patronage faltered after Hülegü (d. 1265) and Abaqa (d. 1282), the observatory was inactive by the early 14th century, and its remnants sit under a protective dome today. Patronage-dependence was a real structural weakness of the medieval observatory as a form — and the succession line's own endpoint proves it in the harshest way: Taqi al-Din's Istanbul observatory, completed in 1577, was demolished in 1580 by order of the very court that had funded it, after a failed comet prognostication (a disputed religious-objection account circulates alongside; see Honest Caveats) — an indigenous demolition, not a conqueror's, and a gap of more than a century separates Maragha's fall from Samarkand's rise. But the resilience claim was never "this building lasted forever"; it is that the civilization's capacity to generate such institutions survived 1258 — demonstrated by Maragha itself in 1259, then by Samarkand in the 1420s, built "according to the plans of the Maragha observatory," then by Istanbul in 1577. A network's health is measured by regeneration, not by the immortality of any node. The mortality of the node is conceded; the regeneration is documented.

### Counter: "One observatory doesn't offset Baghdad — that's cherry-picking."

Response:

> Nothing offsets Baghdad, and this card refuses to try — the sack was mass death and institutional destruction, stated as such in the claim itself. But check what proposition this card is deployed against: "the Mongols ended Islamic science" / "Islamic civilization died in 1258." That is an extinction claim, and an extinction claim is exactly the kind of claim a single dated counterexample can break: a major new scientific institution under construction within a year, funded by the destroyer himself, productive for decades, and used as the template for successors into the 15th and 16th centuries. This card does not quantify total losses from the Mongol conquests — that requires its own cards, and the losses were enormous. It kills the cartoon, not the catastrophe: an emblem calibrated to the claim it answers, which is what an emblem is for (see LB-28 for the broader post-1258 evidence base this card does not carry alone).

### Counter: "al-Tusi was a collaborator serving the men who slaughtered Baghdad."

Response:

> The uncomfortable facts are in the card, not hidden from it: al-Tusi passed from Ismaili service into Mongol service when the fortresses fell in 1256; fetched sources report him with Hülegü's forces during the Baghdad campaign (one marks it "widely assumed" and controversial); MacTutor records an unverified accusation that he betrayed Alamut's defenses. This card documents an institution; it does not beatify its director, and the moral judgment of a scholar who attached himself to the conqueror is a live question the card leaves open rather than answers. But notice that this counter, pressed to its end, concedes the card's actual claim: the route by which Islamic scientific civilization survived the catastrophe ran through the conqueror's own court — scholars making survival choices under empire, and the tradition keeping its corpus, its methods, and its training pipeline intact through them. Whether one calls that collaboration or preservation, it is the opposite of extinction — which is the only proposition this card is built to test.
