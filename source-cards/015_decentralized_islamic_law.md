# Source Card 015: The Decentralization of Classical Islamic Law

Date: 2026-07-21

## Status

**Deployment status:** reconnaissance-closure draft through the mechanical single-file gate; graded B at the drafting ceiling, pending frontier adjudication and Chief-of-Staff integration (grade-A promotion is not this tier's call — see Evidence Grade for what would justify it).

**Best use:** defuse "sharia is a brutal, monolithic state code" and "Islamic law is theocratic state law." Pairs with Card 004 (`004_sharia_moral_ecology.md`): 004 draws the category firewall — source (sharia) / human interpretation (fiqh) / state codification / culture — i.e. what the *layers* are; this card documents how *authority was distributed across* those layers (jurists, not the state, held the law) and why that distribution made the legal-scholarly order resilient to political collapse. **House style:** "jurists' law, not state law," stated precisely — this is decentralization of *doctrinal/interpretive* authority, coexisting with *centralized coercive enforcement*. Never let it read as "sharia was gentle" or "sharia was liberal." Name the hudud and the ruler's siyasa jurisdiction *inside the claim*, not in a buried caveat (Card 010 discipline). ## Claim

For most of its pre-modern history — roughly the 9th to the 18th–19th centuries — Islamic law was what legal historians call a **"jurists' law"**: the authority to determine what the law *said* was held not by the state but by a self-organizing class of private religious scholars (the *fuqaha* / *ulama*), whose doctrine (*fiqh*) was, in the tradition's own self-understanding, a **fallible and changeable** human interpretation of an immutable divine path (*sharia*) — "elaborated and developed over the centuries by legal opinions issued by qualified jurists" who met and taught "in study circles." That scholarship crystallized into **plural schools of law (madhhabs)** — the four Sunni schools (Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali) alongside the Ja'fari, Zaydi, and Isma'ili schools — which "recognize each other's validity," shared most of their rulings, differed on the rest, and each took its name "from the classical jurist who founded them" rather than from a dynasty or a state. The ruler's role in this order was **enforcement and administration, not legislation**: rulers appointed the judges (*qadis*) who applied the jurists' *fiqh* and supplied the coercive sanction "that only the supreme ruler as head of the state could guarantee" — while retaining a **separate, discretionary jurisdiction of their own** (*siyasa*; the *mazalim* grievance courts "presided over by the supreme ruler himself or his governor"), whose judge "was not bound to the rules of Islamic law (*fiqh*)." Because interpretive authority was distributed across scholars and competing schools rather than monopolized by a single sovereign, the legal-scholarly order had a coherence that did not depend on any one political center. This is the load-bearing consequence LB-22 carries: a civilization built on this kind of law could lose political unity — caliphates fragmenting, dynasties falling, the Mongol sack of Baghdad — without simultaneously losing its legal and scholarly coherence, because the map of the law had never been stored in the palace.

This does **not** prove that Islamic law was gentle, liberal, or non-coercive. "Decentralized" describes *who held interpretive authority*, not how humane the law's content was: the jurists' books contained the *hudud* — severe fixed penalties "ranging from public lashing, public stoning to death, amputation of hands, crucifixion." It does not prove the state was weak or absent: **enforcement was the ruler's centralized, coercive power**, and the ruler's own *siyasa* / *mazalim* courts, unbound by *fiqh*, could be more arbitrary than the jurists' law allowed — the same discretionary jurisdiction that let a ruler bypass the jurists' famously high evidentiary thresholds. It does not prove the scholars were always an effective check: some were co-opted court *ulama*, and during the Mihna (833–c.851) caliphs coerced scholars to affirm state theology (LB-41). It does not prove that jurist-made law was distinctively Islamic — Roman law (the jurisconsults), Jewish law (rabbinic *halakha*), and English common law are all, in part, jurists' law. And it does not describe most *present-day* "Islamic law": modern codification (the Ottoman *Mecelle*, colonial-era codes, and the nation-state) largely dissolved this decentralized order by moving the law *into* the state — which is why today's state-enforced penal codes are, in important respects, a modern *centralizing* departure from the classical model, not its continuation. The resilience conclusion is a probable synthesis inference carried by LB-28's post-1258 record, not a fact this fiqh-layer card establishes on its own.

## Source Anchors

### Fiqh (Wikipedia): fiqh is a fallible, changeable human interpretation, not the immutable path

Wikipedia summary:

> "Fiqh is often described as the style of human understanding, research and practices of the sharia," and — the load-bearing distinction — "whereas sharia is considered immutable and infallible by Muslims, fiqh is considered fallible and changeable." Its sources, in order, are given as the Qur'an, Hadith, ijma (consensus), the consensus of the Sahaba, and qiyas (analogy). On the schools: "These schools share many of their rulings, but differ on the particular hadiths they accept as authentic and the weight they give to analogy or reason." On the scholars' posture toward power, the article states early scholars acted "as a counterbalance to the rulers," and "When they saw injustice, all these scholars spoke out against it."

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiqh

### Madhhab (Wikipedia): plural schools that recognize each other's validity, named for jurists not states

Wikipedia summary:

> "A madhhab refers to any school of thought within Islamic jurisprudence." "The major Sunni madhhab are Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, and Hanbali." On the Shia schools: "The development of Shia legal schools ... resulted in the formation of the Ja'fari madhhab amongst Twelver Shias, as well as the Isma'ili and Zaydi madhhabs." The pluralism-with-coherence point: "These four schools recognize each other's validity and they have interacted in legal debate over the centuries." The jurist-not-state locus: "Sunni schools take their name from the classical jurist who founded them." And the geographic spread that outlived any single polity: "the Maliki school is predominant in North and West Africa; the Hanafi school in South and Central Asia; the Shafi'i school in East Africa and Southeast Asia; and the Hanbali school in North and Central Arabia."

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madhhab

### Qadi (Wikipedia): the judge applies the jurists' law; the ruler supplies force and keeps a court of his own

Wikipedia summary — this anchor carries the *fiqh* / *qada* / *siyasa* triangle:

> "A qadi is the magistrate or judge of a Sharia court," and "A qadi is a judge responsible for the application of Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh)." On appointment and administration: "The rulers appointed a qadi in every region, town, and village for judicial and administrative control." On the dependence: "A qadi continued, however, to be a delegate of a higher authority, ultimately the caliph or, after the demise of the caliphate, the supreme ruler in a given territory" — but the dependence was narrow: "What a qadi owed to the supreme ruler was solely the power to apply the law for which sanctions were necessary that only the supreme ruler as head of the state could guarantee." And the ruler's separate, fiqh-unbound jurisdiction: "The maẓālim was a court (presided over by the supreme ruler himself or his governor) that heard complaints addressed to it by virtually any offended party ... The maẓālim judge was not bound to the rules of Islamic law (fiqh)."

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qadi

### Ijtihad (Wikipedia): law made by individual qualified jurists' reasoning, and the disputed "closing of the gate"

Wikipedia summary:

> Ijtihad is "independent reasoning by an expert in Islamic law" — "the process of legal reasoning and hermeneutics through which the jurist-mujtahid derives or rationalizes law on the basis of the Qur'an and the Sunna." "An Islamic scholar who is qualified to perform ijtihad is called a 'mujtahid'." "It is contrasted with taqlid (imitation, conformity to legal precedent)." On the much-cited decline narrative and its revision: "the phrase 'closing of the gate of ijtihād' ... appeared after the 16th century," while "more recent research is said to have disproven the notion that the practice of ijtihad was abandoned in the tenth century — or even later in the 15th century."

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ijtihad

### Hudud (Wikipedia): the coercive edge of the jurists' law — real, severe, and hedged with hard thresholds

Wikipedia summary — the honest-caveat anchor, kept on the *front* of the card, not buried:

> The hudud are "punishments ... ranging from public lashing, public stoning to death, amputation of hands, crucifixion, depending on the crime," for "drinking alcohol, illicit sexual intercourse, false accusations of adultery, theft, apostasy from Islam, highway robbery." On the evidentiary bar the jurists built around them: "Confession and eyewitness testimony are the principle means of establishing guilt for hudud crimes," and "Based on a hadith, jurists stipulated that hudud punishments should be averted by the slightest doubts or ambiguities." On historical frequency: "Hudud were famous for seldom being implemented because the evidentiary standards were very high," and "Because the stringent traditional restrictions on application of hudud punishments, they were rarely applied historically." (This card uses the thresholds to describe the jurists' law honestly — not to imply the penalties were not real, and not to launder them; the ruler's siyasa jurisdiction, being unbound by fiqh, could and did punish outside these limits.)

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hudud

### Sharia (Wikipedia): a law elaborated in scholars' study circles, and the late-modern re-centralization

Wikipedia summary:

> On the jurists-not-state locus: the law "has been elaborated and developed over the centuries by legal opinions issued by qualified jurists – reflecting the tendencies of different schools," and "Juristic thought gradually developed in study circles, where independent scholars met to learn from a local master and discuss religious topics." On where doctrinal authority sat: "As the boundaries of the schools became clearly delineated, the authority of their doctrinal tenets came to be vested in a master jurist from earlier times." And on the modern turn this card is careful to date: "the Islamic revival of the late 20th century brought calls by Islamic movements for full implementation of Sharia, including hudud corporal punishments, such as stoning."

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sharia

### Noah Feldman, *The Fall and Rise of the Islamic State* — scholars as an independent check on the ruler (Princeton University Press)

Publisher's book description:

## Best Load-Bearing Question

> **When you picture "Islamic law," you picture a state enforcing a single code. But for most of a thousand years, who actually decided what the law said — the caliph, or private scholars in competing schools that the caliph appointed judges to obey and could not simply overrule? And if the law was never stored in the palace, what happened to it when the palace fell?**

## S-Tier Lines

> In classical Islam, the state did not own the law. Independent scholars did — organized into competing schools that recognized each other's validity.

> The ruler appointed the judge and supplied the force; the scholars decided what the law was. Enforcement was the state's. Authorship of the law was not.

> That is why the law kept working when empires did not: the authority to say what the law was had never been kept in one capital.

> Decentralized is not the same as gentle. The penalties in the jurists' books were severe — and the ruler kept a court of his own that answered to no school at all. Both things are true, and this card says both.

## Honest Caveats

1. **Decentralized ≠ liberal, humane, or non-coercive.** This is the load-bearing caveat. "Decentralized" is a statement about *who held interpretive authority*, not about the gentleness of the law's content. The jurists' *fiqh* contained the *hudud* — flogging, stoning, amputation, crucifixion for specified crimes including *zina*, theft, highway robbery, and apostasy. Say this plainly; a decentralized authority structure can produce harsh law.
2. **Enforcement was centralized and coercive.** The distribution was of *doctrinal* authority; the *sword* was the ruler's. The qadi depended on the ruler for "the power to apply the law for which sanctions were necessary that only the supreme ruler as head of the state could guarantee." There was a state, and it was coercive; what it did not monopolize was the *authorship* of the law.
3. **The ruler's siyasa/mazalim jurisdiction cuts against romanticism.** The *mazalim* judge "was not bound to the rules of Islamic law (fiqh)." That is a double-edged fact: it shows the ruler's power was real and often *outside* the jurists' restraints — meaning the famously high *hudud* evidentiary thresholds could be bypassed by the ruler's discretionary courts. Do not present the jurists' thresholds as if they governed all punishment; they governed the *fiqh* courts, not the sultan's.
4. **"Separation of powers" is a historical analogy, not modern constitutionalism.** Feldman's framing is explicitly "in historical terms." Do not inflate the scholars-as-check thesis into a claim that classical Islam had liberal constitutional government, elections, or codified rights. The *ulama* were a scholarly elite, not the demos; "communities" shaped custom (*'urf*) but the schools were led by masters.
5. **The check was uneven and sometimes co-opted.** Court *ulama* legitimized rulers; the quietist/obedience strand was real; and during the Mihna (833–c.851) caliphs coerced scholars over state theology (LB-41). The scholars' independence is best evidenced by the fact that such capture attempts are remembered as *aberrations that failed* — not by any claim that it never happened.
6. **Jurist-made law is not distinctively Islamic.** Roman jurisconsults, rabbinic *halakha*, and English common law are all substantially jurists' law. The claim is comparative-descriptive; it is not a superiority or uniqueness claim, and must never be phrased as one (CIG comparative-symmetry: the comparison must survive being run in reverse).
7. **This is largely a *pre-modern* description.** Modern codification — the Ottoman *Mecelle* (1869–1876), colonial-era codes, and the nation-state — moved the law *into* the state. Most present-day state-enforced "Islamic law," including revived *hudud* penal statutes, is a modern *centralizing* development, not a straight continuation of the decentralized classical order. Feldman and Hallaq both read the modern re-centralization as the *break*.
8. **The resilience conclusion is carried, not proven here.** That the civilization "could lose political unity without losing legal and scholarly coherence" is a probable inference resting on LB-28's post-1258 continuation record and LB-29's Maragha emblem — not something the fetched anchors of this card establish on their own. Keep the fiqh-structural claim (established) and the outcome claim (probable) firewalled.
9. **Pluralism had real limits.** The schools policed boundaries; *ijma* could harden positions; *taqlid* constrained; and the "gate of ijtihad" debate is itself contested (the closure thesis is now widely doubted, but so is any simple "it stayed wide open"). "Decentralized" does not mean a free market of legal opinion without orthodoxy or exclusion.

## Unsafe Wording to Avoid

- "Sharia was decentralized, so it was tolerant / liberal / humane." (Conflates the locus of authority with the content of the law — the exact collapse Caveat 1 forbids.)
- "There was no state coercion in Islamic law." (False: enforcement was the ruler's, and the *mazalim* courts were direct state power.)
- "Hudud punishments were basically never carried out." (Overstates the threshold point; the ruler's *siyasa* courts could punish outside *fiqh*, and the modern revival applies them. Say "rarely applied *in the fiqh courts* because the evidentiary bar was high," and stop there.)
- "Islam invented the separation of powers" / "the first separation of powers." (Anachronism and overclaim; Feldman's phrase is a historical analogy.)
- "Only Islam had a jurists' law." (False and needless — Roman, Jewish, and common law share the pattern.)
- "The scholars always kept the rulers in check." (The Mihna and the court *ulama* refute it; the check was real but uneven.)
- "Modern sharia states are just applying the classical system." (Backwards: modern codification centralized the law *into* the state, breaking the classical model.)

## Safe Wording

> Classical Islamic law was a "jurists' law": for most of its history, what the law *said* was determined by independent religious scholars in competing schools — not legislated by the state — so legal and scholarly life could outlast the empires that rose and fell above it.
>
> In more detail: the scholars' doctrine (*fiqh*) was understood as fallible, human, and changeable, distinct from the immutable divine path (*sharia*). It crystallized into plural schools — the four Sunni *madhhabs* plus the Ja'fari, Zaydi, and Isma'ili schools — that "recognize each other's validity" and shared most of their rulings. Rulers appointed the judges (*qadis*) who applied that scholarship and supplied the coercive enforcement, while keeping a separate discretionary jurisdiction of their own (*siyasa* / *mazalim*) that was "not bound to the rules of Islamic law." Because the authority to define the law was distributed among scholars rather than monopolized by one sovereign, the law had a coherence that did not depend on a single capital.
>
> This describes *who held interpretive authority* — not a claim that the law was gentle. The jurists' books contained severe *hudud* penalties, and the ruler's own courts could be more arbitrary still. Nor is jurist-made law distinctively Islamic — Roman, Jewish, and English common law share the pattern. And it is largely a pre-modern description: modern codification moved the law into the state, so today's state-enforced codes are a centralizing departure from the classical model, not its continuation.

## Counterattack Simulation

### Counter: "This is apologetics — sharia was brutal state law. Stoning, amputation, death for apostasy. Calling it 'decentralized' hides the violence."

Response:

> The violence is in the card, not hidden from it: the *hudud* — "public lashing, public stoning to death, amputation of hands, crucifixion" for crimes including *zina*, theft, highway robbery, and apostasy — were genuinely in the jurists' books, and this card says so on its front page, not in a footnote. But two things are being run together that the historical record keeps apart. First, "decentralized" is a claim about *who wrote the law*, not about how gentle it was; a distributed authority structure can produce a harsh penal code, and this one did. Second, and more telling against the "brutal *state* law" framing: the *hudud* belonged to the jurists' *fiqh*, hedged with evidentiary bars so high that "they were rarely applied historically" *in the fiqh courts* — while the genuinely state-run court, the *mazalim*, was "not bound to the rules of Islamic law (fiqh)" and could punish outside those limits. So the raw coercive power you're pointing at often sat in the ruler's *siyasa* jurisdiction, precisely the part that was *least* controlled by the scholars. The honest picture is not "gentle law" — it's a harsh penal doctrine written by decentralized scholars, enforced by a centralized and sometimes more arbitrary state. The card asserts both.

### Counter: "'Independent scholars' is romantic. Court ulama rubber-stamped tyrants, and the Mihna shows the state simply crushed dissenting scholars."

Response:

> Conceded, and in the card (Caveat 5): court *ulama* did legitimize rulers, a quietist obedience strand existed, and during the Mihna (833–c.851) Abbasid caliphs imprisoned and flogged scholars — including Ahmad ibn Hanbal — to force a state theology (that's LB-41, held openly in this archive). The independence claim is not that capture never happened. It's that capture *failed to become permanent*: the Mihna is remembered precisely because the caliphs *lost* — they could not make the state the author of doctrine, and the scholars' resistance became the celebrated precedent. Feldman's point is structural, not sentimental: because the ruler "applied laws as opposed to making them," even a powerful caliph could not simply legislate *fiqh*. An uneven, sometimes-co-opted check is still a different structure from a state that owns the law outright — which is the modern arrangement, not the classical one.

### Counter: "Decentralized just means inconsistent and chaotic — four schools, no appeals court, no uniform rule. That's not resilience, that's the absence of rule of law."

Response:

> There's a real cost conceded here: no single hierarchy of appeals in the modern sense, and forum- and school-selection were genuine features. But "chaotic" mistakes plurality for incoherence. The schools "recognize each other's validity," "share many of their rulings," and differ in bounded, catalogued ways — and the scholarship that carried them ran on a documented provenance system (*isnad* and *ijaza*, LB-17) that made legal knowledge traceable to named chains of transmission. That's coherence-through-network rather than coherence-through-central-command — the same topology that let the four schools map onto entire regions (Maliki in North and West Africa, Hanafi in South and Central Asia, and so on) and persist as those regions passed through different dynasties. Don't overclaim modern uniformity; do notice that a networked legal order is exactly the kind that survives the fall of any one node — which is the resilience the entry is about (and which LB-28 carries the diachronic proof for).

### Counter: "Fine, but today 'sharia' means state penal codes and religious police. Your 'decentralized jurists' law' is gone — so it's just nostalgia."

Response:

> Agreed — and that agreement is the card's sharpest point, not a concession that weakens it. The classical decentralized order was substantially dissolved by modern codification: the Ottoman *Mecelle*, colonial-era legal codes, and above all the nation-state, which pulled the law *into* the state and gave the executive what it never classically had — ownership of the law's content. So when a modern state enforces a "sharia" penal code through official organs, it is doing something structurally *modern* and *centralizing*, much closer to the European codification it borrowed the form from than to the jurists' law it invokes for legitimacy. Feldman reads exactly this re-centralization as what "destroyed" the classical balance and produced today's "unchecked executive dominance." The card is not nostalgia for a golden age — Caveat 7 refuses that — it is a calibration: the thing most people picture when they say "sharia" is a recent state artifact, and the older reality was neither a monolith nor the state's to write.
