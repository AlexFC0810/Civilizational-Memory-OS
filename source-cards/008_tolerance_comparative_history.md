# Source Card 008: Tolerance and Comparative History

Date: 2026-07-10

## Status

**Deployment status:** v2 source-hardened for public use with mandatory caveats. This is a comparative-baseline card, not an "Islam was tolerant" card — it must always ship with all three parts of the claim: (1) dhimma was real structured inequality, (2) same-era Christian-ruled polities often did worse, and (3) Islamic civilization has its own documented atrocities against dhimmis (Granada 1066, Almohad abolition of dhimma) that must not be omitted from the ledger.

**Best use:** respond to two mirror-image smears at once: (1) "Islam is uniquely intolerant / the dhimmi system was just apartheid," and (2) the overcorrection "al-Andalus was a multicultural utopia of tolerance" (romanticized *convivencia*). It is not built to argue "dhimma was always survivable" as an unconditional guarantee — that stronger claim is false and is corrected below.

**House style:** same-era comparative baselines only; concede the real structured inequality of dhimmi status; concede that "Golden Age tolerance" nostalgia is contested scholarship, not settled fact; concede Islamic civilization's own atrocities against dhimmis rather than omitting them; never let a true anchor slide into a triumphalist claim.

## Claim

Classical juristic dhimma frameworks (which varied significantly across Umayyad, Abbasid, Andalusi, and Ottoman practice) codified the jizya-paying non-Muslim "protected" subject as a real, legally structured category of religious inequality by modern standards: dhimmis faced discriminatory taxation, restrictions on dress, arms, and building or repairing houses of worship, inadmissible testimony against Muslims in most courts, and other legal disabilities documented in sources like the Pact of Umar. Enforcement of these restrictions varied significantly by ruler, region, and era — periods of laxity alternated with periods of harsh enforcement, forced conversion, and even massacre (see Honest Caveats).

At the same time, compared to same-era alternatives — Visigothic Spain's 7th-century forced-conversion laws against Jews, Byzantine imperial suppression of "heretics" and pagans in late antiquity, the Crusader massacre of much of Jerusalem's Muslim and Jewish population in 1099, and the 1492 Spanish decree forcing Jews to convert or leave on pain of execution, met by an Ottoman sultan's active rescue of the exiles — the dhimma system more often than not offered non-Muslims a legally guaranteed, taxable, minority status that was survived rather than exterminated, in contrast to the forced-conversion-or-death choices imposed elsewhere in the same centuries. This is a comparative tendency across regimes and centuries, not a property guaranteed by the system or an outcome Islamic rulers always delivered — the Almohad Caliphate's 12th-century abolition of dhimma protection and the 1066 Granada massacre are documented same-civilization exceptions, named honestly below, not omitted.

This does not prove Islamic civilization was "tolerant" in a modern human-rights sense, and it does not prove al-Andalus was a utopia of interfaith harmony. It proves that "Islam was uniquely and exceptionally intolerant compared to its era" is not the same claim as "the dhimma system created real, hierarchical, second-class legal status, sometimes violently enforced" — both can be true, and the second does not entail the first when checked against genuine same-era baselines that include both civilizations' worst episodes, not just one side's.

## Source Anchors

### Pact of Umar — codified dhimmi restrictions

Wikipedia summary (drawing on the classical juristic text):

> "It specifies rights and restrictions for dhimmis, or 'protected persons'... [including] 'We will neither erect in our areas a monastery, church, or a sanctuary for a monk, nor restore any place of worship that needs restoration'... 'We will not imitate their clothing, caps, turbans, sandals, hairstyles, speech, nicknames and title names.'"

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pact_of_Umar

### Dhimmi legal status — protections, disabilities, and variable enforcement

Wikipedia summary:

> "Their testimony against Muslims was inadmissible in courts of law wherein a Muslim could be punished." Dhimmis were "forbidden to carry weapons or ride atop horses and camels" and "were required to wear a yellow badge or other distinctive clothing." They paid the jizya poll tax while Muslims paid zakat, and "dhimmis and Muslims could not inherit from each other." Crucially, the same article states dhimmis were "periodically exposed to forced conversions and massacres," that "instances of forced conversion are well-documented" despite being nominally forbidden, and that "a pattern of stricter, then more lax, enforcement developed over time" — under al-Mutawakkil, "numerous restrictions reinforced the second-class citizen status of dhimmīs and forced their communities into ghettos." The article also notes "most of the restrictions were social and symbolic in nature," and quotes Bernard Lewis's view that the regulations "served to define the relationship between the two communities, and not to oppress the Jewish population" — a characterization in real tension with the ghettoization and massacre material in the same article.

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhimmi

### Jizya — tax burden, exemptions, and comparative variability

Wikipedia summary:

> Jizya was "a type of taxation levied on non-Muslim subjects of a state governed by Islamic law," exempting "women, children, elders, handicapped, the ill, the insane, monks, hermits, slaves" and anyone choosing military service. "Sources comparing taxes levied on Muslims and jizya differ as to their relative burden depending on time, place, specific taxes under consideration, and other factors." Under governor al-Hajjaj's rule in Iraq, jizya was set "sufficiently high and more than taxes on Muslims" that conversions increased specifically to avoid payment — evidence that jizya could function, in some times and places, as fiscal pressure toward conversion rather than a neutral tax.

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jizya

### 1066 Granada massacre — Islamic-civilization atrocity against dhimmis

Wikipedia summary:

> On December 30, 1066, a Muslim mob in Granada killed the Jewish vizier Joseph ibn Naghrela and massacred much of the city's Jewish community; the 1906 Jewish Encyclopedia's figure of "more than 1,500 Jewish families, numbering 4,000 persons" is widely cited but unverified in later scholarship. Historian Bernard Lewis identifies an inflammatory anti-Jewish poem by Abu Ishaq as instrumental in provoking the violence, which followed rumors that Joseph had plotted treason against the Granadan king.

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1066_Granada_massacre

### Almohad Caliphate — abolition of dhimma status, forced emigration

Wikipedia summary:

> "As the Almohads rejected the status of Dhimma, the Almohad conquest of al-Andalus caused the emigration of Andalusi Christians from southern Iberia to the Christian north." "The abolishment of the dhimmi status of religious minorities further stifled the once flourishing Golden age of Jewish culture in Spain; Maimonides went east and many Jews moved to Castillian-controlled Toledo." Initial Almohad rule "drove many Jewish and Christian subjects to take refuge in the growing Christian states of Portugal, Castile, and Aragon."

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almohad_Caliphate

### Visigothic Code — anti-Jewish legislation

Wikipedia summary:

> At the Twelfth Council of Toledo (681 CE), "King Erwig asked that the law code be clarified and revised. Some new laws were added, out of which 28 dealt with Jews." Book XII of the code contains "Title II: Concerning the Eradication of the Errors of all Heretics and Jews" and "Title III: Concerning New Laws against the Jews, in which Old Ones are Confirmed, and New Ones are Added."

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visigothic_Code

### Byzantine Empire — pre-Islamic state suppression of "heretics" and pagans (background baseline, not same-era)

Wikipedia summary:

> Under Justinian I (r. 527-565), the state "reasserted imperial control over religion and morality through purges of pagans, heretics, and other 'deviants.'" Theodosius I (d. 395) earlier "actively condemned paganism, confirmed the primacy of Nicene Orthodoxy over Arianism in the East, and established Christianity as the Roman state religion." Both rulers predate the founding of Islam (7th century) by 200-300 years; this anchor documents the pre-Islamic late-Roman/Byzantine legal culture that both Christendom and the emerging dhimma system inherited or reacted against — it is cited as background, not as a same-era comparator, and should not be presented as a 7th-9th century parallel.

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Empire

### Siege of Jerusalem, 1099 — Crusader massacre, with corroboration limits

Wikipedia summary:

> Eyewitness accounts describe crusaders wading "in blood up to their ankles" at the Temple of Solomon, with "piles of heads, hands, and feet" left in the streets. The Muslim chronicler Ibn al-Qalanisi reported that "the Jews assembled in their synagogue, and the Franks burned it over their heads" — but a contemporary Jewish letter from the Cairo Geniza, written about two weeks after the siege (called "the earliest account on the conquest in any language"), "confirms the destruction of the synagogue, though it does not corroborate that any Jews were inside it when it was burned." Not everyone was killed: some Muslims took refuge in the Tower of David and were granted safe passage out of the city with the Fatimid governor, and Geniza letters document prominent Jews held for ransom by the Crusaders who were freed after the Karaite community in Ascalon paid it. Estimates of the dead range from roughly 3,000 to 70,000; historian Conor Kostick calls the 70,000 figure from Ibn al-Athir (writing c. 1200) "a significant exaggeration," estimating closer to 40,000 given the city's refugee-swollen population.

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Jerusalem_(1099)

### Alhambra Decree, 1492 — expulsion of Spanish Jews

Wikipedia summary:

> The decree was "issued on 31 March 1492" and gave Jews "only four months to either convert to Christianity or leave the country," ordering "the expulsion of unconverted Jews from the Crowns of Castile and Aragon... by 31 July of that year." Jews who neither converted nor left by the deadline faced "penalty of death and confiscation of all property."

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alhambra_Decree

### Bayezid II — Ottoman reception of expelled Sephardic Jews

Wikipedia summary:

> "In July 1492... Bayezid II sent out the Ottoman Navy under the command of admiral Kemal Reis to Spain in 1492 in order to evacuate them safely to Ottoman lands," issuing proclamations welcoming refugees as Ottoman subjects. He reportedly told his courtiers: "You venture to call Ferdinand a wise ruler, he who has impoverished his own country and enriched mine!" — a quote the article footnotes to the 1912 *Jewish Encyclopedia*, i.e. later Ottoman-Jewish historiography rather than a contemporary Ottoman chancery record.

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bayezid_II

### Convivencia — contested scholarship, both sides

Wikipedia summary:

> Yale professor María Rosa Menocal "highlights how Cordoba's libraries were a symbol of its advanced society" (a paraphrase in the article, not a direct Menocal quotation). Against this, David Nirenberg's work "demonstrates that violence was a central and systemic aspect of the coexistence of majority and minority in medieval Spain," and Mark Cohen "calls the idealized interfaith utopia a myth that was first promulgated by Jewish historians such as Heinrich Graetz in the 19th century," arguing both the romantic myth and its "counter-myth" opposite "cannot be maintained in the light of historical reality." Eduardo Manzano Moreno goes further: "the concept of convivencia has no support in the historical record."

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convivencia

### Qur'an 2:256 — "no compulsion in religion" (Blueprint-level anchor only)

Quran.com translation:

> "Let there be no compulsion in religion, for the truth stands out clearly from falsehood. So whoever renounces false gods and believes in Allah has certainly grasped the firmest, unfailing hand-hold."

Source: https://quran.com/2/256

**Note on this anchor:** this is a Blueprint-level anchor only — it documents what the scripture teaches, not evidence of any ruler's or army's practice in any century (see Honest Caveat 5). Do not let it slide into a Builder/Building-level claim that conquest and conversion never overlapped historically.

## Evidence Grade

**A** for the primary/documentary anchors: the Alhambra Decree text and dating, the Siege of Jerusalem 1099 eyewitness and Geniza accounts (including the corroboration gap on the synagogue), the Visigothic Code's Book XII, the 1066 Granada massacre's core facts (date, victim, poem-as-trigger — though the exact death toll is explicitly flagged as unverified even by its own 1906 source), the Almohad abolition of dhimma, and Qur'an 2:256 — these are well-established documentary/textual facts.

**B** for the Pact of Umar as a specific historical document: Bernard Lewis states the document itself "can hardly be authentic," and Mark Cohen dates no surviving text earlier than the 10th-11th century — roughly 300-400 years after Umar I's death, not merely "later copies" of an authentic original but serious doubt about the core document's authenticity as a 7th-century text.

**B** for the Bayezid II quotation (footnoted to an early-20th-century secondary encyclopedia rather than a contemporary chancery record) and for the exact July 1492 dating of the navy's departure (the article's own phrasing is looser than a precise date — "in 1492" — even though it places the decision in the July 1492 expulsion context).

**B/Contested** for the *convivencia* debate itself — by definition this is an active scholarly argument (Menocal vs. Nirenberg/Cohen/Manzano Moreno), not a settled fact in either direction. The card's job is to represent the contest honestly, not resolve it. Note also that the critical-camp quotes (Nirenberg, Cohen, Manzano Moreno) are retrieved via Wikipedia's synthesis of their work, not via direct fetch of *Communities of Violence* or Manzano Moreno's own publications — the same mediation caveat the card already applies to Fernandez-Morera (see Deployment Readiness) applies here too, and no claim in this card should be read as resting on a direct reading of those books.

**B** for the overall comparative-civilizational claim ("dhimma more often than not offered a survivable status compared to same-era alternatives") — each side is well-anchored individually, but the comparison itself is a historian's judgment call about which alternatives are the fairest baseline, and it is now explicitly qualified as a tendency-across-regimes claim, not a universal guarantee, given the Granada 1066 and Almohad counterexamples documented above.

## Best Load-Bearing Question

> **If the dhimma system is proof that Islam is uniquely intolerant, what do we call the same medieval world in which Visigothic Spain wrote forced-conversion law into its legal code (7th century), Crusaders massacred much of Jerusalem's Muslim and Jewish population (1099), and Spain gave its Jews four months to convert or leave on pain of execution (1492)?**

## Alternate Cold-Friendly Question

> **If al-Andalus was a tolerance utopia, why did the same jurists who praised interfaith coexistence also write the legal disabilities in the Pact of Umar, why did Granada's own Jewish community suffer a mob massacre in 1066, and why do serious historians like Nirenberg, Cohen, and Manzano Moreno say the "utopia" framing doesn't survive contact with the record?**

## S-Tier Lines

> **The dhimma system was real, structured religious inequality — enforced with real violence in some eras and regions (Granada 1066, the Almohad abolition of dhimma) — and it was also, more often than not across its long history, a comparatively survivable status next to what Visigothic Spain, Crusader Jerusalem, and 1492 Castile did to religious minorities.**

> **Al-Andalus was neither a utopia nor a myth invented by apologists — it was a hierarchy that, for most of its long run, was safer to be a Jew in than much of Christian Europe in the same centuries, and that, at its worst (Granada, 1066), failed its own Jewish community as violently as anywhere else.**

> **The same year Spain gave its Jews four months to convert or leave on pain of execution, an Ottoman sultan sent his navy to go get them.**

## Best Public Response

> "The dhimma system was real, codified inequality — non-Muslims paid a special tax, faced dress and building restrictions, and their testimony was often inadmissible against a Muslim in court, and enforcement sometimes escalated into forced conversion or massacre, as at Granada in 1066. I'm not going to pretend that's equality, and I'm not going to pretend it was always survivable. But 'uniquely intolerant' has to survive a same-era comparison, and it doesn't: Visigothic Spain wrote 28 new anti-Jewish laws into its legal code in the 7th century, Crusaders massacred much of Jerusalem's Muslims and Jews in 1099, and in 1492 Spain gave its Jews four months to convert or leave on pain of execution — the same year an Ottoman sultan sent his navy to bring those Jews to safety. Neither side of this gets to cherry-pick its own atrocities away. The hierarchy was real, its worst failures were real, and so was the comparative shelter it offered more often than not."

## Short Public Response

> "Dhimmi status was real legal inequality, sometimes violently enforced — not equality. But 'uniquely intolerant' has to beat Visigothic Spain, Crusader Jerusalem, and the 1492 expulsion, and it doesn't."

## Honest Caveats

1. Do not claim dhimmis had equal legal status with Muslims — they did not; jizya, testimony restrictions, dress codes, and inheritance restrictions were real and documented, and enforcement sometimes escalated into forced conversion or ghettoization (e.g., under al-Mutawakkil).
2. Do not claim al-Andalus was a tolerance utopia; Menocal-style *convivencia* framing is contested by serious historians (Nirenberg, Cohen, Manzano Moreno) who argue it understates real violence and structural hierarchy, or has no solid textual basis at all. Their quotes here are mediated through Wikipedia's summary of their work, not a direct fetch of their books — do not present this card as a substitute for reading *Communities of Violence* or Manzano Moreno's own scholarship.
3. Do not treat the Pact of Umar as a single, stable, Umar-authored document. Bernard Lewis states the document "can hardly be authentic," and Mark Cohen finds no surviving text earlier than the 10th-11th century (roughly 300-400 years after Umar I) — treat it as an evolving juristic compilation retroactively attributed to Umar, not a fixed 7th-century treaty text.
4. Do not use the Bayezid II rescue story as proof the Ottomans had no antisemitism or minority hierarchy of their own — the Ottoman Empire ran its own dhimma-style hierarchy and, separately, the devshirme system (forced levy and conversion of Christian boys into imperial service); use the 1492 rescue only as a comparative data point on that specific moment, not as a character reference for Ottoman minority policy generally. The Ferdinand quote itself rests on a 1912 secondary encyclopedia, not a contemporary Ottoman record.
5. Do not use "no compulsion in religion" (Qur'an 2:256) to claim conquest and conversion never overlapped in practice; the verse is a scriptural (Blueprint-level) principle, not a full descriptive history of every ruler's or army's conduct — jizya could function as fiscal pressure toward conversion in some times and places (see the al-Hajjaj example in the Jizya anchor).
6. Do not flatten Byzantine or Visigothic policy into cartoon villainy; both had internal variation across rulers and periods. Note also that the Byzantine anchor (Theodosius, Justinian) predates Islam by 200-300 years and is cited as pre-Islamic background, not a same-era 7th-9th century comparator — do not present it as one.
7. Same-era comparison discipline: never compare the 7th-9th century Islamic dhimma system to 20th-century human rights norms, never compare the Rashidun/early-Umayyad period to 16th-century Ottoman practice as if interchangeable, and never let the Visigothic (7th c.), Crusader (1099), and Alhambra (1492) comparators collapse into one undifferentiated "medieval Christendom" — each is centuries apart from the others and should be named with its own date/century when used together (as done above).
8. Do not omit Islamic civilization's own atrocities against dhimmis from the ledger. The 1066 Granada massacre (an anti-Jewish pogrom that killed the Jewish vizier Joseph ibn Naghrela and, per contested figures, a large portion of Granada's Jewish community) and the 12th-13th century Almohad Caliphate's formal abolition of dhimma status across Iberia and North Africa — which triggered Maimonides' flight east and mass Jewish and Christian emigration to Christian territory — are real, same-civilization exceptions to "dhimma was generally survivable." The comparative claim in this card is a tendency across regimes and centuries, not a claim that zero atrocities occurred under any Islamic dynasty, ever, and it must be presented alongside these episodes, not in their absence.
9. Do not treat "the classical Islamic dhimma system" as one uniform object spanning Umayyad Damascus to 1492 Ottoman practice — Ottoman, Mughal, and Andalusi dhimma law differed significantly by region and era and should not be treated as interchangeable (see Deployment Readiness).

## Unsafe Wording to Avoid

- "Islam was always tolerant of other religions."
- "Al-Andalus proves Islam invented multiculturalism."
- "Dhimmis were treated as equals under Islamic law."
- "Christianity/the West was always more intolerant than Islam."
- "Jews and Christians loved living under jizya."
- "The Ottomans had no antisemitism because they took in Sephardic Jews."
- "Convivencia is a proven historical fact" (or, on the other extreme) "convivencia is a total myth with zero basis."
- "The dhimma system guaranteed survival" or "no massacres happened under Islamic rule" (Granada 1066 and the Almohad abolition of dhimma refute this).
- "Spain gave its Jews four months to convert or die" (the decree's choice was convert or leave; summary execution was the penalty only for those who did neither by the deadline).

## Safe Wording

> Dhimmi status under classical Islamic law was a structured, unequal legal category — protected but subordinate, taxed differently, and legally restricted in specific ways, with enforcement that varied by ruler, region, and era and sometimes escalated into forced conversion or massacre (Granada, 1066; the Almohad abolition of dhimma, 12th-13th century). Compared to several same-era alternatives (Visigothic anti-Jewish law in the 7th century, the Crusader sack of Jerusalem in 1099, and the 1492 Spanish decree forcing Jews to convert or leave on pain of execution), that hierarchy more often than not functioned as a survivable, taxable minority status rather than a forced-conversion-or-death choice — a tendency across regimes and centuries, not a guarantee. The scholarly debate over how harmonious "convivencia" in al-Andalus actually was remains genuinely contested and should be presented as contested, not resolved in either direction.

## Counterattack Simulation

### Counter: "You're just excusing second-class citizenship because it wasn't as bad as a massacre."

Response:

> No — the dhimma system's inequality is conceded, not excused, and so are its own worst episodes (Granada 1066, the Almohad abolition of dhimma). The claim is narrower: "uniquely intolerant" is a comparative claim, and it has to be checked against the actual same-era alternatives, not against a modern human-rights baseline invented centuries later, and not by hiding either side's atrocities.

### Counter: "Convivencia scholars proved al-Andalus was tolerant — you're erasing that."

Response:

> Menocal-style convivencia is one strand of serious scholarship, not the consensus. Nirenberg, Cohen, and Manzano Moreno are also serious historians, and they argue the "utopia" framing overstates the coexistence and understates the violence and hierarchy — including episodes like the 1066 Granada massacre, which happened in al-Andalus itself. The honest position holds both bodies of scholarship in view rather than picking the flattering one.

### Counter: "The Ottomans still had dhimmi restrictions on Jews, and ran the devshirme forced-conversion levy on Christian boys — so the Bayezid story is just propaganda."

Response:

> Correct, and the card says so. The Ottoman Empire ran its own dhimma-style hierarchy and a separate system of forced conscription and conversion. The Bayezid anecdote is not evidence the Ottomans were egalitarian; it's evidence that in that specific 1492 moment, the "protected minority under a hierarchy" option was safer than the "convert or leave on pain of death" option next door. Neither fact cancels the other.

### Counter: "Comparing to Visigothic Spain and the Crusades is cherry-picking the worst of Christendom — what about Granada 1066 and the Almohads, the worst of Islamic rule?"

Response:

> Fair challenge, and this card names both. Granada 1066 and the Almohad abolition of dhimma are real, documented, same-civilization atrocities against dhimmis, listed in the Honest Caveats above, not hidden from it. The comparative claim survives their inclusion: across the centuries and regions covered here, dhimma status was violated violently in specific, nameable episodes rather than as the system's default outcome, while forced-conversion-or-death was the default legal design in Visigothic and 1492 Castilian law. If a fairer, less severe same-era Christian comparator exists for a given decade and region, it should replace the ones used here — but the comparison must stay same-era, and it must not omit either side's atrocities.

### Counter: "This is just a way to avoid criticizing the jizya tax as discriminatory."

Response:

> The jizya was a discriminatory, religion-based tax — that is conceded outright, and in some documented cases (al-Hajjaj's governorship of Iraq) it was set high enough to function as fiscal pressure toward conversion. The comparative point is only that a discriminatory tax with a legal-protection guarantee attached is a different category of harm than forced conversion, expulsion, or massacre as the default legal design — not that the tax itself was fair, or that it never functioned coercively.

## Final Canon

> **Tell the truth: the dhimma system was real, codified, religiously hierarchical inequality — sometimes enforced through forced conversion or massacre, including within Islamic civilization itself (Granada 1066, the Almohad abolition of dhimma) — and "convivencia" is contested scholarship, not a proven utopia. Verbatim fetched excerpt: Jizya was "a type of taxation levied on non-Muslim subjects of a state governed by Islamic law." ### https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1066_Granada_massacre
Retrieval date: 2026-07-10
Verbatim fetched excerpt: The massacre occurred "December 30, 1066 (9 Tevet 4827)." The 1906 Jewish Encyclopedia claimed "More than 1,500 Jewish families, numbering 4,000 persons, fell in one day," though later sources note these figures remain unverified. Jewish vizier Joseph ibn Naghrela "was discovered hiding and killed, with his body subsequently hanged on a cross." Historian Bernard Lewis states an anti-Jewish poem by Abu Ishaq "is said to be instrumental in provoking the anti-Jewish outbreak of that year," following rumors that Joseph had plotted treason against the Granadan king.

### https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Jerusalem_(1099)
Retrieval date: 2026-07-10
Verbatim fetched excerpt: Eyewitness accounts in the *Gesta Francorum* describe crusaders wading "in blood up to their ankles" at the Temple of Solomon; Raymond of Aguilers described "piles of heads, hands, and feet" in the streets. Ibn al-Qalanisi reported "the Jews assembled in their synagogue, and the Franks burned it over their heads," but "a contemporary Jewish communication confirms the destruction of the synagogue, though it does not corroborate that any Jews were inside it when it was burned" — a Cairo Geniza letter written about two weeks after the siege, called "the earliest account on the conquest in any language." Some Muslims "took refuge in the Tower of David" and were granted safe passage out with the Fatimid governor; Geniza letters document ransomed Jewish prisoners freed after Ascalon's Karaite community paid their ransom. Estimates range "3,000~70,000 Muslims and Jews massacred"; historian Conor Kostick calls Ibn al-Athir's 70,000 figure "a significant exaggeration," estimating closer to 40,000 casualties.

### https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alhambra_Decree
Retrieval date: 2026-07-10
Verbatim fetched excerpt: The decree was "issued on 31 March 1492" and gave Jews "only four months to either convert to Christianity or leave the country," ordering "the expulsion of unconverted Jews from the Crowns of Castile and Aragon and its territories and possessions by 31 July of that year." Jews who did neither faced being "forbidden to ever return, under penalty of death and confiscation of all property." Many fled to "the Ottoman Empire, where they were given refuge," with Sultan Bayezid II dispatching "the Ottoman Navy to bring the Jews safely to Ottoman lands, mainly to the cities of Thessaloniki... and İzmir."
