## Anchor 1 — Matthew S. Hopper, *Slaves of One Master* (Yale, 2015) — British Gulf abolition was cosmetic; the trade ended for economic, not humanitarian, reasons

**Full citation:** Matthew S. Hopper, *Slaves of One Master: Globalization and Slavery in Arabia in the Age of Empire* (New Haven: Yale University Press, 2015), 320 pp., ISBN 9780300192018. Finalist, 2016 Frederick Douglass Book Prize. ✅ **VERIFIED** (Yale UP; reviewed in *American Historical Review* 122:1, 277, by Ehud R. Toledano).

**What Hopper actually argues (verified):**
- He **disputes the triumphalist narrative** that the Royal Navy ended the East Africa–Persian Gulf slave trade. He argues Britain *allowed the trade to continue* because enslaved labor was vital to the Gulf economy — and therefore to British strategic and commercial interests in the region.
- Demand for enslaved labor was created by **global commodity markets**: Gulf **pearls** and **dates** feeding the industrializing world economy. Enslavement rose with that boom.
- The trade **collapsed for economic, not moral, reasons**: the rise of cultured **pearls in Japan** and **date production in the U.S. (California)** in the **1920s–1930s** destroyed the Gulf's export economy and with it the demand for slaves.
- Slavery in Arabia therefore **persisted into the twentieth century** (the book covers mid-19th to mid-20th c.).

**SAFE wording (citable):**
> Historian Matthew Hopper (*Slaves of One Master*, Yale UP, 2015) shows that the British "end" of the western Indian Ocean slave trade was far from the heroic Royal-Navy story it's usually told as: Britain tolerated the trade for decades because Gulf slave labor — diving for pearls, tending dates — served the global economy and British interests. What actually ended it was economic collapse: Japanese cultured pearls and American date farming gutted the Gulf's export markets in the 1920s–30s. Abolition followed the money, not the conscience.

**One-line version:**
> Per Hopper (Yale, 2015): the Royal Navy didn't end Gulf slavery — collapsing pearl and date markets did. British abolition there was cosmetic; the trade died when it stopped being profitable.

## Anchor 2 — Robert Harms, *Indian Ocean Slavery in the Age of Abolition* (Yale, 2013) — the East African trade was driven by **economics**, set within an Islamic cultural framework

**Full citation:** Robert Harms, Bernard K. Freamon, and David W. Blight, eds., *Indian Ocean Slavery in the Age of Abolition* (New Haven: Yale University Press, 2013), vii + 253 pp., ISBN 9780300163872. Harms wrote the **Introduction** (pp. 1–20). ✅ **VERIFIED against the primary text.**

### ⚠️ CORRECT THE POST BEFORE DEPLOYING

The post attributes to Harms the line that there was **"little that was Islamic about"** the Islamic slave trade. **That phrase does not appear in Harms's text, and his actual argument is close to the opposite.** Deploying the quote as written would be a fabricated citation — and an overclaim of exactly the kind this engine refuses. Here is what he really wrote:

- The drivers of the western Indian Ocean trade were **commercial and economic** — the "child of the monsoon winds," a dhow-borne commercial system pulled into the 19th-century global capitalist economy. ✅
- **But** Harms explicitly frames Islam as the *cultural and legal connective tissue* of that trading world, not as incidental to it. His verbatim sentence:
  > "The commercial commonalities created by dhows in the western Indian Ocean were part of the cultural commonalities provided mostly by Islam." (Introduction, p. ~5)

  and:
  > "…the dhow traders could find common religious practices and a common framework of Islamic law at most of the ports of the western Indian Ocean."

So the defensible Harms point is: **the trade was economically driven, operating within (not because of) a shared Islamic legal-cultural world.** That is strong and true. "Little that was Islamic about it" is NOT what he said — drop it.

**SAFE wording (citable):**
> Robert Harms, in his introduction to *Indian Ocean Slavery in the Age of Abolition* (Yale UP, 2013), describes the western Indian Ocean slave trade as fundamentally a *commercial* system — "a child of the monsoon winds" drawn into the 19th-century global economy — whose engine was profit and labor demand, not religious doctrine. (Note: Harms also stresses that Islam supplied the shared legal and cultural *framework* of that trading world — so the honest claim is "economically driven," not "nothing Islamic about it.")

**One-line version:**
> Per Harms (Yale, 2013): the East African trade ran on commerce, not creed — driven by economic demand within a shared Islamic legal-cultural world.

## The mandatory caveat (attach to BOTH anchors)

These two anchors **contextualize; they do not exonerate.** They show Western abolition was self-interested and late, and that the trade was economically rather than doctrinally driven — but:

1. They do **NOT** prove "Islam abolished slavery," that Muhammad was an abolitionist, or that abolition came in his lifetime. (The post's claims; refuse them.)
2. They do **NOT** make the East African / Indian Ocean trade any less horrific — Hopper and Harms document a brutal, mass, lethal system that lasted into the 20th century.
3. "Economically driven, not religiously driven" cuts **both ways**: it deflates the smear "Islam is the slave religion," AND it forbids any "Islam wasn't really involved" dodge. Muslim merchants, Muslim states (Oman/Zanzibar), and Islamic law were the framework of the trade. Concede that.
4. The symmetry point — *Western abolition was itself often cosmetic and commerce-driven* — is the load-bearing use of these anchors. They police the Western side of the ledger; they do not clean the Muslim side.

**Load-bearing line:**
> The strongest, truest version isn't "the West freed the slaves and Islam kept them." It's: abolition followed economics on *both* sides of the world — Britain tolerated Gulf slavery while it paid and abandoned it when pearls and dates collapsed; and the East African trade was commerce wearing the shared culture of its time. That's not a defense of anyone. It's the end of everyone's hero story.
