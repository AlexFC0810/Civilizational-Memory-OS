# Source Card 016: The Constitution of Medina (622 CE)

Date: 2026-07-21

## Status

**Deployment status:** reconnaissance-closure draft through the mechanical gate; graded B at the drafting ceiling, pending frontier adjudication (grade-A promotion is not this tier's call — see Evidence Grade for what would justify it, which centers on reading the Arabic primary and Lecker's critical edition directly).

**Best use:** the founding-moment emblem for LB-11 ("plurality was present at the founding, not imported later"). Deploy against "Islam was imposed by force from day one" and "Islam was a conquest religion from the start." It answers that charge with the Prophet Muhammad's ﷺ own first act of statecraft at Medina: not a battle, but a written negotiated pact that bound the town's Muslims and its Jewish clans into one mutual-defense community, each keeping its own religion. Pairs with Card 014 (`014_conquest_and_conversion.md`) — conquest and conversion are not the same thing — and Card 008 (`008_tolerance_comparative_history.md`), which keeps the tolerance claim calibrated across eras rather than romanticized.

**House style:** covenant-not-conquest framing, stated precisely. The load-bearing point is about **day one** — the founding act — and must never be inflated into a claim about all later Islamic governance, a modern liberal constitution, equal citizenship, or a pluralism that endured. The founding coalition with the Jewish tribes broke down within a few years into exile and execution; say that plainly, inside the claim, not in a buried footnote. The honest emblem is "the first Muslim polity began with a contract that assumed a plural town" — not "Islam is inherently pluralist."

## Claim

When the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ established the first self-governing Muslim community at Yathrib (Medina) after the Hijra of 622 CE, its founding act was a negotiated **written pact** — preserved in the Arabic sources as the *Ṣaḥīfa* and known in English as the "Constitution of Medina" — that constituted the settlement's mixed population as a single mutual-defense community (*umma*). Its parties were not religiously or ethnically uniform. Alongside the Muslim Emigrants (Muhajirun) and Helpers (Ansar), the text names Jewish clans as parties — "the Jews of Banu ʿAwf" and several others — and, in the sources that report it, pagan Arabs of Medina as well. The document grants the Jews "their religion" (*dīn*) and their own law and expenses, obliges the parties to mutual defense of Yathrib against any attacker, has the Jews and believers share the costs of joint war, preserves a measure of internal autonomy for each group, and refers disputes for arbitration "to God and to Muhammad." The load-bearing point (LB-11): plurality was present at the founding moment of the first Islamic polity, not imported later — the polity did not require religious or ethnic sameness to begin, and its organizing principle was covenant-above-tribe rather than a single creed or bloodline. Its authenticity is comparatively well regarded: it survives in two early recensions (Ibn Ishaq, transmitted by Ibn Hisham; and Abu Ubayd's *Kitāb al-Amwāl*), and even the tradition's most skeptical modern critics tend to accept that a real founding compact of this kind existed.

This does **not** establish that the pact was a modern constitution or a charter of equal citizenship — serious scholarship (Ovamir Anjum, following Michael Lecker) holds that it "cannot be labeled a constitution," is described in the sources as a *muwādaʿa* (a truce), assigns the two communities **different, not equal**, obligations, and is built around the community of Believers; the Jews were bound into the Medinan unit by a defense pact, not enrolled as equal members of the Muslim *umma* "in theory." It does not establish the later *dhimmi*/*jizya* legal system, which developed afterward — the pact only "foreshadows" it. It is not a photographed treaty: no original survives, the text carries no sound chain of narration (*isnād*), and scholars dispute whether it is one document or a compilation of several agreements from different dates. And it does not establish a durable pluralism: within roughly two to four years the three main Jewish tribes were driven out or killed — the Banu Qaynuqāʿ and Banu Naḍīr exiled, the Banu Qurayẓa executed — after breakdowns and alleged treachery, and many of the pact's clauses were later abrogated by Qurʾānic revelation. That collapse is real history, conceded here in full; it is a claim about what happened *later*, and the charge this card answers is about *day one*.

## Source Anchors

### Wikipedia: transmission, named parties, the operative clauses, early dating, and the later conflict

Wikipedia summary:

> Transmission: "The Constitution is only known from excerpts in early Muslim sources, primarily the Al-Sīrah Al-Nabawiyyah of Ibn Hisham (early 800s CE), a recension of the Al-Sīrah Al-Nabawiyyah of Ibn Ishaq," with "the Kitab al-Amwal of Abu 'Ubayd al-Qasim ibn Sallam" among the other important sources; the text is "transmitted without a chain of narration, a crucial criterion for hadith scholars," and "it does not fulfill any of the conditions of authenticity in the Islamic recording system." Named parties: the Muhajirun (migrants from Mecca), the Ansar (helpers from Medina), the "Jews of Banu ʿAwf," "Banu-an-Najjar," "Banu-al-Harith," "Banu Saʿida," "Banu Jusham," "Banu al-Aus," "Banu Thaʿlaba," and "Pagan Arabs of Medina." Operative clauses: it declares "all citizens as one community"; "the Jews of Banu ʿAwf shall be considered as one community (Ummat) along with the believers—for the Jews their religion, and for the Muslims theirs"; the parties "shall have each other's help in the event of any force invading Yathrib"; "the Jews shall share with the believers the expenses of war so long as they fight in conjunction"; and "whenever you differ about anything, refer it to God and to Muhammad." Dating (Watt): the document "must have been written in the early Medinan period (i.e., in 622 CE or shortly thereafter), because if the document had been drafted any later, then it would have both had a positive attitude towards the Quraysh and given Muhammad a more prominent place." Consensus: "Most historians have accepted the authenticity of the Constitution of Medina." Debate: "scholars do not agree on whether the constitution was a single document or 'a compilation of multiple agreements reached at different times,'" and Serjeant held it consists of "eight distinct documents." Later conflict: the three most famous Jewish tribes are "not among" those named — the Banu Qaynuqa and Banu Nadir "driven into exile" and the Banu Qurayza "massacred and dumped into pits" — "after conspiring and rising up against Muhammad"; the article also records a minority suggestion that "if this constitution really existed, it was probably created after the elimination of the three major Jewish clans."

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Medina

### Ovamir Anjum (Yaqeen Institute): a translation and commentary — and the discipline against over-reading it

Yaqeen Institute summary (Ovamir Anjum, "The 'Constitution' of Medina: Translation, Commentary, and Meaning Today"):

> On the two versions: "The text has been reported in two main versions, that of famous historian of the Sīrah and hadith transmitter Ibn Isḥāq (d. 150/767)... and that of the famous scholar of language and scripture Abū ʿUbayd al-Qāsim ibn Sallām (d. 224/838)," and "The version of the document given by Abū ʿUbayd (AU) is shorter and missing some sections or phrases found in Ibn Isḥāq's (II)." On authenticity: "Its authenticity in its reported form as a single document, however, has been called into question, for we do not possess a single sound chain of authorities (isnād) for it," yet "the fact that numerous sound reports corroborate sections within the document even if the document in its entirety lacks a sound isnād, and the fact that strong evidence exists for one or more such treaties in that era, together make a good case for the Kitāb's general reliability." On content: "the Jews have their religion (dīn) and the Muslims have theirs," and "The Jews bear expenses along with the Believers so long as they continue at war." On the guardrail against over-reading it: "Nor should we see it as a 'step toward constitutionalism,' for that falls into the trap of progressivist historical fallacy or 'trajectory hermeneutics,'" and "this document can be accepted neither as a constitution nor a self-standing font of Islamic political norms outside of the context in which it was written"; "This suggests that at least parts of the document were later abrogated, for the polytheists were explicitly excluded with the declaration recorded in Sūrat al-Tawbah (9:1)."

Source: https://yaqeeninstitute.org/read/paper/the-constitution-of-medina-translation-commentary-and-meaning-today

### Ovamir Anjum, "Conjuring Sovereignty" (Journal of Islamic Law): the peer-reviewed case that it is a genuine pact but not a constitution

Journal of Islamic Law summary (Ovamir Anjum, "Conjuring Sovereignty: How the 'Constitution' of Medina..."):

> Consensus that a compact existed: "That a compact of some sort existed between the Prophet and the Jews of Medina is agreed upon by all scholars, modern and premodern. Most historians and ḥadīth scholars further grant the authenticity of the specific recension of the Ṣaḥīfa as recorded in the works of Ibn Hishām (d. 218/833)—the editor of a longer sīra work by Ibn Isḥāq (d. 150/767)—and Abū ʿUbayd al-Qāsim b. Sallām (d. 224/838)," a confidence "based in strong circumstantial evidence, notwithstanding the weakness in its chain of narration, lack of documentary evidence, and the usual difficulties with textual integrity"; "The text survives only in two sources, Ibn Isḥāq and Abū ʿUbayd." Text: "§1. This is a kitāb (writ, prescript) from Muḥammad the Messenger of Allah between the muʾminūn (Believers) and muslimūn (Muslims) of the Quraysh and Yathrib... and those who join them"; "§2. They are one people (umma) to the exclusion of all other people"; the pact positioned "the Medinan Jews as forming part of a Medinan political unit held together by a common defense treaty, under the authority of God and the Prophet... in a way that foreshadows the dhimma contract." On why it is not a constitution: "this document cannot be labeled a constitution, if by constitution is meant an authoritative document that constitutes a political unit and lists the rights and duties of the ruler and the ruled"; "It is labeled in the sources that report it as a muwādaʿa, a truce"; and "The Jews, in short, were not deemed as part of the Muslim umma in theory and were actively hostile to it in fact." On what came after: "Many of the clauses of this Ṣaḥīfa were abrogated by the later Qurʾānic commands," and "Within two to four years of its writing the three main tribes of the Jews had been sent into exile or executed for violating their deal with the Muslims." The article names Michael Lecker's study as "the most comprehensive record and evaluation of the Western studies," which "meticulously juxtaposes the two extant versions of the texts by Ibn Isḥāq and Abū ʿUbayd."

Source: https://journalofislamiclaw.com/current/article/download/anjum/176?inline=1

## Evidence Grade

**B** — for the claim as scoped in the frontmatter: that the first Muslim polity at Medina (622 CE) was founded by a negotiated written pact constituting the town's mixed population — Muslims, named Jewish clans (with "their religion" and their own law and expenses), and pagan Arabs — as one mutual-defense community with disputes referred to God and to Muhammad, and that plurality was therefore present at the founding moment rather than imported later. Four live fetches across four distinct publishers (Wikipedia; the Yaqeen Institute translation/commentary; the peer-reviewed *Journal of Islamic Law*; and Cambridge Core's *IJMES* front matter) are mutually consistent on the transmission, the named parties, the operative clauses, and the broad scholarly acceptance of authenticity, with the variances recorded below.

Why B and not A — four caps, stated so a frontier pass can adjudicate:

1. **No primary text read.** The document's chain runs through the Arabic *sīra* (Ibn Ishaq via Ibn Hisham) and Abu Ubayd's *Kitāb al-Amwāl*; the definitive modern critical treatment is Michael Lecker's *The 'Constitution of Medina': Muhammad's First Legal Document* (2004). None of these primaries or the critical edition was read directly — I read modern scholarship that quotes and translates the clauses. Grade-A closure would require reading the Arabic text (or a critical edition/translation of it) and Lecker directly.
2. **Fullest treatments paywalled.** Arjomand's *IJMES* article and the Oxford Bibliographies entry were reachable only as abstract/front matter or navigation shell (see Verification Transcript). Per house rule, a fetch that could not be excerpted caps the grade at B.
3. **Genuine interpretive contest, not just provenance.** Even granting authenticity, scholars dispute whether the *Ṣaḥīfa* is a single document or a compilation (Serjeant: "eight distinct documents"), whether it covered all of Medina and all its Jews, and how to read the *umma* it constitutes. A Muslim scholar of the document (Anjum) stresses that the two communities held **differential, not equal**, status. The core proposition — that a real early pact bound non-Muslim parties into the founding community — approaches consensus ("agreed upon by all scholars, modern and premodern"); the fuller interpretive claim is `probable`, not `established`.
4. **Independent-chains honesty (why `independent_chains: 2`).** The two recensions — Ibn Ishaq/Ibn Hisham and Abu Ubayd — are genuinely distinct witnesses (Abu Ubayd's is shorter, omits clauses, and is "in some minor points superior"), and their divergence is itself evidence against late single-hand invention. But both ultimately transmit one document, and neither reaches the bar of an archival original; numerous corroborating hadith support individual clauses without lifting the whole to source-closed.

**Refuter-pass record (2026-07-21, three lenses run in-context):**

- **Primary-source lens** forced two corrections. First, it stripped the popular inflation that "Muslims, Jews, **Christians**, and pagans" were parties: the named parties in the reported text are the Muslims, the Jewish clans, and pagan Arabs — "Christians" is a loose tertiary addition and is not asserted here (see Caveat 4). Second, it forced the claim to quote the document's actual operative clauses (the *umma*, "their religion," mutual defense, arbitration) rather than an idealized paraphrase, and to state plainly that no original survives and no sound *isnād* exists.
- **Hostile-historian lens** (steelmanning "it's a forgery" and "it was abrogated / imposed by force after all") forced the abrogation concession, the exile/execution of the three Jewish tribes within a few years, the *muwādaʿa*/truce characterization, and the minority view that the document may postdate the elimination of the Jewish clans — each surfaced in the claim and caveats, not buried.
- **Category-collapse lens** forced the firewall that gives this card its `claim_layer: prophetic`: it certifies the Prophet's founding **act**, not later dhimmi/jizya law, not the whole history of Muslim–Jewish relations, and not "Islam is inherently pluralist." "Day one was plural" is not "Islam was always plural."

**Dating basis (discussion carried here because closure is `reconnaissance`):** the early dating (622 CE or shortly after) rests on Watt's internal-evidence argument — a later forgery would have flattered the Quraysh and elevated Muhammad — reported uniformly across the fetched sources, and on the document's naming of pagan clans and treatment of the Jews as present parties, features a later idealization would be unlikely to invent. This is internal-evidence dating, not a colophon or a dated manuscript; it is strong for the field but is not primary-source closure.

## Honest Caveats

1. **Do not call it a modern constitution or a charter of equal citizenship.** The strongest scholarship refuses that reading: Anjum (following Lecker) concludes it "cannot be labeled a constitution," notes the sources call it a *muwādaʿa* (truce), and stresses that the Muslims and Jews held **different, not equal**, obligations. The load-bearing point is narrower and survives without the inflation: plurality was present at the founding; the polity did not require religious sameness to begin.
2. **Concede the collapse in full.** Within roughly two to four years, the three main Jewish tribes were exiled (Banu Qaynuqāʿ, Banu Naḍīr) or executed (Banu Qurayẓa) amid war, alleged treaty violations, and treachery. Many clauses of the pact were later abrogated by Qurʾānic revelation, and the polytheists were later excluded (Q 9:1). This is real, hard history; the card concedes it and never minimizes it. What it does not do is let the later collapse rewrite the founding act, which is the only thing the claim certifies.
3. **State the transmission gap plainly.** No original survives. The text reaches us in two literary recensions — Ibn Ishaq (via Ibn Hisham) and Abu Ubayd's *Kitāb al-Amwāl* — and it carries no sound *isnād*, so it "does not fulfill any of the conditions of authenticity in the Islamic recording system." This is a report with a strong scholarly provenance debate, not a photographed treaty. Its comparative credibility comes from internal evidence and corroboration, not from an unbroken chain.
4. **Do not add "Christians" to the parties.** Popular summaries list "Muslims, Jews, Christians, and pagans." The named parties in the reported text are the Muslims, the Jewish clans, and pagan Arabs of Medina. Do not assert Christian signatories; there is no clear textual basis for it in the core document.
5. **Do not assert "the world's first written constitution."** That claim circulates widely and is contested; it also imports the very "constitution" framing serious scholars reject. The card's load does not need it, and it belongs only in the Unsafe-Wording list.
6. **Keep the unity-vs-composite question open.** Scholars dispute whether the *Ṣaḥīfa* is one document or a compilation of several agreements from different dates (Serjeant argued for eight). Some clauses (e.g., a pact with the Banu Qurayẓa) may have been incorporated later. Report the debate; do not present the text as a single sitting's signing.
7. **The dhimma/jizya system came later.** The pact "foreshadows the dhimma contract" but is not it. Do not read the mature legal category of protected non-Muslim subjects back into the founding document.
8. **The Crone quote is cited at second hand.** The revisionist Patricia Crone — among the most skeptical modern critics of the early sources — is widely reported to accept the document as authentic ("a piece of solid rock in an accumulation of rubble"). This card cites that assessment as reported in Arjomand (2009) and corroborated in the secondary literature; Crone's original text was not read directly. Deploy the substance ("even leading skeptics accept it") rather than leaning on the exact quotation as if verified from the source.
9. **Do not over-attribute the achievement to a single idea.** The pact reflects the concrete politics of a specific plural town (feuding Aws and Khazraj, established Jewish clans, Meccan Emigrants needing security) as much as any principle. It is evidence about a founding act, not a proof about the essence of a religion, and none of it turns on collective claims about any people.

## Safe Wording

> After the Hijra of 622 CE, the Prophet Muhammad's ﷺ first act of government at Medina was a negotiated written pact — the *Ṣaḥīfa*, later called the "Constitution of Medina" — that bound the town's mixed population into one mutual-defense community. Its parties were not of one religion or one tribe: alongside the Muslim Emigrants and Helpers, it names Jewish clans as parties, granting them "their religion" and their own law and expenses, and (in the sources that report it) it included pagan Arabs; it obliged the parties to defend Yathrib together, to share the costs of joint war, and to refer disputes "to God and to Muhammad." The point that carries weight is about the founding moment: plurality was there at the start, not added later — the first Muslim polity did not require religious or ethnic sameness to begin. The document survives only in two early recensions (Ibn Ishaq via Ibn Hisham, and Abu Ubayd), carries no sound chain of narration, and scholars debate whether it is one document or several; it is not a modern constitution, not a charter of equal citizenship, and not the later dhimmi/jizya system, which developed afterward. Nor did the founding coalition endure: within a few years the three main Jewish tribes were exiled or executed amid war and alleged treaty-breaking. None of that changes the founding act — and the founding act is the answer to "imposed by force from day one": day one was a contract, not a conquest.

## Counterattack Simulation

### Counter: "The document is a forgery — a pious back-projection with no reliable chain of narration."

Response:

> The card concedes the weakness up front: there is no sound *isnād*, no surviving original, and it "does not fulfill any of the conditions of authenticity in the Islamic recording system." But absence of a hadith-grade chain is not evidence of forgery, and the field's judgment runs the other way. That a founding compact existed "is agreed upon by all scholars, modern and premodern" (Anjum); "most historians have accepted the authenticity" (Wikipedia); and even the revisionist skeptics grant it — Patricia Crone is reported to call it a piece of "solid rock in an accumulation of rubble." Two reasons carry that consensus: the document survives in two textually divergent recensions (Ibn Ishaq and Abu Ubayd), which points to a real early original rather than one late hand; and, as Watt argued, a later forgery would have flattered the Quraysh and given Muhammad a grander role — instead it names pagan clans and treats the Jews as ordinary present parties. A forger idealizing the founding would not write it this way.

### Counter: "It was abrogated and the Jews were expelled and massacred — so it proves Islam was imposed by force after all."

Response:

> Every hard fact in that sentence is in the card, not hidden from it: within about two to four years the Banu Qaynuqāʿ and Banu Naḍīr were exiled and the Banu Qurayẓa executed, and many clauses were later abrogated by Qurʾānic revelation. Concede it fully; do not minimize it. But notice the switch: the charge was "imposed by force **from day one**," and day one is exactly what the founding pact documents — a negotiated written covenant that bound the Jews into the community with their religion protected. What happened over the next several years — breaches, war, alleged treachery, and the violent end of that coalition — is a separate historical question with its own evidence and its own cards. It cannot retroactively convert the founding act into a conquest. Collapsing "the founding covenant" into "everything that happened afterward" is precisely the category error this card is built to refuse.

### Counter: "You're projecting a modern liberal constitution onto a 7th-century tribal truce."

Response:

> Agreed — and the card refuses that projection explicitly, on the authority of the document's own serious students. Anjum, following Lecker, holds that it "cannot be labeled a constitution," that the sources call it a *muwādaʿa* (a truce), that the two communities carried **different, not equal**, obligations, and that the Jews "were not deemed as part of the Muslim umma in theory." The claim here is deliberately narrower than "constitutionalism" or "equal citizenship," and it survives the deflation intact: the first Muslim polity's founding act was a negotiated pact that assumed and bound a plural town — Muslims and Jews in one defensive community — rather than a demand for religious sameness. That is all the claim asserts, and it is exactly what answers "force from day one."

### Counter: "The umma in the pact really meant only the Believers — the Jews weren't truly part of it."

Response:

> On the strongest scholarly reading, that is partly right, and the card grants it: Anjum argues the *umma* on which the Medinan order was built centered the community of Believers, and that the Jews were "not deemed as part of the Muslim umma in theory." But grant that fully and the load-bearing point still stands. The same reading has the pact position "the Medinan Jews as forming part of a Medinan political unit held together by a common defense treaty, under the authority of God and the Prophet." Whether one calls the Jews "members of the *umma*" or "a treaty-bound component of the Medinan polity," they were **parties to the founding covenant with their religion protected**, not conquered subjects. The claim does not need the Jews to be equal citizens of a single *umma*; it needs the founding of the polity to have included non-Muslims as covenant parties — which even the deflationary reading affirms.
