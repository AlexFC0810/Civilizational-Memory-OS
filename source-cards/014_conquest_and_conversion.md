# Source Card 014: Conquest and Conversion — Two Different Timelines

Date: 2026-07-21

## Status

**Deployment status:** reconnaissance-closure draft through the mechanical gate; graded B at the drafting ceiling, pending frontier adjudication (grade-A promotion is not this tier's call — see Evidence Grade for what would justify it).

**Best use:** the defuser for the entire "Islam was spread by the sword" smear family — and, in the same breath, for its over-correction ("it was purely peaceful; power played no role"). Deploy against the ten-second slogan by separating two processes the slogan fuses: the spread of Muslim *rule* (fast, by conquest) and the spread of the Muslim *faith* among conquered populations (slow, over centuries, mostly not at swordpoint). This card is `outcome`-layer: it is about the historical demographic result, not about what Islamic doctrine commands (a separate `quran`/`prophetic`-layer question) and not a defense of any particular conquest's conduct.

**House style:** the two-timelines framing, stated with both halves intact. Never "Islam was never spread by the sword" (false — it sometimes was), and never "the conquests were peaceful" (false — they were wars). The honest, stronger sentence is: *conquest was fast and coercive; conversion was slow and mostly not at swordpoint, but never free of the gravity of power.* Concede the jizya, the dhimmi disabilities, and the documented forced-conversion episodes inside the claim, not in a buried footnote — the corrected picture is more persuasive than the pious one precisely because it carries them.

## Claim

Between roughly 632 and 750 CE — within about a century, much of it in a few decades — Muslim armies brought an enormous territory under Muslim political rule, reaching from the Indus to the Atlantic. The conversion of those conquered populations to Islam was a **separate and far slower process** that unfolded over several centuries and, on the best quantitative reconstruction available, left the conquered core lands still overwhelmingly non-Muslim generations after they were conquered: by Richard Bulliet's conversion-curve estimate, fewer than one in ten inhabitants of Iran, Iraq, Syria, Egypt, Tunisia, and Spain were Muslim at the end of the Umayyad period (c. 750), with the majority-conversion tipping point falling in the 9th–11th centuries. That conversion ran mainly on non-coercive engines — trade, intermarriage, social and economic mobility into the ruling class, Sufi networks, and the slow prestige of the dominant faith — while real coercive pressures also operated: the jizya poll tax on non-Muslims and the disabilities of dhimmi status created a standing incentive to convert, and forced conversions did occur in specific times and places. So the slogan "Islam was spread by the sword" conflates two different things — the rapid spread of Muslim *rule* and the slow spread of the Muslim *faith* — while the opposite claim, that political power played no role in conversion, is equally false.

This does **not** prove the conquests were gentle: they were wars, with battle, tribute, enslavement, and dislocation. It does not prove any conversion was wholly free of power — Muslim political dominance was a background condition of the entire process, the jizya was genuine economic pressure, and episodes of outright forced conversion are documented (the Almohads in the 12th-century Maghreb and al-Andalus, the Fatimid al-Hakim, Safavid Iran's imposition of Shiism — named here from general history, not separately sourced in this card's transcript). It does not adjudicate what Islamic doctrine commands about compulsion; that is a separate scriptural question (Qur'an 2:256, "no compulsion in religion") on a different layer from this demographic one. And it does not claim the pattern is unique to Islam — the same split between conquest and conversion, and the same mix of coercion and diffusion, marks the spread of other religions too.

## Source Anchors

### Wikipedia — Spread of Islam: conquest and conversion on different clocks, and Bulliet's curve

Wikipedia summary:

> "For the subjects of the empire, formerly of the Byzantine and the Sasanian Empires, not much changed in practice... A real Islamization therefore came about only during the subsequent centuries." The core quantitative datum: "At the end of the Umayyad period, less than 10% of the people in Iran, Iraq, Syria, Egypt, Tunisia and Spain were Muslim." On the curve itself: "Richard Bulliet's 'conversion curve' shows a relatively low rate of conversion of non-Arab subjects during the Arab centric Umayyad period of 10%, in contrast with estimates for the more politically-multicultural Abbasid period, which saw the Muslim population grow from around 40% in the mid-9th century, with almost the entire population being converted by the end of the 11th century." On why the conquerors did not force it: "(The Arab conquerors) did not require the conversion as much as the subordination of non-Muslim peoples. At the outset, they were hostile to conversions because new Muslims diluted the economic and status advantages of the Arabs." And on force overall: "conversion by force, while not unknown in Muslim countries, was, in fact, rare. Muslim conquerors ordinarily wished to dominate rather than convert, and most conversions to Islam were voluntary." Mechanisms named include trade ("By sharing a common religion and a common transliteration (Arabic), traders showed greater willingness to trust, and therefore invest, in one another") and pragmatic incentive ("conversions occurred for non-religious reasons of pragmatism and patronage, such as social mobility among the Muslim ruling elite or for relief from taxes").

### Wikipedia — Dhimmi: the protected-subject framework, its real disabilities, and jizya as conversion pressure

Wikipedia summary:

> A dhimmi is a "protected person" — "the state's obligation under sharia to protect the individual's life, property, as well as freedom of religion, in exchange for loyalty to the state and payment of the jizya tax." The disabilities were real and codified: "Dhimmis were subject to specific restrictions as well, which were codified in agreements like the Pact of Umar. These included prohibitions on building new places of worship, repairing existing ones in areas where Muslims lived, teaching children the Quran, and preventing relatives from converting to Islam," and in some formulations "they were required to wear a yellow badge or other distinctive clothing, refrain from carrying weapons, and avoid riding on saddles" — "In a modern sense the dhimmis would be described as second-class citizens." The fiscal pressure toward conversion is stated plainly: "the additional taxation on non-Muslims was a critical factor that drove many dhimmis to leave their religion and accept Islam," and "In some places, for example Egypt, the jizya was a tax incentive for Christians to convert to Islam." Yet the baseline was toleration, not forced conversion: "To begin with, there was no forced conversion, no choice between 'Islam and the Sword'. Islamic law, following a clear Quranic principle (2:256), prohibited any such things [...] although there have been instances of forced conversion in Islamic history, these have been exceptional." This single anchor carries **both** halves this card refuses to drop: the toleration norm and the genuine coercion.

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dhimmi

### Wikipedia — Pact of Umar: the disabilities' charter, and the scholarly doubt about its origin

Wikipedia summary:

> The Pact is presented as "a treaty between the Muslims and non-Muslims who were conquered by Umar during his conquest of the Levant (Syria and Lebanon) in the year 637 CE," specifying "rights and restrictions for dhimmis" — prohibitions on constructing new places of worship, teaching the Qur'an to children, adopting Muslim dress and customs, carrying weapons, and sounding church bells loudly, alongside duties to "respect Muslims" and host them. But its authenticity is seriously questioned: "Bernard Lewis stated the text 'can hardly be authentic,'" and "Mark Cohen noted that 'no text of the document can be dated earlier than the tenth or eleventh century'" — centuries after Umar I. Scholars propose it was "a later invention retroactively associated with Umar... to endow the contract of dhimma with greater normative weight," variously attributed to 9th-century jurists or to the Umayyad caliph Umar II (r. 717–720). This matters both ways: the harshest disabilities may be a later normative back-projection rather than original conquest policy — and yet they did become the codified norm the earlier anchor describes.

Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pact_of_Umar

The archipelago was Islamized commercially and culturally, without a large-scale Muslim military conquest — the single sharpest empirical anvil against "spread by the sword."

## Evidence Grade

## Best Load-Bearing Question

> If Islam was "spread by the sword," why were fewer than one in ten of the conquered — in Iran, Iraq, Syria, Egypt, Tunisia, and Spain — Muslim a full century after the armies arrived? And why is the land with the largest Muslim population on earth today, Indonesia, one that no Muslim army ever conquered — reached by merchants and mystics, not cavalry? But if political power played no part, why did conversion track the jizya, the cities, and access to the ruling class — and why did it come, in some times and places, at the point of a sword after all?

## Honest Caveats

1. **The conquests were violent.** This card separates conquest from conversion; it does not sanitize the conquest. The early expansion was a series of wars — battle, siege, tribute, enslavement, and the dislocation of populations. "Not spread by the sword" refers to the *conversion of populations*, never to the manner of the political conquest, which was frequently brutal.
2. **The jizya was genuine economic coercion.** Concede it plainly: the fetched Dhimmi anchor states the tax on non-Muslims "was a critical factor that drove many dhimmis to leave their religion" and, in Egypt, "a tax incentive for Christians to convert." Slow, structural, fiscal pressure is still pressure. "Voluntary" conversion under a dominant faith that taxed unbelief is not the free choice of a neutral marketplace.
3. **Dhimmi disabilities were real second-class status.** Restrictions on building and repairing places of worship, on dress, on weapons, on riding — codified and, where enforced, humiliating. Do not launder them. Note the two-edged authenticity point: Lewis and Cohen judge the "Pact of Umar" text itself a later composition ("no text... earlier than the tenth or eleventh century"), so the harshest formulae may post-date the conquests — but they became the normative code all the same.
4. **Forced conversions occurred.** The baseline was toleration for a fiscal purpose, and the sources call forced conversion "exceptional" — but exceptions are not nothing. The Almohads (12th-century Maghreb and al-Andalus; Maimonides fled them), the Fatimid al-Hakim, and Safavid Iran's imposition of Shiism are standard examples (named from general history, not separately sourced in this card's transcript). A defense that denied these would be false and would deserve to lose.
5. **Bulliet's numbers are one method with real limits.** The conversion curve proxies conversion through the appearance of Muslim names, over-samples the literate urban elite, and sees rural conversion poorly. Deploy the *shape* (slow, centuries-long, S-curved) as robust; deploy the *exact percentages* as "a leading reconstruction," never as a census.
6. **Power was a background condition throughout.** Do not over-rotate into "purely voluntary." Conversion clustered in cities, near administrative centers, and among those with the most to gain from joining the ruling class. Political dominance was the water everyone swam in, even where no sword touched an individual. "Power played no role" is the mirror-image error to "spread by the sword," and this card refuses both.
7. **Regional variation is enormous — do not universalize one region.** Indonesia (trade, no conquest) and India (frontier, heartland converted least) are the doves; Anatolia is the hawk. There, Speros Vryonis's *Decline of Medieval Hellenism in Asia Minor* (identified via search, not fetched) argues Islamization followed Turkmen migration, conquest-driven dislocation, and dervish orders endowed on "expropriated possessions of the church" over the 11th–15th centuries — though more recent scholarship has challenged Vryonis's "decline and destruction" paradigm. Even the hawkish case is centuries-long and institution-driven, not instant swordpoint — but it is the honest counterweight to the Indonesia anvil.
8. **"People of the Book" was the easy case; polytheists were the hard one.** Classical jurisprudence extended the dhimma (retain your religion, pay the tax) most clearly to Jews and Christians, and generally to Zoroastrians; strict theory was harsher toward pure polytheists. In practice fiscal logic usually won — Hindus in India were treated as de facto dhimmis because the tax base mattered — but the toleration was not uniform across every group, and Zoroastrianism in particular declined sharply under sustained pressure.
9. **Keep the category firewall up.** Three distinct propositions live here: the spread of *rule* (conquest), the spread of *faith* among populations (conversion), and what the *religion teaches* about compulsion (doctrine). The smear collapses the first into the second and often into the third. Equally, "a dynasty forced conversions" (an institution/`fiqh`-layer fact about specific rulers, some of them condemned by contemporary jurists) is not "the conquest forced conversion everywhere." Keep the layers apart in both directions.
10. **Comparative symmetry is a check, not an excuse.** The "spread by the sword" standard, applied evenly, indicts many expansions — Charlemagne's forced conversion of the Saxons, forced baptisms in Reconquista Spain, the Northern Crusades (general history, not sourced here). Use this only to expose *selective* application of the standard; never as "they did it too, so it was fine." The point is that the slogan, run in reverse, fails as history — not that coercion is excused wherever it appears.
11. **Not read directly — closure is reconnaissance.** Bulliet, Eaton, and Vryonis are identified but not read in the original; no primary attestation of the conquest-era tax regime was fetched; one load-bearing India anchor is a blog reproduction. The claim is strong and the sources converge, but the provenance chain has not been walked to primary/edition level. Say "on the best available reconstruction," not "the record proves."

## Unsafe Wording to Avoid

- "Islam was never spread by the sword." (False. Muslim *rule* was, and forced conversions occurred in specific times and places.)
- "The conquests were peaceful." (False — they were wars, with enslavement and dislocation.)
- "Conversion was always completely voluntary." (Erases the jizya, the dhimmi disabilities, and documented forced conversions.)
- "Islam invented religious tolerance." (Overclaim; the dhimma was a specific, disability-bearing arrangement, not modern pluralism, and toleration has many independent histories.)
- "Only Islam spread peacefully" / "no other religion used the sword." (Comparative overclaim that inverts the very error being corrected.)
- "The jizya proves Islam is inherently coercive." (The hostile mirror-overclaim: a graduated tax with a documented toleration rationale is not proof of an essence.)
- "Everyone converted at swordpoint" / "nobody was ever forced to convert." (The two brittle absolutes this card exists to break — one is the smear, the other is the over-correction.)

## Safe Wording

> "Islam was spread by the sword" is half a truth wearing the other half's clothes. Muslim political rule did spread fast and by conquest — a vast empire from the Indus to the Atlantic within about a century. But the conversion of those conquered peoples to Islam was a different, much slower story: by the leading quantitative reconstruction, fewer than one in ten people in the conquered core lands were Muslim even a century on, and most regions did not become majority-Muslim until the 9th–11th centuries or later. That conversion moved mainly through trade, intermarriage, Sufi networks, and the social and economic pull of joining the ruling faith — not through forced conversion, which the sources treat as the exception, not the rule. Indonesia, home to the largest Muslim population on earth, was reached by merchants, not armies. Yet the opposite slogan — that power played no part — is just as wrong: non-Muslims paid a special tax (the jizya), lived under real legal disabilities, and in specific times and places were converted by force. The accurate sentence is the unglamorous one: conquest was fast and coercive; conversion was slow and mostly not at swordpoint, but never free of the gravity of power.

## Counterattack Simulation

### Counter: "Under 10% converted early only because the conquest was still settling — give it time and the sword did the work."

Response:

> Time is exactly the point, and it defeats the slogan rather than saving it. "Spread by the sword" is a claim about *mechanism* — that people became Muslim because a blade was at their throat. But the fetched record shows conquest completed in decades while conversion kept climbing for three to five *centuries*, cresting in the 9th–11th on Bulliet's curve, long after the fighting in a given region had ended and the swords had gone home. A mechanism that acts 200 years after the army leaves is not "the sword"; it is tax structure, intermarriage, urban opportunity, and generational drift. Concede what is true in the counter — the conquest did create the *conditions* (a Muslim ruling class, fiscal incentives, prestige) under which slow conversion later happened. That is precisely the "power played a role" half this card keeps. It is not the "converted at swordpoint" half, which the timeline refutes.

### Counter: "The jizya WAS the sword — a tax engineered to squeeze people into converting. Coercion by another name."

Response:

> This is the strongest counter, and the card concedes its core: the jizya was real pressure, and the fetched Dhimmi source says so outright — the tax "drove many dhimmis to leave their religion," and in Egypt functioned as "a tax incentive for Christians to convert." If your claim is "economic coercion operated," you are right and we agree. But notice three things the "tax = sword" equation has to suppress. First, direction of incentive at the top: the same sources record that the early conquest state was *hostile* to conversion because converts "diluted the economic and status advantages of the Arabs" — a regime optimizing for forced conversion does not tax you *instead* of converting you and then resent it when you convert. Second, magnitude and time: a graduated poll tax that most non-Muslims paid for centuries while remaining non-Muslim is pressure, not a sword — millions chose the tax over conversion, generation after generation, which a literal sword does not permit. Third, precision: "coercion by another name" is true; "the sword by another name" is not — collapsing a slow fiscal gradient into an execution choice is the exact conflation this card exists to stop. Coercion: conceded. Sword: declined.

### Counter: "Indonesia and Bengal are cherry-picked peripheries. In the heartlands it was conquest, forced conversion, and dhimmi humiliation."

Response:

> Two answers, one conceding, one turning the point around. Conceding: the heartlands are where the coercion was realest — the jizya, the codified disabilities, the documented forced-conversion episodes — and this card names them without flinching. But the geography does not rescue the sword thesis; it inverts it. Eaton's finding for India is that Islamization ran *inversely* to Muslim political power: the upper Gangetic heartland, most exposed to the sword, reached only 10–15% Muslim, while the frontiers where the sword was weakest — eastern Bengal, western Punjab — reached 70–90%. If the sword converted people, the map would run the other way. As for "cherry-picking the periphery": Indonesia is not a footnote periphery, it is the single largest Muslim population on the planet, and it was never conquered by a Muslim army at all. You cannot call the biggest case the exception. The honest picture is variation — hawkish Anatolia, dovish Indonesia, inverted India — and *every* region takes centuries. None of them is the ten-second slogan.

### Counter: "This is apologetics — laundering centuries of violent conquest into a tale of merchants and mystics."

Response:

> Then hold me to the audit, because I have not laundered the conquest — I have said, in the claim itself, that it was war: battle, tribute, enslavement, dislocation.
