## Claim

Collective guilt — holding 1.9 billion Muslims morally responsible for the acts of a small number of jihadist terrorist organizations — is (1) morally incoherent by a standard that is rejected across most ethical and legal traditions: collective *moral guilt of every individual adherent* for a movement's crimes is not how responsibility is assigned. Where collective responsibility *is* seriously debated for other cases — German responsibility after 1945 (Jaspers' *Die Schuldfrage*), Serbian nationalism and Srebrenica, Afrikaner responsibility under apartheid, Hindutva-linked pogroms, Buddhist-nationalist violence in Myanmar — the live argument is about national-political-institutional responsibility, not per-individual moral guilt of every co-national or co-religionist, which is the specific attribution this card refuses. It is also (2) in tension with core Islamic legal/theological texts on individual accountability before God (Qur'an 6:164, 17:15: "no soul bears another's burden") and the value of a single life (5:32) — a principle this card does not claim Islamic law applies without exception (classical homicide law's 'aqila collective-liability doctrine is a real internal counter-institution; see Honest Caveat 11 and the Counterattack Simulation). And it is (3) in tension with the empirical record, in which the countries and populations hit hardest by jihadist terrorism are themselves majority-Muslim, and in which the specific jihadist-victim literature (encyclopedic-tier, capped at B here) finds most victims of jihadist attacks are themselves Muslim — a point that rebuts the empirical sub-claim that jihadism is a war *on* non-Muslims or that Muslims support/benefit from it, but does not by itself discharge the alleged moral responsibility, which grounds (1) and (2) instead.

This does not prove that no terrorist ever draws on Islamic language, law-language, or scripture — some explicitly do, and the verses they actually reach for most (9:5, 9:29 — the so-called "sword" and jizya verses) are real, are quoted below, and are not dodged here. It does not prove that jihadist ideology has zero connection to real, historically mainstream debates within Islamic legal history over apostasy, dhimmi status, and offensive jihad (jihad al-talab) — those were majority juristic positions for centuries in the classical schools, not fringe distortions invented from nothing; see Honest Caveat 2. It does not prove that "6:164/17:15 settles the question of intra-Muslim excommunication (takfir) or ends the debate over 9:5/9:29's scope" — those are separate arguments made explicitly below, not smuggled in under the individual-accountability verses. What this claim proves is narrower: the specific move from "some terrorists cite Islamic texts, including real, load-bearing ones" to "Muslims collectively are guilty / Islam-as-such is terrorism" is a category error the sources' own individual-accountability principle argues against, and an empirical claim in tension with who actually dies in these attacks — while conceding that country-level victim data and individual-victim-identity data are not the same measurement, a distinction this card does not blur.

## Source Anchors

### Qur'an 5:32 — one soul weighed as all of humanity, with the "corruption in the land" exception named honestly

Quran.com (Dr. Mustafa Khattab, the default translation displayed on the live page — verified this session; an earlier draft of this card mislabeled this quote as Sahih International, which uses different wording ("Because of that, We decreed...") and has been corrected):

> "That is why We ordained for the Children of Israel that whoever takes a life—unless as a punishment for murder or mischief in the land—it will be as if they killed all of humanity; and whoever saves a life, it will be as if they saved all of humanity."

Note honestly: the verse opens "We ordained for the Children of Israel" — it states a principle given to the Israelites, not a direct command to Muslims. The Islamic argument for its force on Muslims runs through 6:164/17:15 restating individual accountability directly for the Muslim community, and through the verse's placement/reception in the Islamic tradition as a stated universal moral principle — not through 5:32 alone. The verse is immediately followed by 5:33, which prescribes hirabah (violent-crime-against-society) punishments — execution, crucifixion, or amputation — for "waging war against Allah and His Messenger," which Ibn Kathir's tafsir on 5:33 glosses as including "disbelief, blocking roads and spreading fear in the fairways." This is a real continuation a hostile reader will raise; see Counterattack Simulation.

Source: https://quran.com/5/32

### Ibn Kathir's tafsir on 5:33-34 — the exception clause, honestly quoted in full, with correct attribution

Quran.com tafsir (verified this session; an earlier draft of this card misattributed this commentary to verse 5:32 itself — it is Ibn Kathir's commentary on 5:33-34, the two verses immediately following, and the earlier draft also quoted only the narrowing half of his gloss):

> On what "wage war" covers: "'Wage war' mentioned here means, oppose and contradict, and it includes disbelief, blocking roads and spreading fear in the fairways." On the available punishments: "the Muslim Leader has the choice to either have him killed, crucified, or cut off his hands and feet." On repentance (attached to 5:34, the verse after): "This Ayah is clear in its indication that it applies to the idolators. As for the Muslims who commit this crime and repent before they are apprehended, the punishment of killing, crucifixion and cutting the limbs will be waived."

Quoted in full rather than only the mercy half: Ibn Kathir's gloss is genuinely broader than "bounded, mercy-pointing" — it explicitly includes disbelief itself among what counts as "waging war," which is exactly the kind of clause hostile critics and jihadist exegetes alike seize on. The honest claim is narrower: even on this broad reading, the passage (a) frames this as a state/judicial punishment for a defined crime, not private license, and (b) explicitly waives the penalty for repentance before capture — a bounded, adjudicated framework, not blanket war on unbelief. That is a real argument, not a settled one; a hostile reader is entitled to find the "includes disbelief" clause troubling, and this card does not pretend otherwise.

Source: https://quran.com/5/32/tafsirs/en-tafisr-ibn-kathir

### Qur'an 6:164 — no soul bears another's burden

Quran.com translation (verified this session):

> "No soul burdened with sin will bear the burden of another." The verse continues that "no one will reap except what they sow," and all return to God for judgment on what they differed about.

Scope-limiter: this is a statement of individual accountability before God at final judgment — an eschatological/theological claim. It is directly relevant to "does shared religious membership transfer guilt for a co-religionist's acts," but it is not itself a claim about modern civic or political blame-attribution, and it does not by itself settle the separate question of in-group social/reputational responsibility. Both points are argued together below, not conflated.

Source: https://quran.com/6/164

### Qur'an 9:5 — the verse jihadist groups actually cite most often, quoted directly rather than avoided

Quran.com translation (verified this session):

> "But once the Sacred Months have passed, kill the polytheists ˹who violated their treaties˺ wherever you find them, capture them, besiege them, and lie in wait for them on every way. But if they repent, perform prayers, and pay alms-tax, then set them free. Indeed, Allah is All-Forgiving, Most Merciful."

Honest framing: this verse (nicknamed "ayat al-sayf," the sword verse) is real, is quoted by jihadist groups far more often than 5:32, and classical tafsir situates it within the specific historical campaign following the breach of treaties by particular Meccan-era polytheist groups (the "Sacred Months" timeline refers to a four-month grace period given to treaty-violators). Some later exegetes debated whether it functions as a general rule or a bounded, occasion-specific ruling (the naskh/abrogation question); this card does not adjudicate that classical debate here — it is flagged as contested and unresolved, not resolved in Islam's favor by assertion. What this card does argue: even granting the verse at its most expansive historical reading, it explicitly offers exit through repentance/treaty-compliance, and it does not by itself establish that modern jihadist groups' application of it (against civilians, aid workers, fellow Muslims) tracks the classical juristic scope of the verse — mainstream classical fiqh developed extensive limiting conditions (combatant status, treaty context, proportionality) that most jihadist groups explicitly reject in practice (see the rules-of-war comparative case for the fuller doctrinal-vs-practice treatment).

Source: https://quran.com/9/5

### The Amman Message (2004) — a cross-school anti-takfir consensus, narrowly and accurately scoped

Ammanmessage.com (verified this session; an earlier draft of this card overstated this document's scope and endorser list — corrected below):

> Released on the eve of 27 Ramadan 1425 AH / 9 November 2004 CE by King Abdullah II of Jordan and subsequently endorsed by over 500 Muslim scholars across the recognized schools of law. Its Second Point explicitly "forbade takfir (declarations of apostasy) between Muslims" who accept one of the eight recognized schools. The Three Points were adopted by the Organization of the Islamic Conference (Mecca, December 2005) and, over the following year (July 2005–July 2006), by six further international Islamic scholarly assemblies, "culminating with the International Islamic Fiqh Academy of Jeddah, in July 2006." The document names "legitimate jihad" as one of several balanced Islamic concepts it upholds, but does not elaborate a position on jihad against non-Muslims — its substantive ruling is the anti-takfir point, not a general anti-terrorism doctrine.

Corrected endorser/recipient distinction: the site names Shaykh Al-Azhar, Ayatollah Sistani, and Sheikh Qaradawi as three of the 24 senior scholars to whom King Abdullah II originally sent the three founding questions whose answers became the basis of the Amman Message — not explicitly as endorsers of the later 500-scholar consensus statement itself, though the site's framing treats the 24 as part of the broader circle of scholarly support. This card does not claim al-Qaradawi (who separately issued fatwas endorsing suicide attacks against Israeli civilians in specific political contexts) endorses this card's application of the Amman Message against jihadist terrorism generally — the load-bearing point is narrower: a cross-school consensus exists against Muslim-on-Muslim takfir specifically, which is relevant because jihadist groups typically must first declare their Muslim targets (Shia populations, "apostate" rulers, insufficiently strict Sunnis) outside the fold before licensing violence against them. The Amman Message constrains that move in doctrine; it does not by itself dissolve it, because jihadist takfir arguments typically proceed by first arguing their targets fall outside the "recognized schools" boundary the Message itself relies on. It is also not a ruling on jihad against non-Muslims, on classical apostasy law, or on dhimmi subordination — see Honest Caveat 2.

Source: https://ammanmessage.com/

## Honest Caveats

1. **Do not deny that some terrorists cite Islamic texts, symbols, or legal language — including hard verses, not just soft ones.** Groups like Al-Qaeda and ISIS explicitly frame their violence in religious terms, cite 9:5 and 9:29 far more often than 5:32, and recruit within Muslim communities. Denying this, or quietly routing around the verses jihadists actually use, is dishonest and will be caught immediately by any informed critic.
2. **Do not claim jihadist ideology has "nothing to do with" Islamic history, or that its textual/legal anchors are a fringe invention.** Classical Sunni fiqh across all four major schools held, as a mainstream (not marginal) position for centuries, that apostasy (irtidad) could carry the death penalty, that non-Muslim subjects (dhimmis) owed jizya under a subordinationist framework, and that offensive jihad (jihad al-talab) was a legitimate expansionary doctrine. Jihadist groups exploit real, historically mainstream fault lines in classical law — they are not simply inventing from nothing, and this card does not claim they are. The honest claim is that modern mainstream scholarly consensus (Amman Message and others) represents a genuine, contested reform and reinterpretation of that inheritance — not that the older positions were always fringe, and not that the reform is uncontested or complete.
3. **Do not claim the Qur'an is pacifist or that 2:190 forbids fighting; do not claim 9:5/9:29 are somehow not "really" in the Qur'an or are marginal.** 2:190 bounds fighting rather than prohibiting it. 9:5 and 9:29 are real, are the verses jihadist groups actually reach for most, and this card quotes them directly rather than routing around them for the softer verses. The naskh/abrogation debate over their scope is genuine and unresolved in classical scholarship; this card flags that debate rather than claiming victory in it.
4. **Do not present the "80-90% Muslim victims" figure, or any single GTI percentage, as a precisely graded, single settled statistic.** The 80-90% figure is sourced through an encyclopedic tier here (Wikipedia, citing but not directly quoting named studies); treat it as directionally strong but pursue the underlying peer-reviewed studies before using it in a context where a single figure will be forensically challenged. Separately, GTI percentages (87%, 72%) come from different report-years and should never be quoted as if they describe one consistent, unchanging statistic — cite the specific year attached to each figure.
5. **Do not conflate "majority-Muslim country" with "individual Muslim victim."** GTI country rankings measure where attacks occur, not the religious identity of individual victims, and GTI totals include non-jihadist violence (ethnic-militia and separatist conflict, especially across the Sahel). Nigeria, on the "hardest hit" list, includes very large numbers of Christian victims of Boko Haram specifically; do not deploy a country list as if it were victim-religion data. The only anchor in this card that speaks to individual victim religious identity is the B-graded 80-90% figure, and it should be flagged as such wherever used, not left to travel silently under the country-level statistics.
6. **Do not present the Amman Message as a general anti-terrorism consensus or as proof that every signatory (or fatwa-question recipient) rejects all religiously framed violence.** Its substantive ruling is a cross-school prohibition on takfir between Muslims — relevant because jihadist groups typically must first declare their targets outside the fold before licensing violence against them, but this constrains rather than dissolves that move, since jihadist takfir arguments proceed by disputing the "recognized schools" boundary itself. The document does not rule on jihad against non-Muslims, classical apostasy law, or dhimmi subordination, and individual scholars in its orbit (e.g., Sheikh Qaradawi, among the 24 original fatwa-question recipients) have separately taken positions on other conflicts (including endorsing suicide attacks against Israeli civilians in specific political contexts) that this card does not vouch for.
7. **Do not use this card to dismiss legitimate security or foreign-policy discussion.** The claim here is about collective moral/religious guilt, not about whether terrorism, radicalization pathways, or specific organizations deserve serious study and countermeasures. They do.
8. **Do not extend "Muslims are disproportionately victims" into "therefore no Muslim community has a radicalization problem," and do not treat sectarian jihadist violence as somehow non-religious just because most of its victims are Muslim.** A large share of jihadist-caused Muslim deaths are intra-Sunni or Sunni-on-Shia violence coded in explicitly theological categories (takfir, "rafidha," "murtadd"). That victim data disproves collective guilt of 1.9 billion individual Muslims; it does not disprove that this violence draws on internal Islamic theological/legal categories — the opposite is closer to true, and this card does not claim otherwise.
9. **Do not claim the Islamic historical record is free of collective punishment.** The sira and classical practice contain collective-punishment episodes (e.g., the execution of the adult men of Banu Qurayza after the Battle of the Trench, reported in Ibn Ishaq's biography and canonical hadith collections) and classical law accommodated group-status categories (dhimmi communal status, tribal collective-retaliation norms) in tension with a pure individual-accountability reading. This card's claim is about the moral status of 1.9 billion living individuals today, not a claim that Islamic history as practiced never involved collective punishment — those are different claims, and only the first is made here.
10. **Do not claim survey data is uniform.** Some polling in some Muslim-majority populations finds non-trivial minority support for positions like death for apostasy or, in some countries and years, sympathy for suicide bombing in specific circumstances; this varies enormously by country and cannot be waved away. It still does not ground collective guilt of all adherents — distributions of opinion within a population of 1.9 billion are not the same claim as guilt of the whole population, but this card does not pretend the distributions don't exist.
11. **Do not claim Islamic law contains no collective-liability institution — it does: 'aqila.** Classical Islamic homicide law assigns diya (blood-money) collectively to the offender's 'aqila (the solidarity/kin group), with qasama as a related collective-oath procedure. This is a mainstream, cross-school institution discussed in every classical furu' manual, not a tribal survival — and an informed critic will raise it against the card's "Islam's own individual-accountability principle" framing (6:164/17:15), so name it rather than leave its absence reading as ignorance or concealment. The distinction the card actually needs, and makes: 'aqila is *civil/compensatory* liability for *accidental* (unintentional) homicide, apportioned to a *defined, mutually-obligated, consenting* solidarity group under adjudicated rules — whereas for *intentional* murder classical law imposes qisas on the *individual* killer and the 'aqila does *not* cover it. That is categorically different from *moral guilt for intentional atrocity* imputed to 1.9 billion strangers who share only a confessional label. 6:164/17:15 remain in real tension with any *guilt*-transfer across a whole population; 'aqila is a *financial* liability rule for accidents within a bounded group, and classical law's treatment of intentional killing (individual execution of the murderer) actually reinforces, rather than undercuts, the individual-accountability point this card leans on.

## Unsafe Wording to Avoid

- "Terrorism has nothing to do with Islam." (False if the actor explicitly invokes Islamic texts, including real hard verses like 9:5; concede the true part instead.)
- "No real Muslim would ever commit terrorism." (No-true-Scotsman; unfalsifiable and dishonest.)
- "All terrorism is Western-caused / purely political, never religious." (Too simplistic; erases the ideological dimension.)
- "The Qur'an forbids all killing." (False; 5:32, 2:190, 9:5, and 9:29 all retain exceptions/permissions.)
- "Islam is the only religion that condemns collective guilt." (False; most ethical traditions reject collective guilt in some form — this is not an exclusivity claim.)
- "80-90% of all terrorism victims worldwide are Muslim" stated as a settled precise statistic without the encyclopedic-tier caveat.
- "The countries hit hardest by terrorism prove Muslims are the primary victims" — conflates country-level attack concentration with individual victim religious identity; do not use interchangeably.
- "The Amman Message proves mainstream Islam rejects jihadist violence generally" — its ruling is specifically anti-takfir; do not extend it to a blanket anti-terrorism consensus.

## Safe Wording

> Collective guilt — holding all Muslims morally responsible for jihadist terrorism — sits badly with both moral logic (collective moral guilt of every individual adherent for a movement's crimes is rejected across most ethical and legal traditions; where collective responsibility is debated at all — post-1945 Germany, Srebrenica, Myanmar — it is argued as national-political-institutional responsibility, not per-individual guilt of every co-religionist) and the Qur'an's own individual-accountability principle (no soul bears another's burden, stated in multiple places — with the honest note that classical fiqh's 'aqila doctrine is a real collective *financial* liability for *accidental* homicide, distinct from moral guilt for intentional murder, for which classical law executes the individual killer). Mainstream scholarly consensus also explicitly rejects the specific takfir logic terrorist groups depend on to justify killing fellow Muslims. It also sits badly with the empirical record: the populations and countries hit hardest by jihadist terrorism are themselves majority-Muslim, and by some (B-graded, not independently re-verified) studies, 80-90% of jihadist terrorism's individual victims are Muslim — a fact that rebuts the empirical sub-claim that jihadism is a war on non-Muslims or that Muslims benefit from it, while not by itself discharging the moral-responsibility question, which the first two grounds address. This does not mean terrorists never invoke Islamic language — some invoke real, hard verses (9:5, 9:29), not just soft ones — or that jihadist ideology has no connection to real, historically mainstream Islamic legal debates over apostasy, dhimmi status, and offensive jihad; it means the leap from "some organizations cite these texts and this history" to "the religion or its 1.9 billion adherents collectively are guilty" is not supportable, on either moral or evidentiary grounds.

## Counterattack Simulation

### Counter: "But terrorists literally quote the Quran when they attack people — and not just soft verses, they quote 9:5, the actual sword verse."

Response:

> True, and I'm not going to pretend otherwise — 9:5 and 9:29 are real, and jihadist groups reach for them far more often than 5:32. Classical scholarship has a genuine, long-running debate about how far those verses' scope extends and whether they're read as bounded to their historical occasion or as standing rulings — I'm not going to claim that debate is settled in one direction. What I'll say is narrower: even granting the verse its widest classical reading, it explicitly offers an exit through repentance and treaty compliance, and mainstream classical fiqh developed extensive limiting conditions — combatant status, treaty context, proportionality — that most jihadist groups explicitly reject in practice, including by killing civilians, aid workers, and fellow Muslims their own classical tradition would not sanction. Citing a real verse is not the same as applying it the way the tradition that produced it applied it.

### Counter: "Muslim communities produce and shelter these terrorists — that's not nothing."

Response:

> Radicalization inside some Muslim communities is a real, documented phenomenon and deserves honest study, including uncomfortable questions about specific networks and ideologies. But "some communities have a radicalization problem" is a different claim from "Muslims collectively are guilty," and it sits alongside the fact that the same communities are overwhelmingly the ones being killed by these groups. You can hold both: real internal problem, and collective guilt is still the wrong frame.

### Counter: "500 scholars signing a statement doesn't stop a single bombing — the Amman Message is just PR, and one of its own scholars, Qaradawi, endorsed suicide bombings against Israelis."

Response:

> Both fair points. A declaration is not a security operation and hasn't stopped attacks, and yes — Sheikh Qaradawi, one of the 24 scholars whose answers to the founding questions helped shape the document, separately endorsed suicide attacks against Israeli civilians in the context of a specific conflict. I'm not citing the Amman Message as proof that every scholar near it rejects all religiously framed violence everywhere; that would be false. What it does establish is narrower and still real: a cross-school consensus specifically against declaring other Muslims apostates (takfir) — the move jihadist groups need to make before they can justify killing fellow Muslims, who are most of their victims. That's a real doctrinal constraint on one specific mechanism, not a blanket anti-terrorism seal of approval, and I'm not claiming more than that.

### Counter: "Your own tradition has collective punishment in its history — Banu Qurayza, dhimmi group status, and Muslims celebrating Mumtaz Qadri's murder of Salman Taseer in Pakistan by the thousands."

Response:

> Conceded, and I won't pretend the historical or contemporary record is clean. Banu Qurayza's adult men were executed collectively after the Battle of the Trench — that's in the classical sources, not a fabrication by critics. Classical law did accommodate group-status categories like dhimmi communal status. And yes, some populations have shown real public support for extremist positions, including celebrating Qadri's assassination of Taseer. None of that is being denied here. The claim in this card is narrower and different: it's about whether 1.9 billion living individuals today are morally answerable for organizations they didn't join and crimes they didn't commit — not a claim that Islamic history, practice, or contemporary public opinion is free of collective-punishment logic or troubling minority positions. Both can be true: the tradition's practice record includes collective punishment, and collective guilt of a global population today is still the wrong frame.

### Counter: "Your load-bearing verse says Islam rejects transferring liability across a group — but Islamic law's own homicide regime, 'aqila, assigns liability across the kin-group as settled doctrine. Your central claim contradicts your own fiqh."

Response:

> That's a real institution and I'm naming it, not hiding from it: classical Islamic homicide law assigns diya (blood-money) collectively to the offender's 'aqila — the solidarity/kin group — with qasama as a related collective-oath procedure. This is mainstream, cross-school fiqh discussed in every classical furu' manual, not a tribal survival. So yes, Islamic law contains a genuine collective-*liability* mechanism. But look at exactly what it is and what it is not. 'Aqila is (a) *civil and compensatory* — it apportions blood-money, not blame; (b) triggered by *accidental / unintentional* homicide, not intentional murder — for deliberate killing (qatl al-'amd) classical law imposes qisas on the *individual* killer, and the 'aqila does *not* bear it; and (c) applied to a *defined, bounded, mutually-obligated* solidarity group under adjudicated rules, historically the group that would also *receive* compensation and share defense obligations. That is categorically different from moral *guilt* for intentional mass murder imputed to 1.9 billion strangers who share only a confessional label, who never consented to any mutual-liability compact, and whom no court has adjudicated. Classical law's own answer to intentional murder is to execute the murderer individually — which is the opposite of collective guilt. So 'aqila doesn't rescue the collective-guilt claim; if anything it shows the tradition already draws the exact line I'm drawing: shared compensatory liability for accidents within a consenting group is one thing; criminal and moral guilt for deliberate atrocity attaches to the individual who commits it.

### Counter: "Muslims killing other Muslims doesn't disprove religious motivation — sectarian violence (Sunni-on-Shia, takfiri killings of 'insufficiently Islamic' Sunnis) is itself religiously coded. Your victim data actually shows the theology is doing violent work, not that it's irrelevant."

Response:

> That's correct, and I'm conceding it directly: a large share of jihadist-caused Muslim deaths are intra-Sunni or Sunni-on-Shia violence, organized around explicitly theological categories like takfir, "rafidha," and "murtadd" — that is religiously coded violence, not an accident that happens to also kill Muslims. What the victim data refutes is a different claim than the one you're making: it refutes "1.9 billion Muslims worldwide are collectively guilty for this," not "this violence has no connection to internal Islamic theological categories." The second claim isn't one this card makes. Both are true at once: the violence is often organized around real theological categories, and collective guilt of the global Muslim population is still the wrong frame for assigning responsibility.

### Counter: "Comparing this to a lone Christian clinic bomber is a false equivalence — jihadist groups are organized, transnational, and produce fatwa-styled theological justifications at scale. A lone shooter doesn't do that."

Response:

> That asymmetry is real and I won't paper over it — a lone actor and a transnational movement with its own clerics and juristic-styled literature are different categories of case, and jihadism has a traceable internal genealogy (Kharijite precedent, later revivalist and Qutb-influenced strands) rather than being random crime with no roots in the tradition's internal debates. And I'll go further and give you the strongest version of your own analogy: the fair comparison isn't the lone clinic bomber at all — it's other *organized*, religiously-framed movements with their own clerical or ideological apparatus (Christian-nationalist militias and the IRA's Catholic-nationalist framing, the LRA in Uganda, Buddhist-nationalist violence in Myanmar, Hindutva networks). Those are the same-scale comparanda, and they do the work better than the lone shooter. Here's the point: even against *those*, mainstream discourse does not impute per-individual moral guilt to every co-religionist — no serious observer holds every Catholic answerable for the IRA or every Buddhist for anti-Rohingya violence. So the same-category comparison confirms the standard rather than defeating it. And on the underlying question — whether an organized actor's claim to represent a religion, however scripturally elaborated, transfers guilt onto that religion's 1.9 billion adherents collectively — the answer doesn't get easier for the "collectively guilty" side just because the organization is bigger or more doctrinally developed. If anything, a scholarly consensus (Amman Message) built specifically to rebut that doctrinal apparatus is the appropriate-scale response, not a verdict on ordinary believers.
