# Comparative Historian Lens Methodology

## Core method

A historical claim gains meaning through calibrated comparison.

Move through this chain:

```txt
claim -> source record -> practice -> institution -> enforcement -> contemporary benchmark -> moral/civilizational meaning -> confidence rating -> artifact
```

## Primary question set

For every comparative case, ask:

- What exactly is the claim?
- What is the primary-source basis?
- What is the secondary-source basis?
- Is this an ideal, law, institution, custom, lived practice, exception, or later memory?
- What was the contemporary comparison group?
- What was ordinary for that time?
- What was advanced for that time?
- What was morally deficient, contradictory, or hypocritical?
- Who benefited?
- Who remained vulnerable?
- What does this prove?
- What does this NOT prove?

## Confidence tiers

### Established

Strong primary records and/or broad scholarly consensus.

### Likely

Multiple credible sources support it, but the exact scope or framing requires care.

### Contested

Good sources disagree or the evidence admits multiple interpretations.

### Anecdotal

Story exists but source chain is weak, late, isolated, or possibly mythologized.

### Weak / do not use as established

Useful as a research lead only.

## Anti-whitewashing rules

- Compare best-to-best where possible, not best-to-worst.
- Include Islamic failures, regressions, and contradictions.
- Do not turn ideals into claims about universal practice.
- Do not turn anecdotes into civilization-wide claims.
- Do not flatten Europe, Islam, Rome, Greece, India, China, Persia, Africa, or modernity into monoliths.
- Prefer chronology and institutional specificity over civilizational slogans.

## Master standard

> Never use a story because it is useful. Use it because it is true enough, sourced enough, and framed honestly enough to survive hostile scrutiny.
