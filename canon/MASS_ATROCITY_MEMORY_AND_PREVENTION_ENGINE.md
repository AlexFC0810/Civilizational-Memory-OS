# Mass Atrocity Memory and Prevention Engine

**Status:** Canonical architecture v0.1  
**Purpose:** Turn humanity's memories of genocide and mass atrocity into transferable recognition, early warning, public courage, and institutions capable of interrupting the machinery before killing becomes normalized.

## Governing thesis

> **Never Again is not a slogan. It is an operating system.**

Humanity has accumulated memorials, testimony, courts, archives, museums, anniversaries, and solemn promises. Yet mass atrocities recur because remembrance often remains trapped inside the identity, geography, or ceremony of one case.

The system must perform a harder transformation:

`memory → mechanism → recognition → warning → intervention → correction`

The dead must be remembered as persons. The crime must be reconstructed as a process. The process must become recognizable across identities. Recognition must trigger action before destruction becomes irreversible.

> **Memory without mechanism becomes mourning. Mechanism without memory becomes bureaucracy. The purpose is to unite both.**

## The highest moral test: transfer across identity

A community naturally remembers what was done to its own people. That memory is sacred and must not be diluted.

But memory reaches its highest civilizational form when it also helps that community recognize the machinery of destruction when the threatened people are strangers, rivals, minorities, or politically inconvenient.

> **The test of memory is not only whether we honor yesterday's victims. It is whether their memory helps us recognize tomorrow's victims before the graves exist.**

This is the **Universalization Test**:

1. **Fidelity** — Does the community preserve what happened to its own victims accurately?
2. **Humanization** — Does it restore names, lives, families, and worlds rather than only totals?
3. **Mechanism recognition** — Does it understand how ordinary institutions became machinery of destruction?
4. **Cross-group transfer** — Can it recognize comparable warning processes when another group is targeted?
5. **Action transfer** — Does memory produce protective behavior, not only ceremony?
6. **Moral symmetry** — Does it condemn the same machinery when used by allies, heroes, or members of its own group?
7. **Correctability** — Can its memory institutions revise errors without surrendering truth?

The purpose is not to compare suffering competitively or declare distinct crimes equivalent.

> **Compare mechanisms without flattening victims. Preserve uniqueness without making recognition non-transferable.**

## Category precision

The word **genocide** has a specific legal meaning centered on prohibited acts committed with intent to destroy, in whole or in part, a national, ethnical, racial, or religious group as such.

Not every enormous crime is legally genocide. Crimes against humanity, ethnic cleansing, politicide, forced displacement, colonial destruction, war crimes, mass killing, cultural destruction, enslavement, famine policy, and state terror may be catastrophic even when a court has not classified them as genocide.

Therefore the engine uses two parallel tracks.

### Legal track

- controlling legal definition;
- protected group;
- prohibited acts;
- evidence of specific intent;
- court or tribunal findings;
- jurisdiction and procedural status;
- unresolved legal disputes.

### Civilizational-harm track

- people killed, displaced, injured, enslaved, starved, raped, sterilized, abducted, or dispossessed;
- destruction of homes, institutions, language, worship, archives, cemeteries, and cultural continuity;
- intergenerational trauma;
- concealment and denial;
- continuing political consequences.

The legal threshold must not be weakened for rhetorical convenience. It also must not become a moral escape hatch through which catastrophic crimes disappear because one element remains unproven.

## Classification states

Every case must carry a visible status:

1. **Court-established genocide** — genocide established by a competent court or tribunal for the stated group, location, acts, and period.
2. **Broadly recognized historical genocide** — strong scholarly and institutional recognition, even where no contemporaneous international court rendered judgment.
3. **Contested genocide classification** — substantial evidence and serious expert claims exist, but legal, scholarly, state, or community disagreement remains material.
4. **Genocide allegation under active adjudication or investigation** — proceedings or inquiries remain incomplete.
5. **Mass atrocity outside or not dependent upon genocide classification** — severe crimes requiring memory and prevention analysis without forcing the genocide label.
6. **Insufficiently researched** — the AIOS does not yet have enough reliable evidence to classify.

No case may silently move between states.

## Core architecture

### 1. Memory Archive

Preserve:

- victim names and biographies;
- survivor testimony;
- family and community records;
- photographs and personal items;
- demographic and geographic records;
- perpetrator documents;
- diplomatic and military records;
- court judgments;
- journalism and contemporaneous witnesses;
- oral history;
- physical and digital provenance;
- correction and version history.

> **Genocide attempts to turn persons into categories and then categories into absence. Memory reverses the sequence: from absence back to name, face, family, and world.**

### 2. Case Truth Packet

For each case, establish:

- scope and chronology;
- legal status;
- victim groups;
- responsible actors and command structures;
- disputed claims;
- confidence levels;
- source conflicts;
- denial narratives;
- strongest breaker evidence;
- prohibited simplifications.

### 3. Genocidal Process Graph

Map how destruction became thinkable, administratively possible, socially tolerated, and materially executable.

Recommended process vocabulary:

- classification;
- identity hardening;
- discrimination;
- dispossession;
- dehumanization;
- propaganda saturation;
- organization;
- arming and training;
- polarization;
- removal of moderates;
- preparation;
- registration and identification;
- segregation and concentration;
- forced displacement;
- persecution;
- mass killing and destructive living conditions;
- sexual and reproductive violence;
- child transfer or abduction;
- cultural erasure;
- concealment;
- denial;
- glorification;
- impunity;
- recurrence.

These are interacting processes, not a universal linear staircase. Gregory Stanton's stage model is a valuable reference, not a sacred or exhaustive ontology.

### 4. Role Graph

Do not reduce history to victims and monsters.

Map the full human and institutional ecology:

- architects;
- political leaders;
- ideologues;
- propagandists;
- bureaucrats;
- military and police commanders;
- direct perpetrators;
- local collaborators;
- profiteers;
- informants;
- passive bystanders;
- fearful conformists;
- external sponsors;
- international institutions;
- journalists;
- clergy and intellectuals;
- dissenters;
- rescuers;
- defectors;
- documentarians;
- survivors;
- investigators;
- judges;
- deniers;
- memory keepers.

The key question is not only, “Who killed?” It is:

> **Which ordinary roles, incentives, permissions, silences, and institutional routines made extraordinary evil executable?**

### 5. Propaganda and Language Observatory

Track:

- names used for the target group;
- animal, disease, infestation, criminal, traitor, invader, or contamination metaphors;
- conspiracy narratives;
- fabricated atrocities;
- demographic panic;
- collective guilt;
- sexual threat narratives;
- sacred or historical grievance;
- euphemisms such as cleansing, relocation, security, pacification, final solution, or population transfer;
- suppression of moderates;
- repetition networks;
- media ownership and state coordination where evidenced;
- platform amplification;
- the transition from insult to policy.

The purpose is not to equate every hateful statement with imminent genocide. It is to detect when language joins organization, discrimination, impunity, weapons, and political permission.

### 6. Early Warning Engine

Combine:

- long-horizon structural risk;
- event-driven process indicators;
- qualitative country and regional analysis;
- survivor and local civil-society reporting;
- arms flows and militia organization;
- elite rhetoric;
- rights restrictions;
- forced movement;
- attacks on moderates, journalists, courts, and monitors;
- communication shutdowns;
- detention and identification systems;
- historical recurrence;
- denial and glorification of prior atrocities.

Warnings must be probabilistic and calibrated.

False positives can erode trust and be weaponized. False negatives can leave people dead. The system must display uncertainty, evidence, update history, and the consequences of both error types.

### 7. Intervention and Counterfactual Lab

For every case, ask:

- At what earlier point could the trajectory have been interrupted?
- Which actors had leverage?
- Which warnings were available?
- What did decision-makers believe?
- Which interventions were attempted?
- Which failed because of timing, scale, design, politics, or enforcement?
- Which interventions might have worsened the danger?
- Which local institutions resisted successfully?
- What would a realistic prevention package have required?

Intervention classes:

- citizenship and equal-rights protection;
- independent media and counter-propaganda;
- protection of moderates and monitors;
- documentation and secure archives;
- sanctions and financial disruption;
- arms embargoes;
- safe passage and refuge;
- peacekeeping with credible protection capability;
- criminal accountability;
- defections and amnesty design;
- local intergroup institutions;
- humanitarian access;
- command disruption;
- civilian protection;
- post-conflict truth and restitution;
- denial resistance.

### 8. Rescuer and Resistance Atlas

The system must study not only how people became killers, but how people refused.

Record:

- rescuers;
- hidden networks;
- diplomats who issued documents;
- neighbors who sheltered families;
- soldiers who refused orders;
- clerics who protected targets;
- journalists and archivists;
- communities that resisted polarization;
- international actors who acted early;
- institutional designs that made refusal possible.

Do not romanticize rescuers or use them to erase the surrounding crime.

Their value is causal:

> **What moral formation, social bond, authority structure, incentive, identity, or institution enabled a person to resist when obedience was safer?**

### 9. Denial and Memory-War Observatory

Denial can include:

- total negation;
- minimization;
- inflated uncertainty;
- victim blaming;
- intent denial;
- euphemism;
- false equivalence;
- selective chronology;
- destruction of evidence;
- rehabilitation of perpetrators;
- intimidation of witnesses;
- capture of curricula;
- memorial removal;
- algorithmic flooding;
- manufactured document dumps;
- synthetic media.

Denial is not merely a historical disagreement when it continues the erasure, blocks justice, rewards perpetrators, intimidates survivors, or prepares recurrence.

### 10. Public Memory and Transmission Engine

Every case should produce multiple resolutions:

- one name;
- one life story;
- one family or community;
- one map;
- one mechanism;
- one warning sign;
- one rescuer or resistor;
- one institutional failure;
- one correction of denial;
- one action lesson;
- one long-form case narrative;
- one curriculum;
- one comparative module.

The audience should not leave only horrified. It should leave more capable of recognition.

## The controls problem: study where genocide did not happen

A system built only from completed atrocities risks hindsight bias.

To identify causal mechanisms, compare genocide cases with:

- high-risk societies that avoided mass killing;
- regions where violence remained limited;
- communities protected by cross-cutting institutions;
- attempted atrocities interrupted early;
- rescue corridors that worked;
- military units that refused;
- propaganda campaigns that failed;
- political transitions that de-escalated;
- countries with similar structural risk but different outcomes.

> **The dead teach us how destruction happened. The near-misses teach us what can stop it.**

Each comparative cluster should contain:

- a primary atrocity case;
- at least one failed intervention;
- at least one partial or successful interruption;
- at least one structurally similar non-genocide control.

## The case packet lifecycle

`Signal → Case scoping → Classification state → Source ledger → Victim and survivor memory → Chronology → Legal analysis → Process graph → Role graph → Propaganda map → International response → Resistance and rescue → Denial map → Counterfactual interventions → Comparative controls → Transferable warning genes → Public narrative → Curriculum → Policy and institution implications → Revision`

## First cohort

Begin with a deliberately diverse cohort rather than attempting all cases at once.

### P0 founding cases

1. **Srebrenica and the Bosnian war** — safe-area failure, organized killing, international paralysis, mass graves, forensic identification, denial, and living regional consequences.
2. **The Armenian genocide** — deportation, exterminatory conditions, documentation, diaspora memory, cultural destruction, long-duration denial, and the genealogy of the genocide concept.
3. **The Holocaust** — ideological radicalization, state bureaucracy, industrialized killing, collaboration, resistance, liberation, trials, names recovery, and the strongest global memory infrastructure.
4. **The 1994 genocide against the Tutsi in Rwanda** — rapid mobilization, radio and local propaganda, administrative participation, international failure, survivor testimony, justice, and peace education.

### P1 mechanism-expansion cases

- Herero and Nama;
- Cambodia, with precise distinction between the wider Khmer Rouge mass killing and legally established genocide against protected minorities;
- Guatemala's Maya communities;
- the Yazidi genocide by ISIS;
- Darfur;
- Bangladesh 1971;
- Assyrian and Ottoman Greek destruction;
- colonial and Indigenous genocide cases selected with community and legal precision;
- Rohingya persecution and active legal proceedings;
- Holodomor, with recognition and classification disputes made explicit.

### Live and contested cases

Current allegations must exist in a separate **Live Atrocity Review** mode.

Requirements:

- no premature canonical verdict;
- exact procedural status;
- claims from all relevant legal and investigative bodies;
- primary evidence;
- explicit uncertainty;
- continuous updates;
- no use of one historical victim group as a rhetorical weapon against another.

## P0 pilot: Bosnia as the bridge between this project and creator supercharging

Bosnia should be the first complete case because it connects:

- Bek Lover's Balkan history and anti-war mission;
- Srebrenica's court-established genocide;
- propaganda, ethno-nationalism, weapons, and elite manipulation;
- the failure of a declared safe area;
- forensic reconstruction and mass-grave evidence;
- denial and glorification;
- intergenerational memory;
- the risk of renewed polarization;
- the Creator Supercharger's ability to turn deep research into preventive public intelligence.

First flagship working title:

> **How Genocide Becomes Thinkable: Bosnia Before Srebrenica**

The episode should not begin with bodies. It should begin with the earlier machinery:

- categories;
- grievance;
- media;
- institutional collapse;
- removal of moderates;
- organized force;
- abandoned civilians;
- ignored warnings.

Then it should restore the victims as people, document the crime, expose denial, honor resistance, and end with present-day recognition skills.

## Required questions for every case

1. Who defined the target group, and how did the definition change?
2. Which true wounds or grievances were exploited?
3. Which falsehood converted grievance into collective guilt?
4. How were neighbors made legible as enemies?
5. Which laws, lists, maps, IDs, registries, institutions, or technologies enabled targeting?
6. Who organized, funded, armed, trained, transported, documented, and concealed?
7. How were moderates isolated, discredited, or killed?
8. Which institutions failed, and which resisted?
9. What did international actors know, and when?
10. What did ordinary people believe they were participating in?
11. Which incentives made participation, silence, or refusal more likely?
12. What forms of sexual, reproductive, familial, cultural, and child-directed destruction occurred?
13. How was evidence destroyed or recovered?
14. Which denial narratives appeared before, during, and after the crime?
15. Which people or institutions saved lives, and why?
16. What earlier intervention window existed?
17. Which prior atrocity memories failed to transfer?
18. Which warning signs would be visible today?
19. What evidence would change the current interpretation?
20. What institution should exist now because this happened?

## Integrity constitution

The engine must never:

- create a suffering Olympics;
- use one genocide to erase, relativize, or justify another;
- reduce a people to permanent victims or permanent perpetrators;
- assign inherited guilt to descendants;
- treat a whole ethnicity, religion, nation, or civilization as a genocidal essence;
- fabricate testimony or generate synthetic survivor speech;
- use emotionally powerful images without provenance and dignity review;
- force disputed cases into false certainty;
- let legal precision anesthetize moral reality;
- let moral urgency dissolve evidentiary standards;
- present remembrance as sufficient prevention;
- weaponize “Never Again” for selective political immunity.

Perpetrators, commands, institutions, ideologies, incentives, and decisions must be named precisely.

## AI and archive safeguards

AI may assist with:

- multilingual transcription and translation;
- entity and name matching;
- document linking;
- timeline construction;
- claim extraction;
- contradiction detection;
- geospatial and institutional mapping;
- denial-pattern detection;
- public-language drafting;
- curriculum adaptation.

AI must not:

- invent missing testimony;
- reconstruct a victim's voice as if authentic;
- alter images without visible labeling;
- infer legal intent from pattern alone;
- collapse uncertainty;
- merge people with similar names without human verification;
- publish high-stakes allegations without expert review;
- treat the model's pattern recognition as court evidence.

## Success metrics

Do not measure only page views or emotional response.

Measure:

- factual retention;
- victim-name and life-story retention;
- ability to distinguish genocide from adjacent crime categories;
- recognition of unseen dehumanization and organization patterns;
- ability to identify institutional rather than identity-sized culpability;
- cross-group transfer;
- reduction in denial belief;
- willingness to protect an out-group;
- identification of realistic intervention windows;
- creator adoption of source-backed language;
- curriculum use;
- warnings escalated responsibly;
- institutions or policies created;
- corrections made visibly.
