# Question Genome

**Status:** Canonical working architecture  
**Purpose:** Systematically discover, evolve, prioritize, and preserve the questions that reorganize entire fields rather than merely requesting more information.

## Core thesis

AI's highest leverage may not be answering the questions people already ask.

It may be finding the questions civilization forgot were possible.

Answers inherit the assumptions, categories, source layers, comparison classes, and moral boundaries embedded in their questions. A badly framed question can produce technically accurate but deeply misleading answers.

The Question Genome exists to improve the ceiling of the entire AIOS.

> **A great answer fills a gap. A load-bearing question redraws the map.**

## What is a question gene?

A question gene is a reusable interrogative pattern that reveals a class of hidden structure across domains.

Example:

> Which authority layer actually made the claim?

This can be applied to:

- religion;
- law;
- science;
- media;
- corporations;
- political movements;
- historical civilizations.

The surface nouns change. The reasoning function persists.

## The major question genes

### 1. Source-layer gene

**Function:** Separates foundational text, report, interpretation, institution, state, movement, and individual.

**Form:** Which exact authority layer said or authorized this?

### 2. Atomic-claim gene

**Function:** Breaks emotional claim stacks into independently testable propositions.

**Form:** What are the smallest factual, causal, moral, and predictive claims hidden inside this sentence?

### 3. True-fragment gene

**Function:** Preserves legitimate evidence while exposing the unsupported totalizing map attached to it.

**Form:** What true fragment makes this narrative persuasive, and what missing bridge carries it to the conclusion?

### 4. Causal-bridge gene

**Function:** Exposes inferred mechanisms presented as facts.

**Form:** What additional premise must be true for this evidence to establish that conclusion?

### 5. Comparative-baseline gene

**Function:** Prevents exceptionalism and presentism.

**Form:** Compared with whom, in which period, under which constraints, using which equivalent layer?

### 6. Ideal-practice symmetry gene

**Function:** Detects comparisons between one group's ideals and another group's failures.

**Form:** Are we comparing ideal with ideal, law with law, institution with institution, and practice with practice?

### 7. Power-asymmetry gene

**Function:** Reveals whether morality persists when the vulnerable cannot reward or punish the powerful.

**Form:** What did the system do when mercy offered no strategic return?

### 8. Post-use gene

**Function:** Tests moral debt after usefulness ends.

**Form:** What did the institution owe a being after it could no longer produce value?

### 9. Objective-function gene

**Function:** Reveals the behavior the system actually optimizes.

**Form:** What did the institution make profitable, prestigious, measurable, or survivable?

### 10. Invisible-cost gene

**Function:** Surfaces displaced harms and hidden beneficiaries.

**Form:** Who or what paid the cost that the visible success metric excluded?

### 11. Architecture-versus-implementation gene

**Function:** Separates blueprint, interpretation, institutional design, and human violation.

**Form:** Did this outcome follow from the stated blueprint, a later architecture, predictable capture, or simple noncompliance?

### 12. Repetition/recurrence gene

**Function:** Prevents “failed humans” from becoming an unfalsifiable escape hatch.

**Form:** If the failure recurs across places and generations, what architecture repeatedly permits or rewards it?

### 13. Succession gene

**Function:** Tests whether moral authority survives the founder.

**Form:** What happens when the founder dies, the virtuous ruler leaves, or the original coalition fractures?

### 14. Correction-capacity gene

**Function:** Identifies whether a system can recognize and reverse capture.

**Form:** How can this institution discover that it is wrong, and who has the power to correct it?

### 15. Breaker-evidence gene

**Function:** Makes claims revisable.

**Form:** What evidence would force us to abandon or materially change this conclusion?

### 16. Counterfactual gene

**Function:** Tests causal necessity and alternative pathways.

**Form:** What likely changes if this institution, event, person, or knowledge channel is removed?

### 17. Reverse-engineering gene

**Function:** Identifies load-bearing dependencies.

**Form:** If the system disappeared tomorrow, what would have to be rebuilt first for the function to return?

### 18. Civilizational-API gene

**Function:** Extracts reusable institutional modules from history.

**Form:** What problem did this institution solve, under what assumptions, with what interface and failure modes?

### 19. Attention gene

**Function:** Distinguishes intelligence from the ability to sustain shared focus.

**Form:** What load-bearing truth is society structurally unable to notice long enough to act upon?

### 20. Selection-and-silence gene

**Function:** Detects distortion without explicit falsehood.

**Form:** Which facts were selected, repeated, emotionally weighted, or excluded—and what map does that selection create?

### 21. Identity gene

**Function:** Reveals why evidence alone does not move belief.

**Form:** What identity, belonging, innocence, status, or moral self-image does this claim protect?

### 22. Transfer gene

**Function:** Tests whether a lesson is a real skill or a partisan talking point.

**Form:** Can the audience apply the same rule when it threatens its favored group?

### 23. Compression gene

**Function:** Finds the smallest representation that preserves load-bearing truth.

**Form:** What can be removed without changing the audience's reasonable conclusion, and what cannot?

### 24. Institution gene

**Function:** Converts insight into durable action.

**Form:** What institution would make this moral insight survive ordinary human weakness?

### 25. Future-generation gene

**Function:** Adds absent stakeholders.

**Form:** How would this decision be judged by people, animals, ecosystems, or communities that bear its effects but have no present voice?

## Genetic operations

Question genes should be evolved through deliberate operations.

### Mutation

Replace the domain while retaining the reasoning function.

Example:

- Which layer of Islam said it?
- Which layer of Christianity said it?
- Which layer of science said it?
- Which layer of the company authorized it?

### Crossover

Combine two genes.

Example:

> Which authority layer made the claim, and what institutional incentive caused later actors to treat it as binding?

This combines source-layer and objective-function genes.

### Inversion

Reverse the perspective.

Example:

- What did rulers say about animals?
- What would the animals say about the rulers?

### Scale shift

Apply the same question at individual, institutional, civilizational, and planetary levels.

### Temporal shift

Ask about origin, institutionalization, succession, long-run fruit, collapse, and revival.

### Adversarial mutation

Ask the question in the form most threatening to the favored conclusion.

### Compression

Reduce a complex question into a memorable retrieval cue without losing its function.

Example:

> Which layer said it?

## Question scoring

Each candidate question should be scored from 1–5 on:

- **explanatory leverage** — how much terrain can it reorganize?
- **neglectedness** — how rarely is it asked well?
- **transferability** — how many domains can use it?
- **falsifiability** — can it produce breaker evidence?
- **tractability** — can a bounded research packet investigate it?
- **moral stakes** — what harms or opportunities depend on it?
- **institutional yield** — can it generate design implications?
- **narrative yield** — can it produce a memorable story or image?
- **dependency centrality** — how many other questions rely on it?
- **identity resistance** — does it pierce a protected assumption?

A provisional priority function:

`Priority = leverage × transfer × stakes × centrality × neglectedness × tractability`

Use the score as a discussion aid, not an oracle.

## Question lineage

Every important question should preserve:

- `question_id`;
- exact wording;
- gene class;
- origin conversation or source;
- parent questions;
- mutations;
- associated claim families;
- research packets generated;
- findings unlocked;
- narratives changed;
- institutions inspired;
- current status;
- supersession history.

This allows the AIOS to learn which questions actually produced breakthroughs.

- seed;
- promising;
- packetized;
- active research;
- answered provisionally;
- canon-changing;
- dormant;
- superseded;

## Initial load-bearing question bank

### Human history

1. What does humanity repeatedly gain the power to do before it gains the wisdom to govern?
2. Which institutions successfully made power answerable to something beyond itself?
3. Why do reform movements become captured by the incentives they were created to oppose?
4. What is the smallest set of institutions required to rebuild humane civilization?
5. Which moral achievements survived succession, scarcity, war, and elite capture?

### Islamic civilization

6. What does the Qur'an establish, and what did later authorities project onto it?
7. What does obedience to the Messenger mean after the Messenger's death?
8. Which Islamic achievements followed Qur'anic incentives, which came from inherited civilizations, and which emerged through synthesis?
9. Which recurring harms were blueprint, architecture, state incentive, inherited custom, or failure of correction?
10. When did Islamic civilization align moral confidence, intellectual openness, institutional competence, and power restraint most successfully?
11. What authority architecture could enable Islamic renewal without reproducing clerical, dynastic, or state capture?

### Moral Golden Ages

12. Did mercy reach beings that could not reward the civilization?
13. Which protections became institutions rather than sermons?
14. How were animals treated after they stopped being economically useful?
15. Which contemporaries performed better, and by what mechanism?
16. Did moral concern survive famine, war, class difference, and commercial pressure?

### Media and propaganda

17. What true fragment is carrying the false story?
18. Which source layers were collapsed?
19. What identity does the claim protect?
20. Which repeated image or archetype made the narrative feel self-evident?
21. What truthful replacement story can occupy the upper layers of frame, identity, ritual, and memory?
22. Can the audience detect the same manipulation when used against a disliked group?

### Modernity

23. Which cruelties did modernity abolish, and which did it industrialize or hide?
24. Where did modern rights expansion depend on inherited moral capital it no longer reproduces?
25. What does the market value that life itself does not?
26. Which technologies enlarged capacity while weakening correction, belonging, or moral visibility?
27. How can sacred moral limits, scientific truth-seeking, distributed power, and institutional competence coexist?

### AI and the future

28. What forms of knowledge should AI compress, and which distinctions must never be lost?
29. How can an AIOS remain opinionated without becoming an unaccountable oracle?
30. Which correction rights must humans retain over an increasingly autonomous intelligence system?
31. What does AI make possible before institutions can govern it wisely?
32. Can AI enlarge collective moral imagination rather than merely productivity and persuasion?

## Public forms

The Question Genome should generate recurring formats:

- Question of the Week;
- The Question Trial;
- load-bearing question cards;
- interactive question trees;
- classroom Socratic sequences;
- creator prompts;
- research bounties;
- “questions your textbook did not ask” episodes;
- fictional characters whose role is to ask the forbidden or forgotten question.

## Failure modes

- collecting clever questions without packetizing them;
- questions so broad they cannot be falsified;
- rhetorical questions disguised as predetermined conclusions;
- asking only questions that attack outsiders;
- novelty without explanatory leverage;
- generating issue sprawl rather than research structure;
- failing to preserve which question produced which discovery;
- treating high scores as replacements for judgment;
- asking questions whose answers cannot affect doctrine or action.

## Definition of a canon-changing question

A question is canon-changing when it does at least three of the following:

- reveals a category error;
- collapses several weak claims at once;
- forces a new comparison class;
- changes the causal model;
- exposes hidden beneficiaries or victims;
- produces a new framework or institution;
- transfers across domains;
- changes approved public language;
- creates breaker evidence;
- redirects the research agenda.

## Canonical close

> **The quality of the AIOS will never exceed the quality of the questions it can generate, preserve, and allow to overturn its own conclusions.**

> **The legendary question is not the one that sounds profound. It is the one that makes several false answers impossible and several better futures visible.**
