# Civilizational Durability Stack

**Status:** Canonical framework

## Thesis

A civilization is not judged only by the beauty of its founding vision or the achievements of its best generation. It must be evaluated by whether truth, mercy, competence, restraint, and protection of the vulnerable survive ordinary human beings, concentrated power, succession, scarcity, conflict, and time.

> The true test of a civilization is not whether it can produce one righteous founder or one prosperous generation. It is whether it can make truth, mercy, competence, and restraint survive ordinary human beings, concentrated power, and the death of its heroes.

## The ten-layer durability stack

1. **Ontology** — what is ultimately real?
2. **Moral vision** — what is good, forbidden, sacred, and owed?
3. **Human model** — what kind of creature is the human being, and what failure modes should be expected?
4. **Authority** — who may interpret, decide, and bind?
5. **Institutional architecture** — what laws, offices, customs, incentives, checks, and rituals operationalize the vision?
6. **Material capacity** — what can the society actually sustain under its resources, technology, demography, and security conditions?
7. **Incentive structure** — what behaviors are rewarded, punished, normalized, or made profitable?
8. **Implementation** — what happens in ordinary practice rather than ideal theory?
9. **Succession durability** — what happens after the founder, reformer, first generation, or unusually virtuous leadership disappears?
10. **Correction capacity** — how does the system detect, admit, and repair predictable failure?

## The missing middle

The common debate mistake is to compare moral blueprint directly with historical outcome.

The real chain is:

`Moral vision -> institutional architecture -> incentives -> implementation -> succession -> correction -> long-run fruit`

A blueprint may be violated without being disproved. But repeated violations may still reveal:

- under-specified architecture;
- excessive concentration of interpretive power;
- weak enforcement;
- capture by economic or political interests;
- inadequate succession design;
- poor correction mechanisms;
- or material conditions that overwhelmed the intended system.

Therefore:

> Do not blame a blueprint merely because it was violated. But do investigate whether it provided adequate architecture to prevent, expose, and repair predictable violations.

## Succession Stress Test

Evaluate every system under:

- an exceptional founder;
- a mediocre successor;
- a corrupt successor;
- internal factionalism;
- invasion or civil war;
- scarcity and famine;
- prosperity and abundance;
- rapid technological change;
- interpretive disagreement;
- elite capture;
- mass ignorance;
- several generations of cultural drift.

A system that only works under saints is morally attractive but institutionally fragile.

## Capacity-Direction-Capture-Correction model

Every civilization-scale mechanism should be scored on four dimensions:

1. **Capacity** — what can it do?
2. **Direction** — toward what ends does it aim that capacity?
3. **Capture** — who can redirect it toward private or factional advantage?
4. **Correction** — how can capture and error be reversed?

Examples:

- Industrialization increases capacity but does not determine moral allocation.
- Religion may supply direction and internal restraint but may be captured by clergy or rulers.
- Democracy supplies consent and error correction but does not independently define the good.
- Markets coordinate production but may convert wealth into governing power.
- Welfare systems protect vulnerability but can be weakened by fiscal, bureaucratic, or political capture.

## Historical Calibration and Transcendence Tests

Every moral judgment should run two tests.

### Historical calibration

How did the civilization compare with societies facing similar resources, knowledge, threats, and inherited practices?

### Moral transcendence

How far did it rise above its environment, and which injustices did it leave unresolved?

The preferred verdict form is:

> This was a major moral advance relative to its baseline, but it did not complete the moral trajectory it opened.

## Moral innovation vs moral completion

A tradition can introduce a revolutionary moral seed without completing all of its implications.

Do not confuse:

- innovation with completion;
- direction with destination;
- norm with compliance;
- permission with command;
- law with enforcement;
- historical superiority with moral perfection.

## Benchmark questions

For any civilization, religion, ideology, state, company, or movement, ask:

- What does it claim?
- What does it encode institutionally?
- What does it reward?
- What does it repeatedly produce?
- What happens when power concentrates?
- What happens after the founder dies?
- What happens when the system is materially stressed?
- What protects the vulnerable when protection is costly?
- What detects corruption?
- What makes reform possible without destroying valuable inheritance?
- What evidence would show that the architecture itself is defective?

## Canonical synthesis

> A civilization becomes wise when moral vision, material competence, institutional architecture, distributed power, cultural formation, succession durability, and error correction reinforce rather than sabotage one another.
