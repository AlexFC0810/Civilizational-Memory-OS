# Media-to-Immunity Engine

**Status:** Canonical architecture; implementation remains staged and evidence-gated.

## Mission

Turn real debates, interviews, discussions, sermons, podcasts, documentaries, and social videos into a recursively improving public immune system against falsehood, smear, manipulation, and civilizational amnesia.

The engine does not target speakers, communities, religions, or political tribes as pathogens. The metaphor applies only to **claims, frames, and manipulation patterns**.

> The goal is not to silence bad speech. It is to make manipulative claims progressively easier to recognize, test, neutralize, and replace with a better map of reality.

## Why transcripts matter

A transcript is not merely source material for clipping. It is field intelligence about:

- what people actually believe;
- which claims recur across audiences;
- which labels carry hidden conclusions;
- which facts are partly true but causally distorted;
- which objections remain unanswered;
- which stories are emotionally sticky;
- which source gaps block public understanding;
- which corrections are too academic to travel.

Every transcript can therefore produce both **content** and **research intelligence**.

## The fundamental unit

The system should not organize itself primarily around videos or personalities. Its durable unit is the **claim family**.

A claim family includes:

- the atomic claim;
- recurring paraphrases and rhetorical variants;
- source lineage;
- emotional and identity payload;
- manipulation techniques used;
- strongest defensible version;
- current evidence grade;
- versioned verdict;
- best corrective explanations;
- known counterattacks;
- content assets and measured results.

This prevents the same falsehood from being researched from scratch every time it appears in a new video.

## Core vocabulary

### Narrative strain
A recurring variant of a claim, smear, causal story, or rhetorical frame.

### Epistemic antibody
A source-hardened response that neutralizes a specific claim while preserving every true component.

### Immunity gene
A transferable recognition rule that helps audiences detect a broader manipulation class in future contexts. Examples: source-layer collapse, presentism, loaded-label compression, causal monoculture, asymmetric comparison, and ideal-versus-practice switching.

### Booster
A later exposure that refreshes recognition, deepens the model, or inoculates against a mutated strain.

### Immune memory
The versioned registry connecting claims, variants, verdicts, sources, content assets, audience tests, and revisions.

### Map repair
The replacement of a distorted causal or historical model with a more accurate, memorable, and generative model. This is the actual objective; rebuttal alone is insufficient.

## Two-plane architecture

### Civilizational Memory OS: truth plane
Owns:

- claim IDs and claim families;
- source layers and evidence grades;
- research packets;
- strongest-case pairs;
- breaker evidence;
- versioned verdicts;
- prohibited overclaims;
- canonical explanatory models;
- supersession history.

### Truth and Peace Engine: media operations plane
Owns:

- media ingestion;
- transcript normalization;
- timestamped segment selection;
- clip manifests;
- content variants;
- review and approval;
- publishing;
- distribution;
- performance and belief-shift measurement.

### Shared identifiers
Every implementation should preserve stable IDs:

- `media_id`
- `segment_id`
- `claim_id`
- `strain_id`
- `verdict_id`
- `antibody_id`
- `content_id`
- `experiment_id`

The truth plane must remain independent of any single content asset. The media plane must never silently upgrade a draft research claim into publishable truth.

## The operating pipeline

`Media -> Transcript -> Claims -> Strains -> Triage -> Truth closure -> Antibody design -> Content package -> Integrity gate -> Distribution -> Measurement -> Immune-memory update`

### Stage 0 — Intake
Capture:

- source URL or uploaded file;
- title, publisher, date, speakers, format, and audience;
- full transcript with timestamps and speaker turns;
- rights and reuse notes;
- user-supplied reason for importance.

### Stage 1 — Normalize

- preserve original language and punctuation where possible;
- mark uncertain transcription;
- separate speakers;
- retain timestamps;
- distinguish quotations from the speaker's own claims;
- preserve enough surrounding context to avoid deceptive clipping.

### Stage 2 — Atomize
Extract separately:

- factual claims;
- causal claims;
- moral judgments;
- definitions;
- historical analogies;
- predictions;
- labels;
- implied premises;
- concessions;
- unresolved questions.

Do not treat a paragraph as one claim when it contains five dependencies.

### Stage 3 — Detect the mechanism
Tag each claim for any relevant tactic or failure mode:

- loaded-label compression;
- source-layer collapse;
- context stripping;
- cherry-picking;
- false dichotomy;
- causal monoculture;
- presentism;
- guilt by association;
- anecdote-to-system leap;
- ideal/practice asymmetry;
- motte-and-bailey;
- emotional priming;
- scapegoating;
- false equivalence;
- fabricated precision;
- unsupported universal;
- authority laundering;
- category error;
- true fact used to imply a false map.

The engine must also mark **valid criticism**, **reasonable uncertainty**, and **good-faith correction**. Not every adverse claim is propaganda.

### Stage 4 — Triage
Score each claim family on a 1–5 scale:

- prevalence;
- potential harm;
- recurrence;
- audience salience;
- evidentiary tractability;
- strategic leverage;
- amplification risk.

High-amplification-risk fringe claims should often be archived rather than publicly repeated.

### Stage 5 — Truth closure
Route load-bearing claims through the existing evidence system:

1. exact claim;
2. exact source layer;
3. definitions;
4. strongest supporting case;
5. strongest opposing case;
6. primary and specialist evidence;
7. historical comparison class;
8. confounders and causal graph;
9. uncertainty and confidence;
10. breaker evidence;
11. provisional verdict;
12. prohibited overclaims.

### Stage 6 — Design the antibody
A strong corrective unit contains:

1. **Truth anchor** — begin with the clearest established reality.
2. **Concession** — preserve the strongest true part of the opposing claim.
3. **Distortion diagnosis** — name exactly where the inference breaks.
4. **Source repair** — show the relevant source or authority layer.
5. **Replacement model** — provide a better causal or historical map.
6. **Retrieval cue** — a memorable question, distinction, image, or line.
7. **Transfer lesson** — teach the manipulation pattern so it generalizes.
8. **Uncertainty boundary** — say what remains unresolved.
9. **Dignity bridge** — let the audience update without humiliation.
10. **Counterattack preparation** — anticipate the next strongest objection.

> A correction that removes a false belief but leaves an explanatory vacuum is incomplete.

### Stage 7 — Generate a content ladder
One hardened claim family may generate:

- a one-sentence correction;
- a load-bearing question;
- a claim card;
- a 15–30 second inoculation clip;
- a 30–90 second source-backed short;
- a 3–10 minute explainer;
- a debate response;
- a visual timeline or causal map;
- a source thread or article;
- a quiz, game, or conversational exercise;
- a curriculum module;
- recurring booster content.

### Stage 8 — Integrity gate
Before publication, verify:

- the original speaker is not deceptively clipped;
- the correction does not overstate the evidence;
- uncertainty and source grade remain visible;
- the content attacks claims, not human dignity;
- no doxxing, harassment, threats, or collective blame;
- no private person is amplified unnecessarily;
- copyrighted material is used only within an approved legal/editorial policy;
- the original falsehood is not repeated more vividly than the correction;
- the headline is not stronger than the verdict;
- an adversarial reviewer has tested the likely counterattack;
- operator approval is required before publishing.

### Stage 9 — Distribute experimentally
Test separately:

- hook;
- frame;
- emotional register;
- speaker/source choice;
- content length;
- original-clip-first versus truth-first;
- question versus declaration;
- specific refutation versus technique-based inoculation.

Never confuse production volume with immunity gained.

### Stage 10 — Measure the right outcomes
Views and retention matter, but the fitness function should also include:

- factual belief change;
- confidence calibration;
- ability to identify the tactic in a new example;
- recall after delay;
- willingness to share;
- quality of comments and questions;
- cross-tribal acceptance;
- resistance to the strongest counterattack;
- reduction in recurrence of the same misconception;
- correction durability after booster intervals.

### Stage 11 — Recursively improve
After each deployment:

- update the strain taxonomy;
- attach new audience objections;
- revise weak antibodies;
- retire language that backfires or overclaims;
- identify needed booster intervals;
- preserve failed experiments;
- update model routing based on measured performance;
- convert recurring gaps into research packets.

## Technique immunity and claim immunity

The engine needs both.

### Claim-specific immunity
Answers one proposition, such as a historical date, quotation, event, or legal claim.

### Technique-based immunity
Teaches a reusable pattern, such as decontextualization, false dichotomy, scapegoating, or loaded terminology.

Technique-based inoculation is more transferable; claim-specific correction is more precise. The strongest package often combines them.

## The five-level antibody ladder

### Level 1 — Recognition
The audience notices that a manipulation technique is present.

### Level 2 — Refutation
The audience can explain why the specific claim is unsupported or misleading.

### Level 3 — Replacement
The audience possesses a better explanatory model.

### Level 4 — Transfer
The audience detects the same pattern in a new domain or ideological direction.

### Level 5 — Transmission
The audience can explain the correction accurately to someone else without reproducing the smear.

The engine should optimize for Levels 4 and 5, not mere agreement with one clip.

## Audience-state model

Content should distinguish:

- unaware audiences;
- curious but uncertain audiences;
- misinformed audiences;
- identity-protective believers;
- hostile activists;
- experts and validators;
- potential amplifiers.

One script should not be expected to serve all states. The same verdict may require different bridges while preserving identical truth conditions.

## Failure modes

- **Reverse propaganda:** protecting our preferred conclusion instead of truth.
- **Over-amplification:** introducing a fringe smear to a much larger audience.
- **Citation theater:** many links without source relevance or authority.
- **Fact-vacuum correction:** disproving a claim without replacing its causal story.
- **Humiliation framing:** making belief change socially costly.
- **Overgeneralized inoculation:** teaching distrust of all institutions rather than discernment.
- **Clip deception:** removing context that would alter the audience's interpretation.
- **Metric capture:** optimizing views, outrage, or agreement instead of discernment.
- **One-shot illusion:** assuming a single exposure creates durable immunity.
- **Frozen antibody:** failing to update when the claim mutates or evidence changes.
- **Speaker fixation:** organizing around personalities rather than recurring claim families.

## Scientific baseline to dogfood, not worship

The implementation should begin from—but experimentally verify in our own audiences—several research-backed intervention families:

- psychological inoculation and prebunking;
- technique-based short videos;
- post-exposure debunking with a factual replacement;
- accuracy-attention prompts;
- media and information literacy;
- delayed booster exposures;
- active-learning and conversational practice.

Relevant anchors include:

- Roozenbeek et al., “Psychological inoculation improves resilience against misinformation on social media,” *Science Advances* (2022), DOI: 10.1126/sciadv.abo6254.
- Pennycook et al., “Shifting attention to accuracy can reduce misinformation online,” *Nature* (2021), DOI: 10.1038/s41586-021-03344-2.
- Lewandowsky et al., *The Debunking Handbook 2020*.
- Maertens et al., “Long-term effectiveness of inoculation against misinformation: Three longitudinal experiments,” *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Applied* (2021).
- Ecker et al., “The psychological drivers of misinformation belief and its resistance to correction,” *Nature Reviews Psychology* (2022).

Observed effects in this literature are not magic, universal, or necessarily durable. Cultural tailoring, repetition, audience trust, and real-world behavioral measurement remain necessary.

## MVP

The first version does not need automatic video editing or autonomous publishing.

It should accept one transcript and produce:

1. normalized timestamped transcript;
2. claim inventory;
3. recurring-strain matches;
4. tactic map;
5. triage table;
6. research dependencies;
7. provisional verdict cards;
8. candidate source clips;
9. corrective clip concepts;
10. adversarial and integrity review;
11. measurement plan;
12. repository integration packet.

## Canonical questions

For every transcript, ask:

1. What exact beliefs are being transmitted?
2. Which claims are true, false, misleading, contested, or merely offensive?
3. Which true facts are being used to build a false map?
4. What makes each claim memorable or identity-protective?
5. Is the error factual, definitional, causal, comparative, moral, or source-layer based?
6. Which claims recur across other media?
7. Which claims are load-bearing for the speaker's worldview?
8. What is the strongest version of the speaker's case?
9. What evidence would change our own view?
10. What minimum correction is sufficient?
11. What replacement model prevents the misconception from returning?
12. What transferable immunity gene can be taught?
13. Are we about to amplify something that would otherwise remain fringe?
14. Which audience state are we addressing?
15. What clip preserves fair context?
16. What content form best matches the learning objective?
17. What counterattack will follow?
18. What outcome would demonstrate actual immunity rather than passive agreement?
19. When should a booster appear?
20. What must enter Civilizational Memory OS, and what belongs only in the Truth and Peace production layer?

## North star

> Do not merely win the fact-check. Teach the mind to recognize the manipulation, recover the truth, rebuild the map, and carry that capacity into the next encounter.
